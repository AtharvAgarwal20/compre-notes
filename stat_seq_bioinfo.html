1 · Why do we even need statistical significance?

Sequence alignment gives you a single number called a score (S). A higher score means a better-looking alignment. The hard question is the next one:

🔑 The Central Question Is this high alignment score the result of real biological homology, or is it just what you'd get by sliding two unrelated sequences together until they happen to line up?

Have a look at the very examples from the lecture deck:

Pair 1 Seq1 MKTAYIAKQRQISFVK Seq2 MKTAYLSKQRQITFVK ↑ Many conserved positions — this looks meaningful. Pair 2 Seq1 ASDFGHQWERTYPLMN Seq2 QWERTYASDFZXCVBN ↑ Two random-looking strings. Pair 3 (the same Pair 2, but locally aligned) Seq1 ASDFGHQWERTYPLMN Seq2 QWERTYASDFZXCVBN ↑ Suddenly you find a perfect 6-letter local match!
⚠ The Lesson Random sequences can produce high-scoring local alignments. A high raw score by itself does not prove homology. We need a statistical framework that tells us: "yes, that score is too high to be a fluke" — or — "sorry, scores like that happen by accident all the time."

That framework is what the rest of this topic is about. Its end products are the two numbers you see in every BLAST output: the E-value and the bit score.

2 · The Random Sequence Model (Null Hypothesis)

To decide whether a score is "unusually high", we first need to know what a typical score looks like when nothing biological is going on. That baseline is built using a null hypothesis:

📐 Null Hypothesis (H₀) The two aligned sequences are unrelated. Any score we get is therefore a random alignment score.

Assumptions used to generate the random sequences:
  • The sequences are random (no biological signal).
  • They have the same amino acid frequencies as the real sequence being studied.
  • They have the same length as the real sequence.
The statistical test then verifies whether H₀ should be accepted (alignment is random) or rejected (alignment is significant).

How to actually generate a random sequence — Multinomial Model

The standard way is the multinomial model. Imagine a 20-sided "loaded" die where each face is an amino acid, and the face areas are proportional to that amino acid's frequency in the real sequence. You roll the die n times to make a random sequence of length n.

✅ Example from the slides Suppose a real sequence has these amino acid fractions:
p(P) = 0.14, p(A) = 0.28, p(W) = 0.14, p(H) = 0.14, p(E) = 0.28  (all others = 0)

A random sequence generated under this multinomial model will end up with the same length and roughly the same composition — but with the letters in random order. That is exactly what we want as a comparison baseline ("good model — same length, same AA composition").

The lecture deck shows a concrete demonstration: human PTCH2 (228 aa) was aligned against a shuffled version of TRA2 (233 aa, generated by the multinomial model). Even though one sequence is completely scrambled, the alignment score = 51. That number is your baseline "this happens at random."

3 · The Alignment Score — and Why Raw Scores Are Not Enough

The alignment score has the following anatomy:

S = Σ (substitution scores) + Σ (gap penalties)

You add up the substitution score for every aligned pair of residues (from your scoring matrix — BLOSUM, PAM, etc.) and subtract penalties for every gap. The result is the raw score, S.

🚫 Why we cannot use raw scores directly Raw scores depend on:
  • Sequence length — longer sequences naturally accumulate more positive matches, so a longer alignment of unrelated sequences can score higher than a short alignment of related ones.
  • Scoring matrix — BLOSUM62 gives different numbers than PAM250 even for the same alignment.
  • Gap penalties — different gap-opening / gap-extension costs change the answer.
⮕ Conclusion: raw scores from different searches cannot be directly compared. A score of 51 on one search means something completely different from a 51 on another.

This is precisely the motivation for everything that follows. We need to convert S into something scale-free — the E-value and the bit score.

4 · Distribution of Random Alignment Scores

Initial (wrong) expectation — Normal Distribution

A reasonable first guess is: "random scores should be normally distributed around some mean." This would imply a symmetric bell curve, like a Gaussian.

⚠ This is wrong, and here's why When you do local alignment (Smith–Waterman, BLAST), the algorithm maximizes the score over all possible sub-alignments. You don't get the "average" score — you get the best score the algorithm can find. Once you're looking at maxima of random variables, you are no longer in normal-distribution territory.

The real distribution — Gumbel Extreme Value Distribution (EVD)

Because alignment scores are maxima, they follow a special distribution called the Gumbel Extreme Value Distribution (EVD). It is asymmetric — it has a sharp peak and a long, fat right tail.

📈 How to read a Gumbel curve
  • Mode (peak): The most frequent "maximum" score you'd get aligning random sequences.
  • Right tail:
    • High scores are rare, but…
    • …they are much more likely than a normal distribution would predict.
    • A score that lands deep in the right tail is unlikely to occur by chance — it is statistically significant.
🔑 Why this matters If you used a Normal distribution to judge significance, you would think extremely high scores are vanishingly unlikely — and you would overestimate the significance of every hit. The fat-tailed Gumbel distribution correctly tells you "actually, that score isn't quite as rare as you think."

5 · EVD Mathematics — PDF, CDF, and the Tail Probability

You don't need to derive any of these. You only need to know which formula does what.

Probability Density Function (PDF)

f(x) = e−x − e−x

The PDF tells you how likely each score value is for a random alignment. It is the "shape" of the Gumbel curve.

Cumulative Distribution Function (CDF) — score is less than X

The CDF is the integral of the PDF — the area under the curve up to some value X. It answers: "what is the probability that the random alignment score S is below X?"

P(S < X) = ∫−∞X e−x − e−x dx

The integral evaluates to a beautifully compact form:

P(S < X) = e−e−X

The probability we actually care about — score is greater than X

For significance, we want the opposite question: what's the chance a random alignment scores at least as high as our observed score X? That's the right-tail probability.

P(S ≥ X) = 1 − e−e−X
✅ Practical simplification for large X When X is large (a high score), e−X is tiny. Using the approximation 1 − e−ε ≈ ε for small ε:
P(S ≥ X) ≈ e−X
⮕ The probability of observing a very high random score decreases exponentially. This is the key insight that lets the Karlin–Altschul equation take its simple form.

6 · From Probability to Expected Number of Random Hits (E)

So far we have the probability that one random alignment has score ≥ S. But in a BLAST search you compare your query against an entire database — that's many alignments. We need to convert the per-alignment probability into an expected count.

m = query sequence length
n = total database length
S = the observed alignment score (raw)
λ = scale parameter of the Gumbel distribution (depends on scoring matrix)
K = correction constant (depends on AA frequencies & matrix)

Each position in the query can pair with each position in the database, giving us roughly m × n potential starting points for an alignment.

E = (number of opportunities) × (probability of score ≥ S)
E = m·n · P(S ≥ S)
E ≈ m·n · e−λS

The λ appears because the alignment scoring system has its own scale — different scoring matrices "stretch" the score axis differently. λ is the calibration constant for whichever matrix you are using.

7 · The Karlin–Altschul Equation — The Master Formula

The simple E = mn · e−λS isn't quite right yet. The actual number of independent alignment opportunities is not exactly m × n. There are three reasons why:

⚠ Why m × n is not the true alignment count
  1. Alignment lengths — each alignment extends across many positions, so adjacent starts are not really independent.
  2. Edge effects — local alignments near the ends of sequences are underestimated because they can't extend in one direction.
  3. Scoring matrix — different matrices produce different effective search spaces.

To absorb all three corrections, a correction constant K is introduced. The result is the canonical equation of this field:

E = K · m · n · e−λS
🔑 The Karlin–Altschul Equation — What every term means
  • E = expected number of random alignments with score ≥ S
  • K = correction constant for the scoring system and edge effects
  • m = query length
  • n = database length
  • λ = Gumbel scale parameter, scaled by the scoring matrix
  • S = raw alignment score

A few observations from this single equation that explain everything you'll see in BLAST:

If this goes up… What happens to E? Intuition
S (alignment score) E ↓ (exponentially) Higher scores are exponentially less likely by chance.
m (query length) E ↑ (linearly) Longer queries have more chances for random hits.
n (database size) E ↑ (linearly) A bigger database = more random hits.
K, λ scale-dependent Set by the scoring system; usually fixed for a given search.

8 · Interpreting the E-value

This is the single most testable concept in the chapter. Memorize both the definition and the table.

📖 Formal definition E-value = the expected number of random alignments with score ≥ S that would be observed when searching this query against this database.
💬 Plain-English version "How many times would I see a match this good just by chance in this database?"

Visually, the E-value is the area of the right tail of the EVD (the probability of a single random hit) multiplied by the total number of alignments in the search:

E-value = (Area of tail) × (# of alignments)

The interpretation table — memorize this

E-value Biological relevance
10 ~10 random hits expected by chance — not meaningful
1 ~1 random hit — may be meaningful
0.01 Rare by chance — most likely meaningful
1e-20 Extremely unlikely by chance — strong homology
0 Extremely unlikely (reported when below floating-point precision) — strong homology
🧠 Rule of thumb for exam answers
  • Lower E-value ⟶ more significant alignment.
  • E ≤ 0.01 is the conventional threshold for "biologically meaningful."
  • E-value is not a probability — it can be greater than 1 (e.g., E = 10).

Effect of sequence and database length

E ∝ m·n

This is the relationship you'll be asked to reason about. If a colleague says "my E-value is 0.01" — that answer depends on the database she searched. The same alignment against a 10× larger database gives a 10× larger E-value, because there are 10× more chances for random hits. This is also why a BLAST hit from yesterday may become "less significant" tomorrow if the database has grown.

9 · E-value ↔ p-value via the Poisson Distribution

People sometimes report a p-value instead of (or alongside) an E-value. The two are related but they answer slightly different questions.

E-value Expected number of random hits with score ≥ S.
(a count — can be 0.001 or 10).
p-value Probability that at least one random hit with score ≥ S occurs.
(always between 0 and 1).

The bridge: rare independent events follow a Poisson distribution

A high-scoring random hit is a rare event. When rare events occur independently, the number of them in a fixed search follows the Poisson distribution:

P(k) = (e−λ · λk) / k!
λ = expected number of occurrences = E-value
k = the actual observed number of hits with score ≥ S
🔑 The two distributions, two roles • Gumbel Distribution describes the shape of scores (what values they take).
• Poisson Distribution describes the count of those scores (how many of them appear).

Deriving the p-value

The p-value is the probability of seeing at least one random hit with score ≥ S. That equals 1 minus the probability of seeing zero such hits:

p-value = 1 − P(0)
P(0) = (e−E · E0) / 0! = e−E
p-value = 1 − e−E

When E ≈ p, and when they diverge

✅ Small E (≪ 1): E ≈ p When E is very small, the exponential e−E ≈ 1 − E, so p = 1 − (1−E) = E. The two numbers are essentially identical.

Quick check from the slides:
  • If E = 0.01, then p = 1 − e−0.01 ≈ 0.00995. ⇒ E ≈ p. ✅
  • If E = 5, then p = 1 − e−5 ≈ 0.9933. ⇒ Very different! The p-value can never exceed 1, while E can be 5, 10, 100, etc.

10 · Bit Score — The Normalized Score

The raw score S still suffers from one problem: its significance via E = Kmn·e−λS depends on K and λ — which themselves depend on the scoring system. We want a score that you can read off a paper from 1998 and a paper from 2024 and they'll mean the same thing. That's the bit score.

The two-step transformation

1Strip out K and λ to get a "system-free" score S':

S' = λS − ln(K)

2Convert to bits by dividing by ln(2) (so we get a log base 2):

Sbit = (λS − ln K) / ln(2)

Interpretation: bit score as a log-odds ratio

Bit-score = log2 ( Probability of alignment if related / Probability of alignment if random )
🔑 The doubling rule (very testable) Because the bit score is log2, every +1 in bit score doubles the probability of a statistically significant alignment.
A 30-bit alignment is twice as good as a 29-bit one. A 50-bit hit is 220 ≈ 10⁶ times stronger than a 30-bit one.

Final E-value using the bit score

Once the score has been normalized, the E-value becomes radically simpler — no K or λ in sight, because they've been absorbed into S':

E = m · n · 2−S'

This is the form you can actually use for back-of-the-envelope calculations during an exam, when you're given a bit score and a database size.

11 · Statistics for Gapped Alignments

Everything above was derived for ungapped local alignment, but real BLAST does gapped alignment. Why does this matter?

⚠ The complication In gapped alignment:
  • Positions are not independent anymore — a gap "drags" the next residue along with it.
  • The clean mathematical assumptions used to derive the ungapped EVD do not strictly hold.
  • Therefore we need an empirical correction instead of a clean derivation.
✅ How it's handled in practice
  1. Generate a large number of random gapped alignments using the chosen scoring matrix and gap penalties.
  2. Fit a Gumbel distribution to the resulting score distribution.
  3. Read off the empirically estimated values of λ and K from the fit.
  4. Plug those empirical λ and K back into the same Karlin–Altschul equation:
    E = K·m·n · e−λS
Same formula, adjusted (empirical) parameter values.

Step-by-step decoding

1Score: 72.8 bits (177)

  • 72.8 bits = the bit score Sbit. This is the system-free, normalized score.
  • (177) in parentheses = the raw score S (sum of substitution scores − gap penalties).
  • The bit score > 50 — by the doubling rule, this is an enormously strong alignment.

2Expect: 3e-16

  • This is the E-value.
  • Definition: the expected number of alignments scoring ≥ 72.8 bits that you'd get by chance in this database.
  • Plain English: "You'd have to do 3 × 1015 independent searches to expect one fluke this strong."
  • Inference: cross-reference our memorized table — anything below 1e-20 is "strong homology"; 3e-16 is also firmly in the strong-homology zone.

3Identities: 49/165 (30%)

  • 49 of 165 aligned columns have the exact same residue in query and subject.
  • 30% identity falls in the well-known "twilight zone" for proteins (~20–35%), but the very low E-value tells us the homology is still real.

4Positives: 76/165 (46%)

  • 76 of 165 columns are similar residues (positive score in BLOSUM62 — either identical or a conservative substitution).
  • This is the count of all "+" symbols and identical letters shown in the middle row of the alignment.

5Gaps: 25/165 (15%)

  • 25 of 165 alignment columns are gap (−) characters.
  • 15% gapping is moderate — visible in the middle of the alignment as the long --- stretch.

6The alignment symbols

  • Letter in the middle row (e.g., Y) = identical residue.
  • + = positive-scoring substitution (similar amino acid).
  • blank = non-similar substitution (negative or zero score).
  • − in query or subject = gap (insertion/deletion).
  • Numbers at start/end = residue position in the original sequence.
✅ Final inference (full-mark answer) This alignment is highly statistically significant (E = 3 × 10−16, bit score 72.8) despite only 30% identity. Roughly half of the positions show conservative or identical substitution (46% positives), and gaps make up 15% of the alignment. The conclusion is that the query and subject are homologous proteins — likely belonging to the same family (the FPCNQFG motif visible around position 60 is highly conserved, suggesting a functional/structural role).

13 · One-Page Summary & Cheat-Sheet

The logical flow (memorize this chain)

Random sequences ↓
Gumbel EVD distribution ↓
P(S ≥ X) ≈ e−X ↓
E-value (expected count) — Karlin–Altschul ↓
Poisson model ↓
p-value (probability)

All key formulas in one place

Quantity Formula What it gives you
EVD PDF f(x) = e−x − e−x Likelihood of each score value (the "shape")
EVD CDF P(S < X) = e−e−X Probability score is below X
Tail probability P(S ≥ X) = 1 − e−e−X ≈ e−X Probability of a random score ≥ X
Karlin–Altschul E = K · m · n · e−λS Expected # of random hits ≥ S
Bit score Sbit = (λS − ln K) / ln 2 Normalized score (system-free)
E from bit score E = m · n · 2−S' E-value using normalized score
Poisson P(k) = e−λ·λk / k! Count of rare independent events (λ = E)
P(0) hits P(0) = e−E Probability of zero random hits
p-value p = 1 − e−E Probability of ≥1 random hit

Key relationships

Statement Meaning
E ≈ p-value when E is very small For E ≤ 0.01, the two numbers are essentially equal.
E ≠ p-value when E is large e.g., E = 5 ⇒ p ≈ 0.99. The p-value is capped at 1; the E-value is not.
Doubling rule Every +1 of bit score doubles the significance.
E ∝ m·n Doubling the database doubles the E-value.
Gapped alignments use empirical λ, K Same formula, empirically-fit parameters.

14 · Common Traps, Edge Cases & Expected Exam Variants

🪤 Trap 1: "E-value is a probability" No — the E-value is an expected count. It can be 5, 10, 100. Only the p-value is bounded between 0 and 1. Saying "an E-value of 5 means a 500% probability" is wrong.
🪤 Trap 2: "Higher score = more significant" Higher bit score = more significant, yes. But higher raw score alone tells you nothing without knowing the scoring matrix and the database size. This is the entire point of normalization.
🪤 Trap 3: "The threshold of significance is fixed" E ≤ 0.01 is a convention, not a law. For very large databases people often use E ≤ 1e-5; for short queries the threshold is sometimes relaxed. Always discuss it in context.
🪤 Trap 4: Confusing K, λ, and m, n
  • K and λ are properties of the scoring system (matrix + gap penalties + AA composition).
  • m and n are properties of the search (query and database lengths).
🪤 Trap 5: "Normal distribution" Random alignment scores follow the Gumbel EVD, not the Normal distribution. Why? Because alignment scores are maxima (local alignment maximizes the score), and maxima of random variables follow extreme-value distributions.

Expected exam variants and how to handle each

Type of question How to answer
Explain a BLAST output (like Q5a 2024–25) Decode each field: bits, raw score, E-value, identities, positives, gaps, alignment symbols. Then state the biological inference (significant homology / weak hit / not significant).
Calculate E given S, m, n If bit score is given: use E = m·n · 2−S'. If raw + K, λ given: use Karlin–Altschul. Show the substitution explicitly.
Calculate p-value from E Use p = 1 − e−E. Note whether E is small (so E ≈ p) or large (compute exactly).
"What if database doubles?" E doubles (E ∝ n). Significance gets worse. Bit score does not change.
"Why not normal distribution?" Alignment scores are maxima, not means; their distribution is Gumbel EVD; the right tail is fatter than Normal, so significance must be computed differently.
"Why bit score not raw score?" Raw scores depend on scoring matrix, gap penalties, and length — they cannot be compared across searches. Bit score absorbs K and λ into the score itself.
"How is gapped alignment handled?" Karlin–Altschul derivation assumes independence (breaks for gaps). Solution: simulate many random gapped alignments, empirically fit a Gumbel to the scores, and use those empirical λ and K in the same equation.
"Why is m × n not the exact alignment count?" Alignment lengths (positions not independent), edge effects (end alignments underestimated), and scoring-matrix-dependent search space — corrected by K.
🎯 The "if I only remember three things" version
  1. Karlin–Altschul: E = K·m·n·e−λS — the E-value depends exponentially on score and linearly on lengths.
  2. E ↔ p: p = 1 − e−E, and E ≈ p for small E.
  3. Random scores ≠ Normal: They follow the Gumbel EVD because alignment is a maximization process. This has a fatter right tail than Normal.
