Foundations of Metrology — Errors, Tolerances & the ISO System

1.1 What Metrology Is — and Why It Exists

The word metrology comes from the Greek metron (measure) and logos (study). Etymology aside, metrology is the science of measurement — the discipline concerned with the methods, units, instruments, and standards by which physical quantities are quantified.

Course Definition
Metrology (in this course)
The science and engineering practice of measuring geometry and surface attributes of manufactured parts, and verifying their conformance to design specifications.

In the broadest sense, we measure time, temperature, mass, length, electrical quantities, chemical concentrations, and many more. For mechanical engineering, the dominant interest is dimensional metrology — lengths, diameters, angles, surface profiles — and quality metrology — statistical control of manufacturing variability.

Metrology is the silent infrastructure of every modern economy. Without uniform, traceable, reproducible measurements, two companies in two countries cannot make parts that fit together; a tablet manufactured in India cannot certify its drug content for a European regulator; an aircraft engine cannot be inspected at a third-party facility. The slide statement that metrology is an "indispensable part of modern infrastructure" is not poetic — it is literal. The cost of not having reliable measurement is the cost of catastrophic mismatches: failed assemblies, lawsuits, recalls, deaths.

Why It Bridges Design and Manufacturing

A designer specifies a dimension on a drawing — say, a shaft of 25.000 mm. A machinist works on a lathe and tries to produce that shaft. But:

  • The lathe has small geometric errors (spindle runout, bed wear).
  • The cutting tool wears as it removes material.
  • The workpiece itself deflects under the cutting force.
  • Temperature in the workshop fluctuates, expanding and contracting both workpiece and machine.

No part will ever be exactly 25.000 mm. Some will be 24.998, others 25.003. Metrology is the practice of measuring how close we got to the design intent, and statistical quality control is the practice of deciding whether the deviations are acceptable. Metrology, therefore, is the language by which design intent is enforced on the shop floor.

The International Standards Function

One of metrology's most important roles is the maintenance of international standards of measurement. The metre, kilogram, second, ampere, kelvin, mole and candela are all defined by international agreement (most now in terms of fundamental physical constants since the 2019 SI redefinition). National Metrology Institutes (NMIs) — NPL in India, NIST in USA, PTB in Germany — hold the primary realisations of these standards, and a chain of traceability links every workshop micrometer back to those national standards through periodic calibration.

1.2 The Three Branches of Metrology

The course identifies three categories — these are not three different "sciences" but three orientations of measurement, each addressing a different problem.

(a) Legal Metrology

Definition
Legal Metrology
Any application of metrology that is subject to national laws or regulations. Its primary objective is to maintain uniformity of measurement within a country, especially in trade, health, safety and the environment.

The "900 g" stamped on a sauce-jar label is legal metrology in action. So is the calibration certificate behind a fuel-pump dispenser at a petrol station, the weighbridge at a port that determines a shipping duty, or the radar gun that fines a speeding driver. Legal metrology exists wherever a measurement carries a legal consequence. Without it, no commerce based on weight, volume or count could function fairly.

India's Legal Metrology Act, 2009 governs commercial weights and measures; an equivalent national institution audits packagers, retailers, fuel stations, and laboratories.

(b) Dynamic Metrology

Definition
Dynamic Metrology
The measurement of small variations of a continuous nature. It is used wherever a quantity changes over a continuous domain — across a surface, along a time axis, along a path — and where the variation itself is the object of measurement.

Examples are everywhere. Recording the surface profile of a turned shaft along its full length yields a continuous signal of heights as a function of position — that's dynamic metrology. An ECG recording of the heart's electrical activity over time is dynamic metrology. Tracking vibration of a rotating shaft during operation, instead of inspecting it at rest, is dynamic metrology.

The slide makes a powerful claim: dynamic measurements have obvious advantages over individual discrete measurements of a distinctive character. The phrase "of a distinctive character" means: when each individual measurement is an isolated act, you cannot detect trends, periodicities, drifts, or localised defects. A continuous record reveals those features.

(c) Deterministic Metrology

Definition
Deterministic Metrology
A paradigm in which part measurement is substituted by process measurement. The idea: instead of measuring each finished part, measure and control the process so precisely that the part's correctness is guaranteed by the process itself.

The classic example is 3-D error compensation in CNC machines. The CNC controller maintains a real-time model of the machine's thermal and geometric errors and continuously adjusts the tool path to compensate. As a result, every part comes out within tolerance — without the need to measure each one after the fact.

This is the philosophy behind micro- and nano-technology manufacturing: at scales where dimensional inspection of finished parts is itself heroic, you cannot afford to inspect them all; you have to design the process so it cannot make a bad part.

The three branches form a hierarchy of intent. Legal metrology protects the consumer. Dynamic metrology captures phenomena that change. Deterministic metrology removes inspection as a bottleneck by controlling the process upstream. A modern factory uses all three: a deterministic CNC machine produces parts; a dynamic profilometer audits the surface; legal metrology ensures the weighing machine at dispatch is fair.

1.3 Need for Inspection

Definition
Inspection
A procedure in which a part or product characteristic (such as a dimension) is examined to determine whether it conforms to the design specification. It is the act of isolating and evaluating a specific design or quality attribute of a component or product.

Historical Context — Interchangeability

Industrial inspection became important because of mass production — specifically, the demand for interchangeability of parts. Before the 19th century, machines were built by skilled craftsmen who hand-fitted each part to its neighbour. If a single gear failed, you couldn't simply swap in a replacement; you needed the same craftsman to fashion a new one.

Eli Whitney's musket factory (early 1800s) and the Springfield Armory pioneered the concept that all parts of one design should be identical enough that any part could replace any other part. This was revolutionary. The Singer Sewing Machine Company, Henry Ford's assembly line, and ultimately all of modern manufacturing rest on this principle. Interchangeability requires inspection: someone has to verify that every component is "close enough" to the design.

Measurement vs Gauging

Inspection methods divide into two families:

Measurement Technique Gauging Technique
Returns the actual dimension as a number (e.g., shaft diameter = 24.987 mm) Returns only "within limits" or "outside limits" — no numerical value
Slower; requires reading a scale or display; operator-dependent Faster; binary go/no-go decision
Allows statistical analysis, control charts, process capability calculations Allows only count of accept/reject; no SPC on measurements
Uses a Vernier caliper, micrometer, CMM, optical comparator Uses plug gauges, snap gauges, ring gauges
Suited to process improvement and capability studies Suited to high-volume sorting

Industries typically use both: measurement for process setup and periodic audit, gauging for routine 100 % inspection on the line.

The Eight Functions of Inspection — Explained

  1. Conformance verification. Confirm that the part, material, or component meets the established or desired standard.
  2. Interchangeability. Ensure manufacturing produces interchangeable parts so assemblies can be built and serviced without hand-fitting.
  3. Customer goodwill. Prevent defective products from reaching customers, sustaining trust and brand reputation.
  4. Process feedback. Inspection data fed back to the manufacturing department drives reduction in scrap and identifies systematic issues in machines, tools, or operators.
  5. Quality of inputs. Incoming inspection of raw materials, tools and equipment ensures that finished-product quality is not undermined by bad inputs.
  6. Cross-functional coordination. Inspection bridges Quality Control with Production, Purchasing, R&D, and other departments. Bad parts on the assembly line are everyone's problem.
  7. Rework decisions. Inspection identifies parts that can be reworked into acceptable condition versus those that must be scrapped — saving material and money.
  8. Spirit of competition. Inspection metrics make performance visible, allowing teams to compete and improve, eliminating bottlenecks.

1.4 Accuracy and Precision

These two words are colloquially synonymous but in metrology they mean strictly different things. Mastering this distinction is half the conceptual battle of this course.

Definition
Accuracy
The degree of agreement of the measured dimension with its true magnitude. The "true value" is itself defined as the mean of an infinite number of measurements as the average deviation due to all contributing factors tends to zero — a theoretical limit, never reached, but approached by careful technique and large data.
Definition
Precision
The degree of repetitiveness (repeatability) of the measuring process. If an instrument is not precise, repeated readings of the same dimension produce different values. Precision says nothing about whether those values cluster around the true value — only that they cluster.

The Dartboard Picture (Famous Metaphor)

The classic visualisation maps four combinations onto a dartboard:

  • Precise but not accurate — all darts in a tight cluster, far from the bullseye. The shooter is consistent but systematically off.
  • Accurate but not precise — darts spread widely, but their average is near the bullseye. The shooter is randomly bad in both directions, so the mean works out — but no single shot is reliable.
  • Precise and accurate — tight cluster on the bullseye. Goal of every measurement system.
  • Neither precise nor accurate — scattered all over the board. Bad shooter, bad measurement system.
The Statistical Picture

Both accuracy and precision can be visualised on a single normal-distribution plot of repeated measurements:

  • The position of the distribution's mean relative to the true value represents accuracy. The gap between them is the bias or systematic error.
  • The width (standard deviation) of the distribution represents precision. A narrow distribution = high precision; a wide one = low precision.

Modern ISO terminology has further refined this:

  • Trueness = closeness of the mean to the true value (the accuracy component).
  • Precision = closeness of repeated measurements to one another.
  • Accuracy = the combination of trueness and precision (the total closeness of a single reading to the true value).

These concepts map directly onto systematic errors (which affect trueness) and random errors (which affect precision). We will return to this in §1.4.

Sensitivity, Consistency, Range

Definition
Sensitivity
The ability of a measuring instrument to detect small variations in the quantity being measured. Mathematically, sensitivity is the ratio of change in output indication to change in input quantity. A digital scale with a 0.001 g resolution is more sensitive than one with a 0.1 g resolution.

The slide makes two important sub-statements that are easy to miss:

  1. When you try to incorporate higher accuracy, sensitivity must increase — because you cannot measure something you cannot detect.
  2. The permitted degree of sensitivity determines the accuracy — meaning the smallest variation the instrument can resolve sets an upper bound on how accurate it can ever be.
Definition
Consistency
Successive readings of the measured quantity, obtained from the same instrument, are the same all the time. Consistency is the long-term twin of repeatability — it includes stability across many usage cycles and environmental changes.
Definition
Range (Span)
The difference between the lowest and highest values the instrument is capable of measuring. A 0–25 mm micrometer has a range of 25 mm; a 0–150 mm Vernier caliper has a range of 150 mm.

The Accuracy-Cost Tradeoff

A highly accurate instrument possesses both sensitivity and consistency. Cost increases exponentially with accuracy requirement. A 0.01 mm Vernier caliper costs a few hundred rupees; a 0.001 mm digital micrometer costs a few thousand; a sub-micron laser interferometer costs lakhs; a National Metrology Institute's primary length standard costs crores.

The Accuracy-vs-Cost curve looks like a hyperbola: cheap at the low end, asymptotically expensive as you push accuracy. Engineering wisdom is to choose the cheapest instrument that meets the accuracy demand — never over-specify.

1.4 Errors in Measurement

All measurements are imperfect. Understanding the nature of imperfection lets us correct it, bound it, or live with it.

Definition
Error
The difference between the indicated (measured) value and the true value of the measured quantity. Equivalently, the difference between the true value and the mean value of a set of readings of the same component.
Absolute & Relative Error E = Vm − Vt

Relative Error (%) = (E / Vt) × 100%
Why distinguish absolute from relative? Because significance scales with the quantity being measured. A ±2 g error in a 1 kg measurement is 0.2 % — usually negligible. The same ±2 g in a 10 g measurement is 20 % — almost meaningless. Whenever you read an accuracy specification, ask: "Is this absolute or relative?" High-end calipers specify accuracy as ±(0.02 mm + L × 0.005 %) — partly absolute, partly proportional to length L.

Systematic (Controllable) Errors

Systematic errors deviate by a fixed amount from the true value. They are controllable in both magnitude and direction — meaning we can, in principle, identify them and correct for them.

When systematic errors are minimised, the measurement is said to be extremely accurate. Note carefully: minimising systematic error improves accuracy, not precision.

Four Subtypes of Systematic Error

1. Calibration errors. Small variations from the nominal value exist in length standards themselves — slip gauges drift, engraved scales have engraving inaccuracies. The instrument's own inertia and hysteresis prevent it from translating measurements with perfect fidelity.

Definition
Hysteresis
The difference in the instrument's indication when the quantity is measured in ascending order versus descending order. A dial gauge may read 25.030 when approached from below and 25.027 when approached from above — that 3 µm gap is hysteresis. Caused by friction in moving parts, elastic deformation of springs, lash in gear trains.

2. Ambient condition errors. The international standard reference conditions are 20 °C (68 °F) and 760 mmHg (1 atm). Departures from these cause systematic shifts:

  • A 1 °C rise increases the length of C25 steel by 0.3 µm per 25 mm (coefficient of linear expansion ~12 × 10⁻⁶ /°C). For precision work this is enormous — a 25 mm part measured at 25 °C is genuinely 1.5 µm longer than its nominal cold size, but the instrument reads it as if at 20 °C unless corrected.
  • Strain-gauge measurements include temperature compensation circuits because resistance gauges respond to both strain and temperature.
  • Pressure affects gauge blocks marginally; humidity affects certain materials (wood, plastic, hygroscopic alloys).

3. Workpiece deformation errors. Any elastic body deforms under load. The stylus pressure of a measuring contact (a Vernier jaw, a micrometer anvil, a CMM probe) compresses the workpiece at the contact point. Magnitude of deformation depends on:

  • Applied load (Newtons).
  • Area of contact (point, line, or area contact — Hertzian contact mechanics).
  • Mechanical properties: Young's modulus, Poisson's ratio, hardness.

For soft materials like aluminium or rubber, contact deformation can be the dominant error source. Special non-contact methods (laser triangulation, white-light interferometry) are used for these.

4. Avoidable errors. The slides group together five mistakes that are entirely the operator's fault:

  • Datum errors — wrong reference selected for a measurement (e.g., measuring from a chamfer instead of the flat face).
  • Reading errors — mis-reading a Vernier scale, mis-counting micrometer thimble divisions.
  • Parallax errors — the apparent shift of a reading when the eye is not perpendicular to the scale. Eliminated by mirror scales or digital displays.
  • Misalignment errors — violating the Principle of Alignment (line of measurement not coincident with line of dimension).
  • Zero errors — instrument not properly zeroed before use; every reading inherits a constant offset.

Random Errors

Random errors provide a measure of the random deviations obtained when measurements are repeated. Their specific causes cannot be identified individually — they are the cumulative effect of innumerable small uncontrollable factors: air currents, micro-vibrations, slight differences in pressure applied, slight variations in the way the operator looks at the scale. They are unpredictable and uncontrollable — random in the deepest sense.

When repeated measurements are plotted, they form a normal (Gaussian) distribution by the Central Limit Theorem — because they are the sum of many independent small effects, no one of which dominates.

Statistics of n random readings v1, v2, …, vn Arithmetic mean:  v̄ = (v₁ + v₂ + … + vn) / n

Standard deviation:  σ = ± √[ Σ (v − v̄)² / n ]

The mean is our best single estimate of the true value; the standard deviation is our measure of the precision of any one reading. By the empirical (68-95-99.7) rule:

  • 68 % of readings lie within ±1σ of the mean.
  • 95 % within ±2σ.
  • 99.74 % within ±3σ. (This is why control charts use ±3σ limits.)
The Combined Picture

Imagine plotting a histogram of many repeated measurements of one object:

  • The peak of the distribution is the mean value of measurement.
  • The true value is somewhere on the x-axis.
  • The horizontal distance from the peak to the true value = systematic error.
  • The width of the distribution (σ) = random error.

To improve accuracy, shift the peak to overlap the true value — eliminate bias. To improve precision, narrow the distribution — reduce noise. They are independent goals; you can have any combination of high/low for each.

1.5 Calibration

Definition
Calibration
A comparison between a known measurement (the standard) and the measurement obtained using your instrument. The result of calibration is documented evidence of the instrument's accuracy, and any necessary corrections to be applied.

The 10× Rule

The slides state that the accuracy of the standard should ideally be 10 times the accuracy of the device being tested. This is the Test Accuracy Ratio (TAR) rule. If you are calibrating a micrometer that reads to 0.01 mm, your reference standard should be accurate to 0.001 mm. Anything less, and the standard contributes too much to the apparent error to make the calibration meaningful.

The Traceability Chain

An ordinary workshop micrometer is calibrated against a set of slip gauges. Those slip gauges are calibrated against a more accurate set held by the company's quality lab. That set is calibrated by a national-accredited laboratory against a primary set held by the National Metrology Institute (NPL India). The NPL primary set is calibrated against the realisation of the SI metre — currently defined in terms of the speed of light.

This unbroken chain of comparisons, each documented and dated, is called traceability. Without it, no measurement has meaning beyond the laboratory in which it was made.

When to Calibrate

An instrument must be calibrated:

  • According to the manufacturer's recommendation (typically annually for shop instruments).
  • After any mechanical or electrical shock — a dropped micrometer cannot be trusted until re-verified.
  • Periodically, on a schedule that reflects the instrument's drift behaviour and the cost of being wrong.
  • After repair or significant disassembly.
  • When unusual readings suggest something has changed.

1.6 Limits, Fits & Tolerances

The 5 M's of Manufacturing

Variation enters the shop through five channels — the five M's: Man, Machine, Materials, Money, Management. Variations in the first three (man, machine, materials) drive the immediate process variation. All three are subject to two qualitatively different kinds of variation.

Natural Variation (Common Causes)
  • Random in nature.
  • The cumulative effect of many small, essentially uncontrollable causes.
  • Each individual cause is too small to identify economically; together they form the noise floor of the process.
  • When natural variations are small, they are accepted as an inherent property of the process.
  • Removing them would require redesigning the process from scratch.
Characteristic Variation (Assignable Causes / Special Causes)
  • Arises from specific, identifiable disturbances: improperly adjusted machines, operator error, tool wear, defective raw materials, a temperature spike, a power fluctuation.
  • Generally large compared to natural variability.
  • Each cause is identifiable — and once identified, can be removed economically.
  • The presence of assignable causes means the process is "out of control" in the statistical sense.
The distinction matters because it dictates strategy. Common causes are addressed by redesigning the process (e.g., upgrading the machine). Assignable causes are addressed by fixing the specific problem (sharpen the tool, retrain the operator, reject the bad lot of material). Statistical Process Control (Lecture 5) is, fundamentally, a method to distinguish these two kinds of variation in real time.

The fourth M — money — enters because eliminating assignable causes must be economical. The fifth M — management — is the human-systems layer that decides whether to invest in process improvement.

Why Tolerances Exist

Fundamental Truth
No part is exact.
Because of the variability described above, no component can be manufactured precisely to a given dimension. It can only be made to lie between two specified limits — an upper (maximum) limit and a lower (minimum) limit. The total permissible variation between these limits is the tolerance.

Tolerance is the designer's acknowledgement that perfection is impossible — and a contract specifying how imperfect is "good enough". Specifying a tighter tolerance:

  • Forces the use of more accurate machines, finer tooling, more skilled operators.
  • Increases inspection rigour.
  • Drives up cost — typically exponentially.

The art of design is choosing the loosest tolerance that still ensures function. A tight tolerance on a non-critical dimension is waste; a loose tolerance on a critical mating dimension is failure.

Classification of Tolerance

(a) Unilateral Tolerance

The tolerance distribution is on one side of the basic size only. Used where dimensions are expected to deviate predominantly in one direction. Drilling is the textbook example: a drill bit produces holes that tend to be oversize (never undersize), because of bit wander, vibration, and material spring-back.

Notation examples: 40+0.02+0.01   40+0.02+0.00   40−0.01−0.02   40+0.00−0.02.

(b) Bilateral Tolerance

Distribution lies on either side of the basic size. The dimension is allowed to vary on both sides, but not necessarily by the same amount. Preferred in mass production where the machine is set to the basic size and natural variation falls symmetrically about it.

Notation: 40 ± 0.02   or   40+0.02−0.01.

(c) Compound Tolerance

Tolerance is determined by established tolerances on more than one dimension acting together. The classic example is a feature whose location depends on a radius R, an angle, and a linear distance — each individually toleranced — leading to a compound tolerance on the feature's actual location.

Compound tolerances should be avoided in practice, because the resulting tolerance build-up is hard to control and inspect.

(d) Geometric Tolerance

Geometric tolerance is the total amount by which the dimension of a manufactured part can vary — but its emphasis is on the shape of a feature rather than its size. Sometimes a part can have correct dimensions but the wrong shape (a cylinder with a barrel-shaped profile, a flat surface that is actually slightly concave). Geometric tolerance addresses these.

Geometric Tolerance — Three Families

Depending on functional requirements, geometric tolerance is specified on diameter, straightness, roundness, etc. The categories:

Family Members What it controls
Form (independent) Straightness, circularity, flatness, cylindricity, profile of a line, profile of a surface The shape of an isolated feature, with no reference to any other feature.
Orientation (related) Perpendicularity, parallelism, angularity The angular relationship of a feature to a datum.
Position (related) Position (true position), symmetry, concentricity, circular runout, total runout The location of a feature relative to a datum.
Reading a Feature Control Frame

Geometric tolerance is communicated by a feature control frame on the drawing — a small rectangle divided into compartments. Reading left-to-right:

  1. The symbol for the geometric characteristic (e.g., ⊚ = concentricity, ⫛ = perpendicularity, ⌒ = circularity).
  2. The tolerance zone — usually a number prefixed by Ø for a diametral zone.
  3. The datum letter(s) — the reference feature(s) the tolerance is measured from.

Example: ⊚ Ø0.01 | X means "concentricity — the two centres in question must lie within a Ø0.01 mm cylinder — referenced to datum X". For a stepped cylinder, this means the smaller and larger diameters share the same axis to within 0.01 mm of datum X.

Tolerance Accumulation (Stack-up)

When parts or features are dimensioned in a chain — each from the next — the individual tolerances add up, leading to a large cumulative tolerance on the overall dimension.

Example: three blocks A (30+0.02−0.01), B (20+0.02−0.01), C (10+0.02−0.01) in a chain.

  • Nominal total L = 30 + 20 + 10 = 60 mm.
  • Cumulative upper tolerance = +0.02 + 0.02 + 0.02 = +0.06
  • Cumulative lower tolerance = −0.01 − 0.01 − 0.01 = −0.03
  • Assembly dimension: 60+0.06−0.03 mm

This buildup is undesirable. The remedy is progressive (baseline) dimensioning — dimension every feature from one common reference (a baseline), instead of in a chain. The tolerance of each feature is then independent of the others, and the cumulative tolerance is just the tolerance of any one feature.

1.7 Shaft, Hole, MML & LML

The words "shaft" and "hole" in ISO terminology are generalised, not literal.

  • Shaft designates all external features of a part — including non-cylindrical ones. The width of a key, the thickness of a flange, the outside dimension of a slot — all "shafts" in the ISO sense.
  • Hole designates all internal features — including non-cylindrical. The width of a keyway, the inside dimension of a recess — all "holes".
Why this generalisation? Because the mathematical relationships between an external feature and an internal feature (in terms of fits, tolerances, allowance) are the same regardless of whether they are cylindrical or not. By generalising, ISO gives us one set of rules for all mating-feature relationships.
Definitions
Maximum & Minimum Metal Limits
MML (Maximum Metal Limit) = the limit at which the part has the maximum possible amount of metal.
LML (Least Metal Limit) = the limit at which the part has the least possible amount of metal.

For a shaft: MML = upper limit (HLS); LML = lower limit (LLS). [The biggest shaft = most metal.]
For a hole: MML = lower limit (LLH); LML = upper limit (HLH). [The smallest hole = most metal around it.]

Worked example: Shaft 40 ± 0.05 mm → HLS = 40.05, LLS = 39.95. MML = 40.05, LML = 39.95. Hole 45 ± 0.05 mm → HLH = 45.05, LLH = 44.95. MML = 44.95, LML = 45.05.

1.8 Fits

Whenever two parts mate — a shaft in a hole — their relationship before assembly is described by a fit.

Definition
Fit
The relationship between two mating parts (a hole and a shaft) with respect to the difference in their dimensions before assembly. An "ideal fit" is one that produces the correct functional behaviour after assembly — free rotation, secure press, locating accuracy, etc.

There are three basic types, determined by the relative positions of the hole and shaft tolerance zones.

(a) Clearance Fit

Definition: The largest permissible diameter of the shaft is smaller than the diameter of the smallest hole.

  • Always produces clearance after assembly — the shaft is always smaller than the hole.
  • The difference (hole − shaft) is always positive.
  • Small clearances are used for precise locating fits that can be assembled by hand without tools.
  • Large clearances are used where relative motion is required — a rotating shaft in a journal bush, a piston in a cylinder.
  • Mathematical condition: HLS < LLH, equivalently LLH − HLS > 0.

(b) Interference Fit

Definition: The minimum permissible diameter of the shaft is greater than the maximum allowable diameter of the hole.

  • A tight, force-required fit. Often called a "press fit" or "shrink fit".
  • Assembled parts will not come apart or move during use — essentially permanent.
  • The difference is always negative.
  • Used where accurate location is critical and no relative motion can be tolerated — dowel pins, gear hubs on shafts, valve seat inserts pressed into engine heads.
  • Methods of assembly: pressing (force fit), heating the outer part (shrink fit — thermal expansion enlarges the hole, the cold shaft inserted, then cooling shrinks the hole onto the shaft), cooling the inner part (freeze fit — liquid nitrogen).
  • Mathematical condition: LLS > HLH.

(c) Transition Fit

Definition: The diameter of the largest permissible hole is greater than the diameter of the smallest shaft, AND the diameter of the smallest hole is smaller than the diameter of the largest shaft.

  • The tolerance zones overlap.
  • Depending on the specific instances of the shaft and hole produced, the fit may end up as clearance OR interference — it could go either way.
  • Used where accurate location is needed but disassembly should still be possible — pulleys on shafts, ball-bearing inner rings.

Decision Rules at a Glance

HLH − LLS > 0 AND LLH − HLS > 0→ Clearance fit HLH − LLS < 0 AND LLH − HLS < 0→ Interference fit One inequality positive, the other negative→ Transition fit (tolerance zones overlap)

1.9 Allowance

Definition
Allowance
The intentional difference between the maximum-metal limits of the two mating parts — i.e., between LLH and HLS. It represents either the minimum clearance (for a clearance fit) or the maximum interference (for an interference fit). Allowance is the prescribed dimensional difference that produces the desired type of fit.
Allowance Definition Allowance = LLH − HLS
  • Positive allowance ⇒ clearance fit (the allowance is the minimum clearance).
  • Negative allowance ⇒ interference fit (the magnitude is the maximum interference).
  • Zero allowance is a boundary case — line-to-line fit.
Tolerance and allowance are often confused. Tolerance is the permissible variation on a single feature; allowance is the prescribed dimensional offset between two mating features. Tolerance lives on one part; allowance lives between two parts.
Problem 1.1  Tolerances for a hole-shaft pair of nominal size 50 mm:
Hole = 50+0.02+0.00 mm; Shaft = 50−0.05−0.08 mm.
Find (i) max & min clearance, (ii) tolerance on shaft & hole, (iii) allowance, (iv) MML of both, (v) type of fit.
Step 1 · Extract limits HLH = 50 + 0.02 = 50.02,  LLH = 50 + 0.00 = 50.00
HLS = 50 − 0.05 = 49.95,  LLS = 50 − 0.08 = 49.92
Step 2 · Clearances Maximum clearance occurs when hole is largest AND shaft is smallest:
Cmax = HLH − LLS = 50.02 − 49.92 = 0.10 mm

1.10 Hole-Basis vs Shaft-Basis Systems

When designing a system of fits, one of the two mating parts must be held to a standard dimension and the other allowed to vary. This produces two equivalent design philosophies.

Hole-Basis System

  • The hole size is kept constant; the shaft size is varied to obtain different fits.
  • Fundamental deviation (lower deviation) of the hole = 0 → LLH = basic size.
  • The two limits of the shaft and the higher dimension of the hole are then varied to obtain the desired fit.
  • Widely adopted in industry for one practical reason: it is much easier and cheaper to turn a shaft to a desired diameter than to bore a hole to a desired diameter.
  • A finite set of standard drills and reamers can produce all the holes; the shaft is then turned to whatever size produces the required fit.
  • Drawing notation: capital letter H (as in 40H7).

Shaft-Basis System

  • The shaft size is kept constant; the hole size is varied.
  • Fundamental deviation (upper deviation) of the shaft = 0 → HLS = basic size.
  • Not preferred in general industry — would require many standard sizes of reamers, broaches, and gauges, increasing manufacturing and inspection costs.
  • Used in situations where the standard shaft determines the dimensions of mating parts — couplings, bearings, collars, gears, bushings. (Standard shafts in stock; mating components made to fit.)
  • Drawing notation: small letter h (as in 40h7).

1.11 ISO System of Limits & Fits

Why ISO Standardised It

Before international standardisation, every country (and often every factory) had its own conventions for tolerances. A part designed in Britain and made in Germany might not fit a German-made hole. The International Organization for Standardization (ISO) consolidated this into a single global system of limits and fits, allowing parts from any country to mate with parts from any other.

The ISO system specifies 18 grades of fundamental tolerance indicating the degree of manufacturing accuracy required. These grades are designated by the letters IT followed by a number:

IT01, IT0, IT1, IT2, IT3, IT4, IT5, IT6, IT7, IT8, IT9, IT10, IT11, IT12, IT13, IT14, IT15, IT16

The Standard Tolerance Unit i

For grades IT5 through IT16, the tolerance value is expressed in terms of a standard tolerance unit i in micrometres, which depends on the basic size:

Standard tolerance unit (µm) i = 0.453 ∛D + 0.001 D

  • The cube-root term (0.453 ∛D) dominates at small sizes and reflects the fact that manufacturing variability grows slowly with size — you can hold tighter relative tolerances on small parts than large ones.
  • The linear term (0.001 D) becomes more significant at large sizes and reflects measurement-system errors that scale linearly with length (thermal expansion, instrument arm length).

2.1 Design of Gauges — The Inspection Tool of Mass Production

In mass production, parts are made by the millions. Measuring each part individually — even with a fast instrument — would be ruinously slow and expensive. Manufacturing engineers therefore developed gauges: scaleless inspection tools that quickly verify whether a part lies within the permissible limits, without producing an actual numerical value.

A complete gauging system uses two gauges per dimension, corresponding to the two limits:

  • A GO gauge, sized to the maximum-metal limit of the part.
  • A NOT-GO gauge (sometimes "NO-GO"), sized to the least-metal limit.

If the GO enters/passes and the NOT-GO does not, the part is accepted. If the GO fails, the part is too small/large in the wrong direction. If the NOT-GO enters/passes, the part is too small/large in the other direction.

2.2 Taylor's Principle

Principle
Taylor's Principle
(a) The GO gauge is designed to check maximum-metal conditions (LLH for a hole, HLS for a shaft). It should also simultaneously check as many related dimensions as possible — size, roundness, location — because its size and shape act as a "master replica" of the mating part at its tightest condition.

(b) The NOT-GO gauge is designed to check minimum-metal conditions (HLH for a hole, LLS for a shaft). It should check only one dimension at a time — a separate NOT-GO is required for each individual dimension.

(c) During inspection: the GO side should enter the hole (or pass over the shaft) under its own weight, with no undue force. The NOT-GO side should not enter or pass.

Why GO and NOT-GO Have Different Designs

Why GO checks the maximum-metal condition

At MML, the hole is at its smallest and the shaft at its largest. If a gauge sized at MML fits — at MML — then the actual part (which has more clearance) will definitely fit too. The GO gauge thus verifies the worst case from the assembly standpoint.

Moreover, because a GO gauge is built as a "replica of the mating part at MML", its shape matters as much as its size. A GO plug gauge that enters a hole is verifying not just diameter but also roundness, straightness, and any taper — all simultaneously. This is the "complex gauge" property.

Why NOT-GO checks only one dimension at a time

NOT-GO checks the least-metal limit. Its job is to refuse entry — to make sure the part is not too far in the LML direction. If a NOT-GO gauge tried to check multiple features at once, a part out of tolerance on one feature might still let the gauge enter because of compensating geometry on another feature. Each NOT-GO is therefore a dedicated single-feature check.

This is why a single threaded part needs multiple NOT-GO ring gauges: one for major diameter, one for pitch diameter, one for minor diameter — each separately.

Important Points for Gauge Design
  • The form of GO gauges should be a replica of the mating part's form.
  • GO gauges enable several related dimensions to be checked simultaneously — they are "complex gauges".
  • During inspection, GO gauges must always be put into conditions of maximum impassability (no help to enter).
  • NOT-GO gauges check a single feature at a time.
  • NOT-GO gauges must be put into conditions of maximum passability (every chance given to enter, so refusal is meaningful).

2.3 Gauge Tolerance & Wear Allowance

Gauges, like any other manufactured object, cannot be made to exact size. The tolerance permitted in their manufacture is called the gauge maker's tolerance or simply gauge tolerance.

Trade-off:

  • If gauge tolerance is too large, the gauge itself may incorrectly accept bad parts or reject good ones.
  • If gauge tolerance is too small, the gauge becomes prohibitively expensive to manufacture.

Industry rule of thumb: gauge tolerance ≈ 10 % of the work tolerance. A 10× more accurate gauge contributes about 1 % to the variance of the measurement decision, which is negligible.

Wear Allowance — Why Only on the GO Gauge

The GO gauge is the one repeatedly passing through holes or over shafts during 100 % inspection. It wears.

  • For a plug (hole) gauge: as the GO plug slides in and out of holes, its diameter decreases over time.
  • For a snap or ring (shaft) gauge: as the GO snap slides over shafts, the opening increases over time.

To extend the useful life of the GO gauge, it is initially manufactured with a small wear allowance — its starting size is offset toward the MML side of the work tolerance zone, so that even after some wear, it is still close to checking MML.

  • GO plug gauge: starts larger than LLH; wear shrinks it toward LLH. (Wear allowance added to LLH.)
  • GO snap gauge: starts smaller than HLS; wear enlarges its opening toward HLS. (Wear allowance subtracted from HLS.)
  • NOT-GO gauges ideally never enter at all, so they wear negligibly — no wear allowance is applied.

Standard practice: wear allowance ≈ 10 % of the gauge tolerance.

Disposition of Gauge Tolerance (Present British / Indian System)

The slide example follows the convention that the entire gauge tolerance lies inside the work tolerance zone. This is to ensure that the gauge can never accept a part that lies outside the work tolerance. The gauge tolerance is therefore disposed unilaterally into the metal of the work:

  • GO plug gauge: nominal at (LLH + wear allowance); gauge tolerance grows toward HLH side, i.e., the gauge can only be the nominal size or slightly larger.
  • NOT-GO plug gauge: nominal at HLH; gauge tolerance grows toward LLH side, i.e., the gauge can only be the nominal size or slightly smaller.
  • GO snap gauge: nominal at (HLS − wear allowance); gauge tolerance grows toward LLS side.
  • NOT-GO snap gauge: nominal at LLS; gauge tolerance grows toward HLS side.

2.4 Linear Measurement — Line vs End Standards

Linear measurement is the measurement of distance. There are three geometric scenarios:

  1. Distance between two lines (e.g., engraved marks on a ruler).
  2. Distance between two surfaces (e.g., between two parallel faces of a block).
  3. Distance between a line and a surface (e.g., scribed mark to an edge).

Line Standard

The unit of length is the distance between two engraved lines. Historic example: the original platinum-iridium International Prototype Metre held at Sèvres, France, was a line standard — the metre was defined as the distance between two scratch marks at 0 °C.

Everyday examples: a steel rule, a yardstick, a metre stick.

End Standard

The unit of length is the distance between two flat parallel surfaces. The measurement is the physical length of a piece of material.

Examples: slip gauges (gauge blocks), the anvils of a micrometer, the jaws of a Vernier caliper, end bars.

Design Considerations for Linear-Measurement Instruments

Linear-measurement instruments must meet stringent accuracy and precision demands while remaining simple to operate and economically priced. Important design considerations:

  • Accuracy of line graduations. For line-graduated instruments, the measuring accuracy depends on the original accuracy of the engraved lines. Excessive thickness or poor definition affects readings.
  • Compensation against wear. Any scaled instrument is suspect unless it provides wear compensation — adjustment screws, zero-set provisions, etc.
  • Attachments. Versatility-boosting attachments (depth rods on a Vernier caliper, knife-edge anvils on a micrometer) help — but every unused or misused attachment is a source of accumulated error.
  • Operator "feel". Calipers and outside micrometers depend on the operator's feel for proper contact pressure. Modern micrometers include ratchet stops to remove this variability. Operators must still be properly trained.
  • Principle of Alignment (Abbe's Principle). The line of measurement and the line of dimension being measured should be coincident. When they are not, even small angular errors produce cosine-error or sine-error contributions. The Vernier caliper inherently violates Abbe's principle (the scale is offset from the jaws); the micrometer respects it (the scale axis is the same as the measurement axis), which is why micrometers are inherently more accurate than calipers of the same nominal grade.
  • Readability. One important element of reliability is readability. A micrometer's smallest scale division is much larger than a steel rule's 0.1 mm, yet the micrometer's least count is finer (0.01 mm). Easier-to-read scales reduce reading errors.
  • Dial and digital versions. Dial gauges and digital readouts add convenience. Digital instruments are preferred when cost is not an issue.
  • Contact force. Whenever the instrument touches the workpiece, the contact force must be optimum — large enough to ensure repeatable contact, small enough to avoid distortion.

Important Measurement Devices & Setups

(a) Surface Plate

A surface plate is a hard, solid, horizontal flat plate used as the reference plane (datum) for precision inspection, marking-out, and tooling set-up. Every measurement is taken with respect to it.

Because the plate is the datum for everything, it must be finished to a high degree of flatness, robust against repeated contact with metallic workpieces, and resistant to wear and tear.

Modern surface plates are almost always granite, not cast iron:

  • Not vulnerable to rust.
  • Non-magnetic — important for workshops dealing with magnetic chucks or fine ferrous particles.
  • Free from burrs or protrusions because of granite's very fine grain structure.
  • Granite is also dimensionally stable over decades — cast iron creeps and warps over years.
(b) V-Block

A V-block is used for inspection of jobs with a circular cross-section. Its main purpose is to hold cylindrical workpieces for measurement.

The cylindrical surface rests firmly on the two sides of the "V", and by symmetry, the cylinder's axis is parallel to both the base and the sides of the V-block. The block thus locates a cylinder repeatably in 3-D, regardless of cylinder diameter (within range).

Construction: high-grade steel, hardened above 60 Rockwell C, ground to high precision. Magnetic V-blocks include a switchable magnet to hold the workpiece in place.

(c) Depth Gauge

A depth gauge is the preferred instrument for measuring holes, grooves and recesses. It has:

  • A graduated blade (or rod) that descends into the feature being measured.
  • A head that rests on the surrounding reference surface.
  • A screw clamp to lock the reading.

Two types: rod type (slim, for deep narrow features) and blade type (wider, for stepped recesses).

(d) Combination Set

The combination set is a clever tool comprising three devices on one graduated steel rule:

  1. Combination square (square head + steel rule) — measures depths or heights at 90°.
  2. Protractor head — measures angles of jobs.
  3. Centre head — finds the centre of a round cross-section (by virtue of two equal-angle faces meeting at the rule axis).
(e) Calipers

Calipers are used where a steel rule alone cannot accurately span a feature — e.g., the diameter of a cylinder where the rule cannot be positioned diametrically.

Procedure (three-step):

  1. Open the caliper to span the job.
  2. Lock the setting.
  3. Transfer the locked caliper to a steel rule to read the dimension.

Types: outside caliper (measures external dimensions, like a shaft diameter), inside caliper (internal, like a bore), transfer caliper (with a movable pivot, used for inaccessible inside dimensions like ring-groove diameters).

2.5 Slip Gauges

Slip gauges (a.k.a. gauge blocks, or by their inventor's name Johansson blocks) are the workhorse end-standards of precision measurement.

What They Are

  • Rectangular blocks of hardened alloy steel (or carbide), with two opposite faces lapped flat and parallel to extreme precision.
  • Economical — same coefficient of thermal expansion as the majority of steel components, so they expand together with the parts they measure, cancelling temperature effects.
  • Hardening makes them resistant to wear from repeated handling.
  • After hardening they are stabilised at a sub-zero temperature (sometimes called "cryogenic treatment" or "deep freezing") to relieve residual stresses from heat treatment, ensuring long-term dimensional stability.
  • The measuring faces are finished by lapping — an abrasive super-finishing process — to achieve:
    • High dimensional accuracy.
    • Excellent flatness.
    • Mirror-grade surface finish (<0.025 µm).
  • The block's nominal height is engraved on a rectangular face, along with a symbol indicating which two planes are the measured faces.

The three most important requirements of a slip gauge are: length between measuring faces, flatness of measuring faces, and surface condition of measuring faces.

Materials

Beyond alloy steel, carbide gauge blocks exist for harsh use:

  • Superior wear resistance and longer life.
  • Low coefficient of thermal expansion.
  • However, expensive — used where rapid wear of steel gauges is unacceptable.

Grades

Slip gauges are classified into grades based on their guaranteed accuracy. Common grades (rough idea — exact tolerances per standard):

  • Grade 00 / K — reference standard for calibration of other gauges.
  • Grade 0 — inspection grade, high-precision measurements.
  • Grade 1 — general inspection.
  • Grade 2 — workshop grade, rough setup work.

Within a grade, you choose a set (e.g., 87-piece, 112-piece) that gives sufficient combinations to build any required size to the nearest micrometre.

Wringing — The Phenomenon of Free Adhesion

Definition
Wringing
The phenomenon of adhesion of two flat smooth surfaces when brought into close contact. The adhesion force is sufficient that the stack of blocks behaves as a single block — it can be picked up and moved without separation, and even resists a substantial perpendicular pull.

This is critical because slip gauges are sold in sets; you build any required size by wringing together a few blocks. If a thin air film or dirt layer separated the blocks, the stack length would be greater than the sum of nominal heights — and the measurement would be wrong.

When two surfaces are brought into contact, some space ordinarily exists between them due to:

  • Microscopic surface irregularities.
  • Presence of dirt, oil, grease, or air pockets.

For ordinary surfaces, this gap is non-negligible and varies. But slip-gauge surfaces are so flat and clean that the gap between them collapses to negligible thickness — so wrung stack length ≈ sum of individual heights.

The Two-Factor Explanation of Wringing
(1) Atmospheric pressure

When two blocks are brought into contact and the air between them is squeezed out, the outside atmospheric pressure (~1 bar = 10⁵ N/m²) presses on the exposed outer faces. A typical slip-gauge face area is ~9 mm × 35 mm = 315 mm² = 3.15 × 10⁻⁴ m². Atmospheric pressure on this is ~31.5 N — enough to hold a stack of moderate weight against perpendicular pull.

(2) Molecular adhesion

When two surfaces are truly close (separated by less than a few nanometres), Van der Waals forces and short-range electrostatic interactions kick in. The lapped slip-gauge surfaces are flat enough that vast portions of the two faces come into this intimate proximity, producing a high effective adhesion.

The slide adds a remarkable subtlety: lapping is a super-finishing operation in which material is removed at the molecular level. Some surface molecules are lost during lapping, leaving the surface "receptive" to the molecules of the mating surface. When two such surfaces are brought into contact, a high-magnitude molecular adhesion forms.

A stack of n wrung slip gauges has a length equal to the sum of the individual heights — to within sub-micrometre error.

Definition
Wringability
The ability of two surfaces to adhere tightly to each other in the absence of external means.

Minimum conditions for wringability:
  • Surface finish ≤ 0.025 µm (i.e., 25 nm).
  • Flatness ≤ 0.13 µm.
Technique of Wringing (Four Steps)
  1. Clean the faces of both gauges thoroughly (lint-free cloth, light oil film if specified).
  2. Overlap the gauging surfaces by about one-fourth of their length.
  3. Apply moderate pressure and slide one block perpendicularly across the other.
  4. Gently rotate one of the blocks until it is fully aligned with the other. The blocks are now wrung; lift the stack as a single piece and use.

To unwring, slide them apart with a sliding motion — never pull straight apart, as this can damage the lapped faces.

2.6 Angular Measurement

Angles are as important as lengths in machine design. Common needs:

  • Tapers of bores (Morse tapers, machine spindles).
  • Flank angle and included angle of gear teeth.
  • Angles of jigs, fixtures, and cutting tools.
  • Straightness, parallelism, and flatness of machine parts — measured indirectly via small angles (e.g., the autocollimator's angle reading is itself a measure of error of alignment).

The instruments range from very simple to highly sophisticated.

(1) Simple Protractor

A basic instrument — a graduated semicircle with a swinging arm.

  • Least count: typically 1° for small ones, ½° for large.
  • Limited use in engineering metrology; suitable for layout work, not for precision inspection.

(2) Universal Bevel Protractor

A massively upgraded protractor with:

  • A main scale graduated in degrees (0° – 360°, divided in four 90° arcs).
  • A Vernier scale graduated in minutes of arc (typically 5' least count).
  • An adjustable blade that rotates about the axis of the scale.
  • An acute angle attachment for awkward features.
  • A base plate that locates against the workpiece datum.

Reading procedure: align the blade with the workpiece feature; read the integer degrees from the main scale; read the minutes from the Vernier scale (counting the alignment of Vernier division with main-scale division — same logic as a Vernier caliper).

(3) Sine Bar — Using Trigonometry

A sine bar measures angles by setting up a triangle whose hypotenuse is the bar and whose opposite side is built up using slip gauges. The angle is computed by trigonometry.

Construction
  • A precision steel bar with two identical hardened cylindrical rollers fitted near each end.
  • The roller centres are precisely L (typically 100, 200 or 300 mm) apart.
  • The bar's upper surface is flat and parallel to the line joining the roller centres.
  • Relief holes in the bar reduce weight.
Working Principle

Place the sine bar on a surface plate (the datum). One roller rests directly on the plate. Under the other roller, stack slip gauges of total height h.

The angle θ between the bar's top surface and the datum is given by:

Sine Bar Principle sin θ = h / L

Place the workpiece on the inclined upper surface. By trial-and-error (or direct calculation), adjust the slip-gauge stack until the workpiece feature is parallel to the datum (verified by a dial gauge sweeping over the feature with zero deflection). The slip-gauge stack height then gives the workpiece angle.

Why Sine Bars Fail Above 45°

It is not recommended to use a sine bar for angles greater than 45°. The reason is mathematical: above 45°, the sine function grows slowly while the slip-gauge stack must grow rapidly. Equivalently, any error in slip-gauge height or in the sine bar's roller distance is magnified when converted back to angle.

Differentiate sin θ = h/L → cos θ · dθ = dh/L → dθ = dh / (L cos θ). As θ → 90°, cos θ → 0, so the angular error per unit slip-gauge error blows up.

For angles > 45°, the workpiece is usually mounted with its complementary face on the sine bar, measuring (90° − θ) instead.

(4) Angle Gauges

An angle gauge is the angular analogue of a slip gauge. Made of high-grade wear-resistant steel, they come as a standard set of blocks that can be wrung together to build any required angle.

The Critical Feature: Subtraction

Slip gauges can only be added — stacking them increases length. Angle gauges can be both added and subtracted:

  • If two angle gauges are placed face-to-face with their angles in the same direction, the angles add: 30° + 5° = 35°.
  • If they are placed with their angles in opposite directions, the angles subtract: 30° − 5° = 25°.

This doubling of arithmetic operations means a set of just 10 angle gauges can build a huge range of angles in tiny increments — far more than 10 slip gauges could produce in linear dimensions.

Typical sets include blocks of 1°, 3°, 9°, 27°, 41° (large series) and 1', 3', 9', 27' (small series), plus a 90° block. Combinations cover 0° to 90° in 1' increments.

Uses
  • Tool-room measurement and calibration — checking angles on die inserts, compound angles of cutting tools.
  • Machine-shop setup — setting the revolving centre of a magnetic chuck, grinding notches on a cylindrical grinding machine.

(5) Autocollimator

An autocollimator is an optical instrument that measures very small angles with extreme sensitivity. Used for:

  • Precision alignment — verifying that two distant surfaces are parallel.
  • Detection of angular movement.
  • Verification of angle standards (sine bars, angle gauges).
  • Measuring straightness, parallelism and flatness of machine ways.
Working Principle

Inside the autocollimator:

  1. A light source illuminates a graticule (typically cross-hairs).
  2. A collimating lens turns the divergent rays into a parallel beam.
  3. The parallel beam exits the instrument and strikes a plane reflector placed on the workpiece.
  4. If the reflector is exactly perpendicular to the beam (θ = 0°), the beam returns straight back along itself, and the cross-hair image overlaps the original.
  5. If the reflector is tilted by angle θ, the beam is reflected at angle 2θ. After returning through the same lens, the image of the cross-hair is laterally displaced by x = 2 f tan θ ≈ 2 f θ (for small θ), where f is the focal length.
  6. The lateral displacement x is measured on a graduated eyepiece (or a position-sensitive photodetector), converting to angle via θ = x / (2 f).

The auto-collimator can resolve angles down to fractions of an arcsecond (1" = 1/3600°). This is why it is the instrument of choice for inspecting precision machine ways and large reference surfaces.

2.7 Surface Quality Inspection

No machined surface is geometrically perfect. Microscopically, every surface is a succession of hills and valleys varying in both height and spacing. The systematic study of these surface features is critical because surface quality affects:

  • Friction and wear — rougher surfaces wear faster, generate more heat.
  • Fatigue strength — surface roughness valleys are stress concentrators that crack-initiate.
  • Sealing — gaskets and O-rings need surfaces below a certain roughness to seal.
  • Optical performance — lenses and mirrors require nanometre-level smoothness.
  • Aesthetics — finish affects perceived quality.
  • Coating adhesion — a totally smooth surface may not hold paint or plating; a controlled roughness is often needed.

Sources of Surface Irregularity

  • Feed marks of cutting tools — the deterministic spiral grooves left by a single-point cutting tool on a lathe.
  • Chatter marks — from vibrations during the manufacturing operation; produce wavy patterns.
  • Material rupture — when the cutting process tears rather than shears, leaving torn surface.
  • Workpiece deformation — under cutting forces, the workpiece bends, leaving an inconsistent surface.
  • Machine-tool irregularities — lack of straightness of guideways translates into surface waviness.

A surface produced by a single-point cutting tool has roughness that is uniformly spaced and directional — you can see the feed marks clearly. A finish-machined surface (e.g., ground or lapped) has roughness that is irregular and non-directional.

Vocabulary of a Surface Profile

Looking at a magnified profile cross-section, several features are defined:

Roughness
The finer irregularities of the surface texture. Spacing = distance between successive peaks/ridges of the predominant pattern. Height = arithmetic average deviation (in µm) measured perpendicular to the centre line.
Waviness
The more widely spaced component of surface texture, on which roughness is superimposed. Waviness is an error in form due to incorrect geometry of the tool producing the surface (e.g., a wobbly spindle). Spacing = width between successive wave peaks/valleys. Height = peak-to-valley distance.
Lay
The direction of the predominant surface pattern, determined by the production process. Symbols on drawings indicate lay: ∥ (parallel to edge), ⊥ (perpendicular), × (crossed), M (multidirectional), C (circular), R (radial).
Surface texture
The complete pattern of repetitive or random deviations from the nominal surface — encompassing roughness, waviness, lay, and any flaws.
Flaws
Unintentional irregularities — scratches, dents, inclusions — that are not part of the regular texture.
Centre line / Mean line
A reference line drawn through the profile such that the area above equals the area below — the "average" line of the surface.
Traversing length / Sampling length
The length of the surface over which the measurement is taken.

Three Quantitative Measures of Roughness

(1) Centre-Line Average (Ra)

Also called arithmetic average roughness. Take the absolute deviations of the profile from the mean line, average them over the sampling length.

Ra (Centre-Line Average) Ra = (A₁ + A₂ + … + AN) / L

where Ai are the absolute areas above/below the mean line, L = sampling length

Equivalently, for discrete sampled heights hi (each measured from the mean line, taken as absolute values):

Ra = (|h₁| + |h₂| + … + |hn|) / n

Ra is the most widely used surface-roughness parameter because it is robust to outliers and easy to compute.

(2) Root-Mean-Square (Rq or RMS)

The square root of the mean of the squared deviations:

RRMS hRMS = √[ (h₁² + h₂² + … + hn²) / n ]

RMS is mathematically more rigorous than Ra (it is the standard deviation of the surface profile), and is always slightly larger than Ra for the same surface. For sinusoidal surfaces, Rq / Ra = π/(2√2) ≈ 1.11.

(3) Ten-Point Height (Rz)

The average of the five highest peaks minus the average of the five lowest valleys:

Rz (Ten-point average height) Rz = [(h₁+h₃+h₅+h₇+h₉) − (h₂+h₄+h₆+h₈+h₁₀)] / 5 × (1000 / vertical magnification)

(Peaks are odd-indexed, valleys even-indexed in this convention.)

Rz emphasises the extreme features of the profile, ignoring the bulk — useful when fatigue or sealing is the concern (since cracks and leaks start at the extremes).

Two Approaches to Surface-Finish Measurement

(a) Comparison Method

The simpler of the two but more subjective:

  • Visual comparison — view the surface against a roughness comparison specimen (a set of reference plates with known Ra values). Choose the one that "looks the same".
  • Tactile comparison — touch the surface and compare to references. Surprisingly, touch is somewhat more sensitive than vision for medium roughness.

Limitations: the view can be deceptive — two surfaces that look identical can be very different. Touch is subjective and depends on the operator's training. Not reliable for precision work.

(b) Direct (Stylus) Method

The stylus system is the most popular method. A diamond-tipped stylus is drawn across the workpiece surface; its vertical motions are converted into electrical signals (typically via an LVDT or piezoelectric pickup) that are proportional to the heights of the asperities. The signal can be:

  • Filtered to separate roughness from waviness.
  • Displayed as a profile on a hardcopy unit or screen.
  • Stored on magnetisable media for later analysis.
  • Processed numerically to extract Ra, Rq, Rz, and other parameters.

Stylus instruments include the venerable Taylor-Hobson Talysurf and modern digital profilometers.

Wavelength, Frequency, and Cut-Off

As the stylus traverses the surface, its vertical motion produces a temporal signal. The frequency of this signal depends on the traverse speed and the surface wavelength:

Stylus signal frequency f = v / λ

f = signal frequency (Hz);  v = traverse speed (mm/s);  λ = surface wavelength (mm)
Cut-Off Length

A real surface profile contains a mixture of wavelengths — from very fine roughness (small λ) to large-scale waviness and form errors (large λ). To extract roughness alone, the signal is filtered to discard wavelengths longer than a chosen cut-off length.

  • For surfaces produced by single-point cutting tools, cut-off should not exceed one feed spacing.
  • For fine irregular surfaces (ground, lapped), cut-off 0.8 mm is recommended.

Optical Measurement & Interferometry

3.1 Why Optics in Metrology?

Light has properties that make it uniquely suited to dimensional metrology:

  • It travels in straight lines at the speed c (in vacuum), giving us an absolute reference.
  • Its wavelength is fixed for a given colour (especially for laser light) and is naturally on the scale of submicrometres — perfect for fine measurement.
  • It does not physically touch the workpiece — eliminating contact-deformation errors.
  • It can be deflected, focused, split, and reunited using lenses and mirrors, enabling huge magnification of small dimensional changes.

The slide states: "Light waves provide the best standard for length" — and indeed, the SI metre is now defined in terms of the speed of light (1 metre = the distance light travels in 1/299,792,458 of a second).

Optical measurement is simple, easy, accurate, and reliable. But it imposes design demands: the projected image must be clear, sharp, and dimensionally accurate, and all the mechanical and electronic elements must be compatible with the main optical system.

Essential Features of an Optical Instrument

  1. A light source — incandescent, LED, or laser, depending on application.
  2. A condensing/collimating lens system to direct the light past the workpiece and into the optical system.
  3. A suitable stage or table to position the workpiece — preferably with translation in two directions and rotation about a vertical axis.
  4. The projection optics — lenses and mirrors that shape and direct the image.
  5. A viewing screen or eyepiece on which the image is received.
  6. Measuring and recording devices — graduated reticles, micrometer screws, digital encoders, or imaging sensors.

The Four Application Domains of Optics in Metrology

  1. Magnification — making small features visible/measurable. Microscopes, projectors.
  2. Alignment — verifying that two distant objects or surfaces share a common axis. Autocollimators, alignment telescopes.
  3. Interferometry — exploiting wave interference for sub-micrometre measurements. Laser interferometers.
  4. Absolute standard of length — the SI metre itself.

3.2 Tool Maker's Microscope (TMM)

A microscope serves two roles in metrology: high magnification of small features, and a convenient means for taking readings via cross-hair reticles or graduated stages.

The Tool Maker's Microscope is a multifunctional shop-floor instrument designed primarily for measurement (not biology). It enables both:

  • Absolute measurements — actual numerical dimensions of features.
  • Comparative measurements — comparing one feature to a reference.

The main use of a TMM is to measure the shape, size, angle, and position of small components that fit within its measuring range — typically gears, threads, small tools, drill bits, taps, dies, and other tool-room artifacts.

Operating Principle

Conceptually a TMM is a compound microscope with:

  • An objective lens close to the workpiece, forming a magnified real image.
  • An eyepiece further magnifying that image for viewing.
  • A cross-hair reticle in the eyepiece, used to align with workpiece features.
  • A graduated stage (X-Y) with micrometer screws or digital readouts for moving the workpiece in known steps.

To measure, say, the diameter of a drilled hole: bring the cross-hair to one edge, zero the X-readout; move the stage until the cross-hair reaches the opposite edge; the X displacement is the diameter.

3.3 Optical Interference — Wave Theory of Light

A ray of light is composed of an infinite number of waves of equal wavelength. The value of the wavelength determines the colour: red ~ 700 nm, green ~ 550 nm, blue ~ 450 nm.

When two light rays meet, their amplitudes superpose. This produces the phenomenon of interference, which is at the heart of all interferometric metrology.

Two Rays In Phase (Constructive Interference)

Suppose two rays have amplitudes yA and yB respectively, and they are in phase (peaks align with peaks, troughs with troughs). The resultant wave has amplitude:

yR = yA + yB

The combined wave is brighter than either component — this is constructive interference.

Two Rays Out of Phase by α (Partial Interference)

If the two rays have a phase difference α (not 0°, not 180°):

yR = (yA + yB) cos(α / 2)

Note: as α varies from 0° to 180°, cos(α/2) goes from 1 to 0 — the resultant amplitude smoothly decreases.

Two Rays Out of Phase by 180° (Destructive Interference)

If the two rays have equal wavelength and amplitude but are 180° out of phase (peak meets trough), they cancel completely:

yR = 0  →  DARKNESS

This is complete destructive interference — and it is the key to interferometric measurement. Two beams of light, properly combined, can produce darkness — a deeply non-intuitive result that arises because light is a wave, not a particle.

Splitting a Single Source

One important property of light: light from a single source can be split into two component rays (using a half-silvered mirror, a beam splitter, or a diffraction grating). Two rays split from the same source are coherent — they maintain a fixed phase relationship — which is required to produce stable interference fringes.

Formation of Fringes

Place two coherent point sources A and B near each other. On a distant screen, the light from A and B traverses different path lengths to different points. At points where the path difference is an integer multiple of λ, the rays arrive in phase → bright fringe. At points where the path difference is a half-integer multiple of λ (λ/2, 3λ/2, …), the rays arrive 180° out of phase → dark fringe.

The result on the screen is an alternating pattern of bright and dark bands: O₁ bright, O₂ dark, O₃ bright, … . This is Young's double-slit experiment, the foundational demonstration of light's wave nature.

3.4 Interferometry

The number of fringes that appear in a given length on the screen is a measure of the distance between the two point sources (or more generally, of the path difference). Counting fringes is therefore a way to measure distance — with a resolution of half a wavelength per fringe.

Definitions
Interferometry / Interferometer
The measurement technique that uses optical interference to measure very small linear dimensions. The instrument used for these measurements is an interferometer.

For visible light (λ ≈ 500 nm), one fringe corresponds to about 250 nm of optical path difference — meaning interferometers routinely measure to a fraction of a wavelength, deep into the sub-micrometre regime.

Optical-Flat Fringes

An optical flat is a precisely flat reference glass (flatness ≤ λ/10). When placed over a workpiece surface at a very small angle θ, light reflects off both:

  • The bottom face of the optical flat.
  • The top face of the workpiece.

These two reflections interfere. Where the air gap between the surfaces is an integer multiple of λ/2, the reflections cancel → dark fringe. Where it is a half-integer multiple, they add → bright fringe. The result is a series of alternating dark and bright bands — contour lines of constant gap thickness.

By examining the shape of these fringes, you can deduce the workpiece's surface flatness:

Fringe pattern Surface condition
A — straight, parallel, evenly spaced Block is nearly flat along its length
B — fringes curve toward the line of contact Surface is convex and high in the centre
C — fringes curve away from line of contact Surface is concave and low in the centre
D — straight at one end, becoming curved Surface flat at one end but becoming convex toward the other
E — fringes progressively closer-spaced toward a corner Surface is progressively lower toward the bottom-left corner
F — two distinct contact areas with fringes elsewhere Two points of contact higher than the rest of the surface

Each fringe spacing corresponds to a height difference of λ/2 (about 0.3 µm for visible light). So if you count five fringes across a 50 mm length, the height difference across that length is 5 × λ/2 ≈ 1.5 µm — that's the surface's deviation from flat.

3.5 Laser Interferometer

Why Lasers?

Ordinary light sources (incandescent bulbs, mercury vapour lamps) emit light over a range of wavelengths and from many independent atoms — making the light incoherent over distances longer than micrometres. Lasers, by contrast, emit:

  • Monochromatic light (a single, well-defined wavelength).
  • Coherent light (the wave-train remains phase-locked over metres or even kilometres).
  • Collimated light (the beam stays parallel over long distances).
  • Intense light (high photon flux, allowing precision detection).

These properties make lasers the natural choice for interferometric measurement of large displacements. The slide notes: "from the measurement point of view, laser interferometry can be used for measurements of small diameters as well as large displacements." Modern industrial laser interferometers can measure positions up to many metres with nanometre resolution.

Typical Layout (Michelson-style)

Components and Beam Path
  1. A helium-neon (HeNe) laser emits a beam of λ ≈ 632.8 nm.
  2. The beam enters the fixed unit, striking a semi-reflector (beam splitter) P.
  3. One portion of the beam continues to a fixed corner-cube retroreflector Q inside the fixed unit; the other portion exits to a corner-cube retroreflector R on the moving unit.
  4. Both beams return from Q and R and meet at a second semi-reflector S, where they recombine.
  5. The recombined beam falls on photodiodes, which detect the alternating bright/dark intensity as the moving unit translates.
  6. The photodiode signal is amplified and fed to a digital counter, which counts each fringe transition.
  7. Each fringe = λ/2 of moving-unit displacement (or some defined fraction with phase-quadrature electronics down to λ/1024 or finer).

By counting fringes, the total displacement of the moving unit is reconstructed with nanometre resolution.

Applications

  • Calibration of CMM axes.
  • Verification of CNC machine-tool positioning accuracy.
  • Measurement of straightness, squareness, angular pitch, yaw of machine slides.
  • Surface topography measurement (white-light interferometers).

White-Light Interferometer

A specialised laser interferometer that uses a broad-spectrum (white) light source instead of a single-wavelength laser. White light's short coherence length means interference is only observed when the two beam paths are nearly equal — this enables absolute distance measurement (no ambiguity from counting fringes wrong) and is ideal for 3-D surface topography.

Other Automated Measurement Systems

The slides showcase several measurement systems used in automated production:

  • Coordinate Measuring Machines (CMM) — a touch-probe (or non-contact optical probe) on a precise X-Y-Z gantry traces the workpiece surface and digitises its geometry, comparing it to the CAD model.
  • Bruker Alicona µCMM — a high-resolution focus-variation microscope CMM, used for very small features (e.g., cutting-tool edge geometry).
  • Large-volume optical scanning stations — for inspecting whole car bodies, aircraft assemblies, etc.

Quality Management — Philosophy & Practice

4.1 What is Quality?

"Quality" is a word everyone uses but few define rigorously. The slides walk through several products — a Mercedes-Benz, a basket of fruit, an LG washing machine, smartphones, a defensive spike for pigeons, Nike sneakers — and ask: what makes each "good quality"?

The answer is not the same in each case. A Mercedes is "quality" because of luxury, smoothness, reliability and brand. Fruit is "quality" because of freshness, taste, and absence of bruising. A washing machine is "quality" because of cleaning performance, durability, and quiet operation. The defensive spike is "quality" if it keeps pigeons off the ledge effectively. Sneakers are "quality" if they last, fit, and look right.

Quality is therefore context-dependent — defined by the purpose of the product and the expectations of its user.

Working Definition
Quality
The ability to meet a customer's expectations. Quality is not absolute "goodness"; it is fitness-for-purpose as judged by the user.

This definition has profound implications:

  • You cannot define quality without first understanding the customer.
  • The same product can be "high quality" for one customer and "low quality" for another with different expectations.
  • As customer expectations evolve, the definition of quality evolves — and the standard for "good enough" rises over time.

Modern quality theorists (Juran, Crosby, Deming) emphasise variations on this theme:

  • Juran: "Fitness for use."
  • Crosby: "Conformance to requirements."
  • Deming: "Quality is the responsibility of management."
  • ISO 9000: "Degree to which a set of inherent characteristics fulfils requirements."

4.2 What is Management?

Working Definition
Management
The act of getting people together to accomplish desired goals and objectives using available resources efficiently (with minimum waste) and effectively (achieving the intended result).

The phrase "getting people together" is significant. Management is fundamentally about people, not processes or machines. Efficient resource use without effective outcomes is busywork; effective outcomes achieved with massive waste are unsustainable. Management balances both.

4.3 What is Quality Management?

Working Definition
Quality Management
A process that ensures the quality of the product throughout its entire life cycle — from concept and design through manufacturing, delivery, use, and eventual disposal.

"Throughout its life cycle" is the operative phrase. Quality is not a property installed at final inspection — it is built in at every stage. By the time a part reaches final inspection, its quality is fixed; you can only sort good from bad.

The Four Pillars of Quality Management

  1. Quality Planning. The first step. Requirements must be identified, criteria must be set, and important procedures must be recognised as part of the plan. Outputs: quality plans, target metrics, inspection criteria.
  2. Quality Control. Review of product/service quality after the fact. Inspection and testing are necessary to identify problems and defects that need correction. Outputs: defect reports, accept/reject decisions, scrap counts.
  3. Quality Assurance. The proactive prevention of defects. Companies need to assure that defects are avoided in manufacturing or in service delivery. Quality assurance guarantees consistent results. Outputs: process audits, certifications, supplier qualifications.
  4. Quality Improvement. There is always room for improvement. Through structured improvement, results can be measured and incremental gains in products or services can be made. Outputs: Kaizen events, redesigns, capability improvements.
The crucial conceptual difference: Quality Control is reactive — it detects defects after they happen. Quality Assurance is proactive — it prevents defects from happening. A factory that relies only on QC will produce defects and find them; a factory with strong QA will produce far fewer defects in the first place. The modern philosophy is to shift effort from QC to QA — from detection to prevention.

4.4 Manufacturing the Toyota Way

The Toyota Production System (TPS), developed in post-WWII Japan by Taiichi Ohno and Shigeo Shingo, transformed global manufacturing. Its three iconic concepts:

(a) Just-In-Time (JIT)

Just-In-Time is the philosophy that materials and components should arrive at each production step exactly when needed — not earlier, not later, in the exact quantity required.

Why JIT Works
  • Eliminates storage inventory. Inventory ties up capital, occupies floor space, requires handling, and ages.
  • Exposes problems. When buffer stocks are present, defects, machine breakdowns, and process inefficiencies are hidden. When buffers shrink, problems become immediately visible, forcing them to be solved.
  • Forces quality. A JIT system cannot tolerate defective parts — there is no time to wait for replacements. Quality becomes mandatory.
  • Reduces waste in all forms — overproduction, waiting, transportation, over-processing, inventory, motion, and defects (the "seven wastes" of lean manufacturing).

JIT is the central principle of lean manufacturing, now widely adopted by Toyota, McDonald's, Apple, Dell, and countless other companies.

(b) Kanban (literally "sign-board")

JIT requires precise coordination between production steps. Kanban is the visual control system that makes JIT work in practice. The original Kanban used physical cards attached to bins of parts; modern Kanban uses electronic boards.

How Kanban Works
  • Each production step has a set of physical or virtual cards indicating "produce this part" or "request this part".
  • When a downstream step consumes a part, a card flows back to the upstream step authorising the next part's production.
  • Without a Kanban, the upstream step does not produce — preventing overproduction.
  • The system is pull-based — production is triggered by downstream demand, not pushed by upstream schedules.
Kanban Board for Software / Knowledge Work

Modern adaptation: a board with columns labelled Backlog → To Do → Ongoing → Done. Each task is a card. As work progresses, cards move rightward. Limits on work-in-progress (WIP) in each column prevent overloading. This mirrors the original goods-in / goods-in-production / goods-out flow.

(c) Kaizen — Continuous Improvement

Kaizen (改善, literally "good change") is the philosophy of small, continuous, structured improvements. Rather than one big revolution, Kaizen advocates a steady stream of tiny improvements made by everyone, every day.

The 5 S Framework

The most famous operational tool from Kaizen is the 5 S framework — five Japanese words all starting with S — for organising and standardising a workspace:

  • Seiri (整理) — Sort. Remove all unnecessary items from the workspace. If you don't use it, get rid of it. Identify each remaining item and decide whether it stays.
  • Seiton (整頓) — Set in Order. Organise the remaining items so the most-used are most accessible. Every tool has a place; every place has a tool. Marked outlines on workbenches, colour-coded bins, labelled drawers.
  • Seiso (清掃) — Shine. Clean the workspace thoroughly and inspect while cleaning. Cleaning reveals issues — leaks, loose bolts, wear patterns — that would otherwise go unnoticed.
  • Seiketsu (整潔) — Standardise. Create written routines and procedures to maintain the first three S's. Everyone follows the same procedure; deviations are visible.
  • Shitsuke (躾) — Sustain. Build the habits, discipline, and training that keep the 5 S culture going. The hardest of the five, because it requires culture change over time.
Other Pillars of Kaizen
  • Elimination of waste (the seven wastes mentioned earlier).
  • Standardization of best practices.
  • Teamwork — improvement is a group activity.
  • Self-discipline — every worker takes responsibility for their work area.
  • Improved morale — Kaizen empowers workers to suggest improvements, raising engagement.
  • Quality circles — small group meetings to identify and solve quality problems.
  • Improvement suggestions — formal systems to collect and act on worker ideas.
Kaizen vs No-Kaizen
Without Kaizen With Kaizen
No structure to the improvement process; few set procedures Consistent, ongoing process of improvement
Goals not defined or are vague/difficult to measure Improvement process has clearly defined, measurable goals
Changes to processes are infrequent; little reflection on their effectiveness Constant review of successes occurs, and the improvement process itself is evaluated
No plan for improvement; improvement is haphazard Consistency of the process leads to new, higher goals
Kaizen is the antidote to "if it ain't broke, don't fix it". In a Kaizen culture, the very absence of obvious problems is suspicious — there are always small improvements to be made. The cumulative effect of thousands of small improvements over years is greater than any single revolutionary change.

4.5 Total Quality Management (TQM)

Definition
Total Quality Management (TQM)
A comprehensive, organisation-wide approach to improving quality across every function — from the boardroom to the shop floor, from product design to customer support. The "total" in TQM means everyone in the organisation, in every activity.

TQM is not a tool or a technique — it is a management philosophy. It synthesises ideas from Deming, Juran, Crosby, Ishikawa and the Toyota Production System into a coherent approach.

Three Main Themes

TQM revolves around three themes that recur in every discussion:

  1. The customer — the ultimate definer of quality.
  2. The process — quality is built by managing the process, not by inspecting the product.
  3. The people — only engaged, empowered workers can deliver consistent quality.

The Eight Tenets of TQM

  1. Customer Focus. Ultimately the customer determines whether quality has been achieved. All efforts orient toward customer needs.
  2. Employee Involvement. Every employee, top to bottom, is involved in quality. Quality is everyone's job, not just the QC department's.
  3. Process-Centered. Identify the processes that produce quality; manage and improve them.
  4. Integrated System. All functions of the organisation — design, purchasing, production, marketing — are linked. Quality is a system property, not a departmental one.
  5. Strategic & Systematic Approach. Quality is part of long-term strategic planning, not an afterthought.
  6. Decision-Making Based on Facts. Data, not opinion, drives decisions. Statistical tools are essential.
  7. Communication. Open communication up, down, and across the organisation, so everyone understands goals and progress.
  8. Continuous Improvement. Kaizen — endless small improvements — is the engine of TQM.

The TQM Mantras

  • "First time and every time." Defects should not happen even once; the goal is zero defects, achieved by getting it right the first time.
  • "Nothing less, nothing more." Specifications should be met exactly — over-engineering is as wasteful as under-engineering.
  • "Zero waste." Every gram of material, every minute of time, every rupee of capital should be value-adding.
  • "Zero inventory." The JIT ideal — no parts sitting idle in storage.
  • "Just in time." Production aligned with demand, not with schedule.

Productivity and TQM

One of the great debates in industrial management:

Traditional View

"Quality and productivity are in tension. Improving quality requires more inspection, more rework, more rejection — all of which reduce productivity. There is an inherent trade-off."

TQM View

"Improved quality leads to improved productivity. Doing it right the first time eliminates rework, scrap, customer complaints, returns, and warranty repairs — all sources of negative productivity. Better quality = less waste = higher productivity."

The TQM view is now overwhelmingly supported by empirical evidence. Toyota's defect rates are tiny compared to its competitors, and its productivity is the highest in the industry. The two are not opposed; they are reinforcing.

5.1 Performance Standards — The Right Way to Measure Quality

An ideal outcome of any quality policy is a defect-free product that meets or exceeds customer needs. Because customer needs change over time, continuous improvement is the only sustainable strategy.

A natural temptation is to set a numerical standard — "achieve fewer than 100 defects per million" or "reduce scrap by 25 % this year". The slides warn against this:

Why Numerical Goals Often Backfire

  • Negative emotional impact. Numbers that turn out to be unachievable demoralise the team — "we worked hard and still missed".
  • Demoralising even when progress is real. A team that improves from 500 defects/million to 200 defects/million has made enormous progress — but if the goal was 100, they "failed". Real improvement gets lost.
  • Shifts emphasis to the short term. Numerical targets, especially annual ones, push managers to take short-term actions that hit the number — at the cost of long-term capability investment.
  • Numbers can be gamed. "Defects per million" can be reduced by reclassifying defects, by selective sampling, or by hiding rework. The number improves; reality doesn't.

What Works Instead

  1. Measure the trend, not the absolute level. Continuously improve the goal, and track whether quality is improving over time. The trajectory matters more than the destination.
  2. Benchmarking. Identify high-performance companies — or high-performance departments within your own company — and use their performance as the improvement target. Benchmarks are credible because they're actually achieved by someone.

Deming's famous Point 11 of his 14 Points for Management: "Eliminate work standards (quotas) on the factory floor. Substitute leadership." The TQM tradition is deeply suspicious of numerical management.

5.2 Six Sigma Quality

Six Sigma is a quality management methodology pioneered by Motorola in the 1980s and made famous by Jack Welch at GE in the 1990s. Its core idea is to limit process variability so tightly that the probability of producing a defective unit becomes vanishingly small.

The Statistical Idea

Process output is modelled as a normal distribution. The Empirical Rule (68-95-99.7) tells us:

  • 68 % of output lies within ±1σ of the mean.
  • 95 % within ±2σ.
  • 99.74 % within ±3σ.

If the specification limits are at ±3σ from the mean, then 100 % − 99.74 % = 0.26 % of output is out of specification — that is, 2,700 defective parts per million.

If specification limits are at ±6σ from the mean — and we allow for a 1.5σ "process drift" over time, which Motorola's empirical data showed was typical — the defect rate falls to a stunning 3.4 defective parts per million (DPM).

The Six-Sigma Yield Table

Notice the dramatic improvement: each additional sigma of quality reduces defects roughly by a factor of 30–60. Moving from 3σ to 6σ improves quality by more than 19,000-fold.

Why does this matter? Modern complex products have thousands of components, each of which must work. If each component has 99.38 % yield (4σ), a product with 100 components has overall yield of 0.9938100 ≈ 53.6 %. Almost half the products are defective! At 6σ quality (99.99966 % per component), the same 100-component product has yield ≈ 99.97 %. This is why 6σ became the standard for industries where reliability is paramount — aerospace, automotive, medical devices, semiconductors.

DMAIC — The Six-Sigma Improvement Cycle

Six Sigma is operationalised through a five-step methodology: DMAIC.

  1. D — Define. Define the problem to be solved, the customer requirements, the project scope and goals. Charter a team.
  2. M — Measure. Measure the current process performance. Collect baseline data. Establish what "good" and "bad" look like quantitatively.
  3. A — Analyze. Analyze the data to identify root causes of defects. Use Pareto analysis, fishbone diagrams, hypothesis testing, regression.
  4. I — Improve. Improve the process by addressing the root causes. Implement changes; verify they work.
  5. C — Control. Control the improved process so the gains are sustained. Implement SPC, document procedures, train operators, audit.

DMAIC is rigorous and data-driven — every step has deliverables, gate reviews, and statistical evidence. Practitioners are certified at coloured-belt levels: Yellow Belt (basic awareness), Green Belt (project leader), Black Belt (full-time project leader and trainer), Master Black Belt (expert and mentor).

Tools for Continuous Quality Improvement

The slides list six fundamental tools for continuous improvement work — these are often called "the seven quality tools" in the broader literature (with check sheets and histograms added):

  • Pareto diagrams. A bar chart showing causes of problems in decreasing order of frequency, with a cumulative percentage line. Embodies the 80/20 principle: usually 80 % of problems come from 20 % of causes. Tells you where to focus effort.
  • Flow charts. Visual representation of process steps. Makes the process visible; reveals redundancies, bottlenecks, decision points, and rework loops.
  • Cause-and-Effect (Ishikawa / Fishbone) diagrams. Branching diagram with the "effect" (problem) on the right and "causes" branching to the left. Standard categories: Man, Machine, Method, Material, Measurement, Mother Nature (environment) — the "6 M's". Forces structured brainstorming of all potential root causes.
  • Scatter plots. Reveal correlations between two variables. If defect rate correlates with humidity, the scatter plot will show it.
  • Multivariable charts. Display relationships among three or more variables simultaneously (e.g., variability across machines, across shifts, across time of day).
  • Matrix and 3-D plots. Higher-dimensional displays of relationships among many factors.

5.3 International Standards — ISO 9000 Family

Quality has become a precondition for participation in the global economy. Without standardised quality systems, suppliers cannot prove their reliability to customers in other countries. The ISO 9000 family of standards provides that proof.

The slides note that "without quality programs, the global economy would not exist" — because quality programs are what allowed the explosive growth of international trade and globalised supply chains. Total quality systems are no longer optional; they are required for any company doing business beyond its borders.

The Three Primary Standards

The ISO 9000 series was first published in 1987 and has been revised several times. The current generation (revised 2015) consists of three primary standards, collectively called Quality Management Systems (QMSs):

  1. ISO 9000 — Quality Management Systems: Fundamentals and Vocabulary. Defines the terminology used in the rest of the family. The "dictionary" of quality management.
  2. ISO 9001 — Quality Management Systems: Requirements. The actual standard that companies are certified against. Spells out what a QMS must include: customer focus, leadership commitment, planning, support, operation, performance evaluation, improvement. This is the standard that matters for certification.
  3. ISO 9004 — Quality Management Systems: Guidelines for Performance Improvements. Goes beyond the bare requirements of 9001 to provide guidance on improving QMS performance over time.

Why ISO 9001 Matters

ISO 9001 is a generic standard — applicable to both manufacturing and service industries. Organisations may omit requirements that do not apply (e.g., a service company may omit production-control requirements). Only ISO 9001 can be certified; the others provide context and guidance but are not certifiable.

An ISO 9001 certified company has demonstrated to an independent auditor that it has:

  • A documented quality management system.
  • A customer focus, with mechanisms to capture and act on customer feedback.
  • Top-management commitment to quality.
  • Defined processes with clear inputs, outputs, and responsibilities.
  • A culture of continuous improvement, with regular internal audits.
  • Corrective and preventive action procedures.

Certification is renewable (typically every three years) and is performed by accredited third-party bodies such as TÜV, BSI, Bureau Veritas, DNV.

5.4 Statistical Techniques in Quality Control

Definition
Statistics
The science that deals with the collection, classification, analysis, and making of inferences from data or information.

The Two Big Branches

Descriptive Statistics

Describes the characteristics of a product or process using information already collected. Summarises data without going beyond it.

Tools: mean, median, mode, standard deviation, range, histograms, box plots.

Example: "Last month's average defect rate was 2.3 %."

Inferential Statistics

Draws conclusions about unknown product or process parameters based on information contained in a sample. Goes beyond the data to make claims about the population.

Tools: hypothesis tests, confidence intervals, regression, ANOVA.

Example: "We are 95 % confident the true defect rate lies between 2.0 % and 2.6 %."

Four Key Statistical Techniques in QC

(1) Hypothesis Testing

Two competing hypotheses are evaluated:

  • Null hypothesis (H₀) — the default assumption (e.g., "the process mean has not shifted").
  • Alternative hypothesis (H₁) — the claim we'd believe if H₀ is wrong (e.g., "the process mean has shifted").

Based on sample data and a significance level α (commonly 0.05), the test reaches one of two conclusions:

  • Reject H₀ in favour of H₁.
  • Fail to reject H₀ (NOT "accept H₀" — absence of evidence is not evidence of absence).

Two errors are possible:

  • Type I error (α) — rejecting H₀ when it's true ("false alarm").
  • Type II error (β) — failing to reject H₀ when H₁ is true ("missed signal").
(2) Regression Analysis

Determines a mathematical expression describing the functional relationship between one response variable and one or more independent variables. For example: does part diameter depend linearly on cutting speed, feed, and depth-of-cut?

Simple linear regression: y = β₀ + β₁ x + ε. Multiple regression: y = β₀ + β₁ x₁ + β₂ x₂ + … + ε.

Regression supports both understanding (which factors matter most?) and prediction (what diameter will we get if we set cutting speed to 200 m/min?).

(3) Statistical Process Control (SPC)

Monitors, controls, and improves processes through statistical techniques. SPC identifies when processes are out of control due to special-cause variation (variation caused by specific identifiable circumstances, not inherent to the process). Once identified, practitioners can seek to remove that variation.

SPC is, in essence, an automated detector for assignable causes — a kind of "is something unusual happening right now?" alarm system.

(4) Design and Analysis of Experiments (DOE)

Planning, conducting, analyzing, and interpreting controlled tests to evaluate the factors that may influence a response variable.

Where regression asks "what relationship exists in the data we have?", DOE asks "what relationship would we see if we deliberately varied these factors?". A well-designed experiment with factor levels and randomisation can identify causal effects (not just correlations) economically — a 25 factorial design tests 32 combinations of 5 factors, far less than a one-at-a-time approach.

5.5 Statistical Process Control — Foundations

Definition
Statistical Process Control (SPC)
The use of statistical techniques to monitor a production process to detect and prevent poor quality. SPC tools — primarily control charts — allow real-time judgement of whether a process is operating consistently or has been disturbed by an assignable cause.

Two Key Terms

  • Sample — a subset of the items produced, selected for inspection. Sample size and frequency are chosen to balance information against cost.
  • Control charts — graphical tools showing whether a process is within statistical control limits.

Two Kinds of Variation (Walter Shewhart's Great Insight)

Walter Shewhart of Bell Labs, in the 1920s, made the foundational distinction that underpins all of SPC:

Random (Common-Cause / Chance) Variation Non-Random (Special-Cause / Assignable) Variation
Inherent in the process Identifiable and correctable
Natural occurrences — many tiny influences Examples: equipment out of adjustment, defective materials, change in part or material, broken machinery, operator fatigue, poor work methods, lack of training
Depends on equipment, machinery, engineering, operator, system of measurement Each cause individually identifiable
Removing it requires fundamental process redesign Removing it requires targeted action
Process showing only common-cause variation is "in control" Process with assignable causes present is "out of control"
Why the Distinction Matters Strategically

Common-cause and special-cause variation require different management responses:

  • Reacting to common-cause variation as if it were special-cause is what Deming called "tampering" — every small random deviation triggers an adjustment, which itself adds variability. The process gets worse, not better.
  • Treating special-cause variation as if it were common-cause means letting genuine problems hide in the noise. Defects continue.

The only sustainable approach is to first use control charts to verify the process is in statistical control (no special causes), then work on reducing common-cause variation through process redesign. This is the heart of Deming's philosophy.

SPC Contributes to TQM's Goal of Continuous Improvement

SPC is not standalone — it is a tool inside the broader TQM framework. By distinguishing common from special causes, SPC tells management when intervention is needed and when it would be counterproductive.

Attribute vs Variable Data

  • Attribute — a product characteristic evaluated with a discrete response. Pass/fail, good/bad, conforming/non-conforming. Counted, not measured. Examples: number of defective bottles in a batch, number of scratches on a painted surface.
  • Variable — a product characteristic that is continuous and measurable on a numerical scale. Examples: weight, length, diameter, temperature, viscosity.

Variables provide more information than attributes — a measurement of "24.987 mm" tells you everything; "pass" tells you only that it was inside some range. As a result, variable control charts can detect smaller process shifts than attribute charts using the same sample size.

SPC Applied to Services

SPC originated in manufacturing but applies broadly to service industries. A "service defect" is a failure to meet customer requirements. Service organisations monitor:

  • Time — wait times, service durations, response times.
  • Customer satisfaction — survey scores, complaint rates, retention.

Example service-quality metrics (from the slides):

  • Hospitals — timeliness/quickness of care, staff responses to requests, accuracy of lab tests, cleanliness, courtesy, accuracy of paperwork, speed of admittance and checkouts.
  • Grocery stores — wait time to check out, frequency of out-of-stock items, quality of food items, cleanliness, customer complaints, checkout register errors.
  • Airlines — flight delays, lost luggage and luggage handling, wait time at ticket counters and check-in, agent and flight attendant courtesy, accurate flight information, passenger cabin cleanliness and maintenance.
  • Fast-food restaurants — wait time for service, customer complaints, cleanliness, food quality, order accuracy, employee courtesy.
  • Catalogue-order companies — order accuracy, operator knowledge and courtesy, packaging, delivery time, phone-order wait time.
  • Insurance companies — billing accuracy, timeliness of claims processing, agent availability and response time.

Where to Use Control Charts in a Process

  • Where the process has a tendency to go out of control — historical evidence of variability.
  • Where the process is particularly harmful and costly if it goes out of control.
  • At the beginning of a process — wasteful to start production with bad supplies.
  • Before a costly or irreversible point — after which the product is hard to rework (heat treatment, painting, plating).
  • Before and after assembly or painting operations that might cover up defects.
  • Before the outgoing final product or service is delivered.

5.6 Anatomy of a Control Chart

A control chart is a graphical tool for monitoring the activity of an ongoing process. It plots sample statistics (mean, range, proportion defective, etc.) on the y-axis against sample number (or time) on the x-axis, with three reference lines:

  • Centreline — the long-term process average.
  • Upper Control Limit (UCL) — typically at +3σ above the centreline.
  • Lower Control Limit (LCL) — typically at −3σ below the centreline.

The choice of ±3σ is not arbitrary: assuming a normal distribution, only 0.27 % of samples should fall outside these limits by chance alone. A point outside ±3σ is therefore strong evidence of an assignable cause — strong enough to act on, but rare enough not to trigger excessive false alarms.

When is a Process "In Control"?

A process is judged in control when all four of these conditions are met:

  1. No sample points fall outside the control limits.
  2. Most points are near the process average (the centreline).
  3. About equal numbers of points above and below the centreline.
  4. Points appear randomly distributed — no patterns, no long runs on one side, no trends.

Violating any of these — even without a point outside the limits — is grounds for investigation. Modern control-chart practice uses formal "rules" (Western Electric rules, Nelson rules) to flag such patterns: e.g., "9 points in a row on the same side of centreline", "6 points in a row trending upward", "2 of 3 points outside 2σ", and so on.

Two Families of Control Charts

  • Attribute charts — for discrete pass/fail or count data.
    • p-chart — proportion of defective items in each sample.
    • c-chart — count of defects per unit.
  • Variable charts — for continuous measurement data.
    • X̄-chart — sample mean.
    • R-chart — sample range.

5.7 Attribute Control Charts

p-Chart — Proportion of Nonconforming Items

Use a p-chart when each item is classified as conforming or non-conforming and you record the proportion defective in each sample. The underlying distribution is binomial, approximated as normal when n is large and p is not too small.

p-chart limits UCL = p̄ + z · σp
LCL = p̄ − z · σp

σp = √[ p̄(1 − p̄) / n ]
znumber of standard deviations (typically 3 → 99.74 % coverage) p̄overall sample proportion defective; estimate of the long-run process average σpstandard deviation of the sample proportion (binomial standard error) nsample size (number of items inspected per sample)

Important practical detail: the LCL cannot go negative — a proportion of defectives below 0 is impossible. When the formula gives a negative LCL, set LCL = 0.

Problem 5.1 (p-chart)  25 samples of 50 containers each are inspected over 4 days. Total nonconforming = 90. Construct a p-chart.
Step 1 · Compute p̄ Total inspected = 25 × 50 = 1250.
p̄ = 90 / 1250 = 0.072
Step 2 · Compute σp σp = √(0.072 × 0.928 / 50) = √(0.0668 / 50) = √0.001336 = 0.0366
Step 3 · 3σ Control Limits UCL = 0.072 + 3 × 0.0366 = 0.072 + 0.1098 = 0.1818
LCL = 0.072 − 0.1098 = −0.0378 → clip to 0 (proportion can't be negative)
p̄ = 0.072 · UCL = 0.1818 · LCL = 0

c-Chart — Count of Nonconformities per Unit

Use a c-chart when each inspection unit can have multiple defects (a roll of fabric with multiple flaws; a casting with multiple inclusions; a circuit board with multiple solder defects). The underlying distribution is Poisson, where the mean equals the variance — so the standard deviation of the count is √(mean).

c-chart limits UCL = c̄ + z · σc
LCL = c̄ − z · σc

σc = √c̄
c̄mean number of nonconformities per unit (across all sampled units) σcstandard deviation = √c̄ (Poisson property)

As with p-chart, clip LCL to 0 if the formula gives a negative value.

5.8 Variable Control Charts

For continuous measurement data, we use two charts in parallel: one to monitor the process mean (the X̄-chart) and another to monitor the process dispersion (the R-chart). Both must be in control for the process to be considered in control.

Why Two Charts

A process can go out of control in two distinct ways:

  • The mean shifts — the process is now producing parts centred on a different size than before. Detected by the X̄-chart.
  • The variability changes — the spread of measurements has widened (or narrowed). Detected by the R-chart.

Either change is bad news — even a reduction in variability should be investigated (it might be due to a sensor saturating, or to deliberate cherry-picking of samples). The two charts together give a complete picture.

Calculating the Statistics

Sample and grand statistics Sample mean:  X̄ = (Σ Xi) / n
Sample range:  R = Xmax − Xmin

Grand mean (over g samples):  X̿ = (Σ X̄i) / g
Mean range:  R̄ = (Σ Ri) / g

Control Limits

The "first-principles" approach is to compute limits as X̿ ± 3 σX̄, where σX̄ = σ / √n is the standard error of the sample mean. But σ is usually unknown; we estimate it from the range using σ = R̄/d2, where d2 is a tabled constant depending on sample size.

This calculation is conveniently bundled into the constant A₂ = 3 / (d2√n), leading to the practical formula:

X̄-chart (using R̄ to estimate σ) UCLX̄ = X̿ + A₂ R̄
LCLX̄ = X̿ − A₂ R̄

For the R-chart, control limits depend on the distribution of the range, which is non-symmetric. We use two more tabled constants D₃ and D₄:

R-chart UCLR = D₄ R̄
LCLR = D₃ R̄

For small sample sizes (n ≤ 6), D₃ = 0 — the R-chart has no lower limit. This is a known limitation: for small samples we cannot detect a decrease in variability with the R-chart alone.

How the Two Charts Together Detect Different Problems

Mean shift only

Suppose a tool wears, causing all parts produced to be progressively larger. The mean of each sample drifts upward. The range within each sample may stay normal (each shift's parts are similar to each other). The X̄-chart shows the problem; the R-chart does not.

Variability change only

Suppose a clamping mechanism develops slack, allowing the workpiece to chatter randomly. Each sample now contains parts with a wider spread, but the mean may still be on target. The R-chart shows the problem; the X̄-chart does not.

Both

A bad batch of raw material affects both the typical part size and the spread of part sizes. Both charts flag it.

This is why both charts are always used together. Reading one without the other is incomplete diagnostics.

5.9 Process Capability Analysis

SPC (control charts) tells you whether your process is stable — whether it produces the same kind of output it always has. But stability is not the same as "good enough". A process can be in statistical control while consistently producing parts outside the design specifications. That's where capability analysis comes in.

Concept

Definition
Process Capability
The variation in the product quality characteristic that remains after removal of all special causes. It is a measure of the inherent ability of an in-control process to meet the customer's specifications.

Two pre-requisites for capability analysis:

  1. The process must first be in statistical control (no assignable causes operating). Otherwise the calculation is meaningless.
  2. The output must be approximately normally distributed.

The 6σ Spread

For a normal distribution, 99.74 % of the values lie within ±3σ of the mean — i.e., within a total spread of 6σ. This is called the process spread: a virtually complete range of what the process can produce.

The customer's specification width is T = USL − LSL (Upper Specification Limit minus Lower Specification Limit). If 6σ < T, the process spread fits inside the specification: most parts will be acceptable. If 6σ > T, parts will routinely fall outside specification.

The Capability Index Cp

Process Capability Index Cp = (USL − LSL) / 6σ = T / 6σ

Or equivalently, using the symmetric half-tolerance:
Cp = 2Thalf / 6σ = Thalf / 3σ

Interpretation:

  • Cp < 1 — process spread is wider than spec width; defects are inevitable.
  • Cp = 1 — process spread exactly equals spec width; about 2,700 defects per million if the process is perfectly centred. Marginal capability.
  • Cp = 1.33 — spec width is 33 % wider than the process spread; provides a safety margin against process drift.
  • Cp ≥ 2 — six-sigma quality; defects per million in single digits.

Industry Benchmarks

  • Cp = 1.00 — typical USA acceptance level.
  • Cp = 1.33 — typical Japanese acceptance level.
  • Cp = 1.66 — Japanese watch-making industry, with target to extend to 2.0.

The slide presents a powerful cost curve: as Cp rises, the appropriate inspection strategy shifts:

  • Cp ~ 1 or below — total inspection required, with check-and-repair stations.
  • Cp ~ 1 – 1.33 — lot inspection with sampling.
  • Cp ~ 1.33 – 2 — sampling inspection at much lower rate.
  • Cp ~ 2 — statistical process control on the process itself, with little or no product inspection.
  • Cp > 5 — "no defect" zone; the process is so good that defects are vanishingly rare; only periodic audits needed.

Cp vs Cpk

Cp measures process spread versus specification width — it assumes the process is centred on the target. But what if the process mean has drifted off-centre?

The more useful index is Cpk, which accounts for centring:

Cpk = min[ (USL − µ)/(3σ), (µ − LSL)/(3σ) ]

Cpk equals Cp when the process is perfectly centred; Cpk < Cp when the process is off-centre. A high Cp with a low Cpk says "the process is precise enough, but mis-centred — adjust the setpoint, don't redesign the process".

The slides only mention Cp; in practice, both are reported.

5.10 Measurement System Analysis (MSA)

If the decisions you make depend on measurements, then errors in the measurements lead directly to errors in the decisions. A capability study, a control chart, an acceptance-sampling plan — all rest on the assumption that the measurements feeding them are accurate. MSA is the discipline of qualifying a measurement system for its intended use by quantifying its accuracy, precision, and stability.

Definition
Measurement System Analysis (MSA)
The systematic evaluation of a measurement system — instrument, operator, procedure, environment — to determine how much of the observed variation in measurements is due to the system rather than to actual differences in the parts being measured.

Accuracy vs Precision Revisited

MSA returns us to the accuracy/precision distinction, this time with a focus on the measurement system itself:

  • Accurate & Precise — tight cluster on the true value. Ideal.
  • Accurate, not precise — wide spread, mean centred. The system is unbiased but noisy.
  • Not accurate, precise — tight cluster, offset from the true value. The system is consistent but biased — recalibration can fix this.
  • Not accurate, not precise — scattered everywhere. The system needs replacement or major service.

Efforts to improve a measurement system aim at improving both accuracy and precision.

Requirements of a Good Measurement System

  • Statistical stability over time. The measurement system itself must be in statistical control — repeated measurements of the same artefact over time should produce a stable distribution, no drift, no special-cause shifts.
  • Variability small compared to the process variability. The "noise" of measurement should be small relative to the actual variation in parts. A widely used rule: measurement system variation should be less than ~30 % of total observed variation (the "Gauge R&R %" metric).
  • Variability small compared to the specification limits. The measurement system must be able to discriminate between in-spec and out-of-spec parts. If measurement uncertainty is half the tolerance band, the measurement is nearly useless for accept/reject decisions.
  • Resolution adequate. The resolution of the measuring device must be small relative to the smaller of (specification tolerance, process spread). A common rule: resolution ≤ 1/10 of tolerance.

Gauge R&R (Repeatability and Reproducibility)

Although not in the slides explicitly, this is the core of practical MSA. Two sources of measurement-system variation are distinguished:

  • Repeatability — variation when the same operator measures the same part with the same instrument multiple times. Reflects instrument noise.
  • Reproducibility — variation when different operators measure the same part with the same instrument. Reflects training, technique, and operator-to-operator differences.

A standard Gauge R&R study uses 10 parts, 3 operators, 2 or 3 trials each. ANOVA decomposes the total variation into repeatability, reproducibility, and part-to-part variation. The Gauge R&R as a percentage of total variation is the standard acceptance metric: under 10 % is excellent, under 30 % is acceptable, over 30 % is unacceptable.

5.11 Acceptance Sampling Plan

When a large lot of parts arrives — incoming materials from a supplier, or finished product going to a customer — should you inspect every part, or just a sample? Acceptance sampling is the statistical method for making this decision economically and rigorously.

Definition
Acceptance Sampling Plan
A statistical procedure for deciding whether to accept or reject a lot based on inspection of a sample drawn from the lot. The plan specifies the sample size n and the acceptance number c: accept the lot if the sample contains ≤ c defects; otherwise reject.

Attribute vs Variable Plans

Sampling plans come in two varieties (parallel to the data types we saw with control charts):

  • Attribute plan — each item is classified as conforming or non-conforming. Lot accepted if the number of defectives in the sample is at or below the acceptance number. The terms "defect" / "defective" are used interchangeably with "nonconformity" / "nonconforming item" in many plans.
  • Variable plan — the quality characteristic is measured as a numerical value. The plan computes the sample mean and compares it (with allowance for sample standard deviation) against the specification limits.

Variable plans require smaller sample sizes for the same protection but assume the data is normally distributed.

Where Acceptance Sampling is Used

Acceptance sampling can be performed:

  • During inspection of incoming raw materials, components, and assemblies — verifying suppliers' shipments.
  • In various phases of in-process operations — gating production from one stage to the next.
  • During final product or service inspection — before shipping to customers.

It can be used as a form of product inspection:

  • Between companies and their vendors.
  • Between manufacturers and their customers.
  • Between departments or divisions within the same company.

What Acceptance Sampling Cannot Do

Acceptance sampling has fundamental limitations that must be understood:

  • It does not control or improve the quality level of the process. It only decides accept/reject for existing lots.
  • Because of the very nature of sampling, the procedure will accept some lots and reject others of the same true quality. There is always sampling risk.
  • The only way to actually improve quality is through process control and improvement. Sampling is a downstream filter, not an upstream lever.
Two Sampling Risks

Any sampling plan has two error probabilities, completely analogous to Type I and Type II errors in hypothesis testing:

  • Producer's risk (α) — the probability of rejecting a good lot. From the producer's perspective, a false alarm is costly: a good lot you shipped is sent back.
  • Consumer's risk (β) — the probability of accepting a bad lot. From the consumer's perspective, this is catastrophic: bad parts reach production.

The Operating Characteristic (OC) Curve plots the probability of accepting a lot as a function of the lot's actual fraction defective. A well-designed plan has high probability of accepting lots at the AQL (Acceptable Quality Level) — keeping producer's risk small — and low probability of accepting lots at the LTPD (Lot Tolerance Percent Defective) — keeping consumer's risk small.

Why Use Acceptance Sampling at All?

Given its limitations, why is acceptance sampling still widely used? Three powerful reasons:

  • If inspection is destructive, 100 % inspection is impossible. You cannot tensile-test every steel rod and still have rods to sell. Sampling is the only option.
  • Sampling is economical and causes less handling damage. 100 % inspection of every item is expensive in time and money — and the handling itself can introduce defects.
  • Sampling provides a strong motivation to improve quality. The entire lot may be rejected based on the sample — a powerful signal that drives the producer to improve. A single defective part in a 100 % inspection scheme could just be removed and quietly forgotten.

5.12 Reliability Assessment of Systems

Definition
Reliability
The ability of a product to function effectively over a certain period of time under certain conditions. It is closely related to quality of performance — and the higher the reliability, the greater the customer's satisfaction over the product's life.

Quality, in the simple sense, is about whether a product works when you first use it. Reliability is about whether it continues to work, over time, under conditions of normal (or even adverse) use. A car that drives off the lot smoothly has "first-use quality"; a car that runs reliably for 200,000 km has reliability.

The Bathtub Curve

Most products exhibit a characteristic failure-rate-vs-time pattern called the bathtub curve, consisting of three regions:

  • Infant mortality (decreasing failure rate) — early failures due to manufacturing defects, weak components, assembly errors. Mitigated by burn-in / run-in periods before shipping.
  • Useful life (constant failure rate) — random failures from unforeseeable causes. The lowest, flat portion of the curve. Reliability metrics like MTBF (Mean Time Between Failures) apply here.
  • Wear-out (increasing failure rate) — components fatigue, corrode, age out. Mitigated by preventive maintenance and replacement schedules.

Reliability engineering aims to flatten and lower the entire curve, particularly extending the useful-life region.

Reliability and Life Tests

How do you measure reliability? You test items until they fail and observe how long they last. Such tests are usually destructive:

  • Items are run until they fail — they cannot then be sold.
  • Test plans observe a sample either until a preset number of failures occur, or for a preset duration, or a combination.
  • Testing is usually done at the prototype stage — expensive if the unit cost is high (think: aircraft engines, nuclear reactor components).

The Test-Time vs Cost Trade-off

A longer accumulated test time produces a more precise estimate of product reliability or mean life. But test time costs money — the units under test cannot be sold, the test equipment is occupied, engineers monitor the test. The trade-off between precision and cost is the central design decision in a reliability test plan.

The testing is conducted under simulated conditions, but these should mimic the actual operating conditions as closely as possible. Accelerated life tests apply elevated stresses (higher temperature, higher voltage, more vibration) to accelerate failure mechanisms and shorten test duration — but the extrapolation from accelerated to actual conditions itself introduces uncertainty.

Three Types of Reliability Test

(1) Failure-Terminated Test

The test terminates when a preassigned number of failures occurs in the sample.

  • You decide in advance: "test until 10 units fail".
  • Test duration is unknown ahead of time — depends on how reliable the units turn out to be.
  • Good for estimating the failure rate when you don't know roughly what it is.
(2) Time-Terminated Test

The test terminates when a preassigned time is reached.

  • You decide in advance: "test for 1000 hours".
  • The number of failures observed in that time is variable — could be 0, could be many.
  • Good for budgeting purposes — you know exactly how long the test will run.
(3) Sequential Reliability Test

No prior decision is made about the number of failures or the test duration. Instead, the accumulated results of the test are continually evaluated to decide:

  • Accept the lot (or design) — reliability is good.
  • Reject the lot — reliability is bad.
  • Continue testing — not enough information yet.

This is the most efficient on average — good lots and bad lots can be identified with smaller sample sizes than fixed-time or fixed-failure plans. The mathematics is more complex (Wald's sequential probability ratio test).

Twelve Conceptual Drill Points (Expect These as MCQ or Short Q)

  1. GO gauge checks Maximum Metal Limit: LLH for a hole, HLS for a shaft. Designed as a "replica" of the mating part — checks several related dimensions at once.
  2. NOT-GO gauge checks Least Metal Limit: HLH for a hole, LLS for a shaft. Checks one dimension at a time.
  3. Wear allowance is applied only to the GO gauge, pointing into the work-tolerance zone (away from the LML side). Both gauge tolerance and wear allowance are typically 10 % of the work tolerance.
  4. Hole basis (capital H) has fundamental deviation = 0, so LLH = basic size. Shaft basis (small h) has fundamental upper deviation = 0, so HLS = basic size.
  5. Standard reference conditions are 20 °C and 760 mmHg. A 1 °C rise expands C25 steel by 0.3 µm per 25 mm.
  6. Sine bar limit: angles greater than 45° are not recommended, because dh-to-dθ amplification grows large as cos θ → 0.
  7. Wringability requirements: surface finish ≤ 0.025 µm and flatness ≤ 0.13 µm.
  8. Six-Sigma yield: 99.99966 % yield, 3.4 defects per million (DPM).
  9. Cp benchmarks: 1.00 (USA), 1.33 (Japan), 1.66 (Japanese watchmaking), 2.0 (target).
  10. p-chart uses proportion defective (binomial); c-chart uses count of defects per unit (Poisson). LCL clipped to 0 if formula gives negative.
  11. Random error → affects precision (width of distribution). Systematic error → affects accuracy (offset of mean from true value).
  12. Hysteresis = difference of instrument indication when measured ascending vs descending.

▷ Q1 — Tolerance Stack Three blocks A (40+0.03−0.01), B (25+0.02−0.01), C (15+0.01−0.00) are stacked in chain. Find the assembly dimension.
Show solution Nominal L = 40 + 25 + 15 = 80 mm.
Cumulative upper tolerance = +0.03 + 0.02 + 0.01 = +0.06.
Cumulative lower tolerance = −0.01 − 0.01 + 0.00 = −0.02.
Result: 80+0.06−0.02 mm.
▷ Q2 — Allowance & Fit Type For Hole = 25+0.021+0.000 mm, Shaft = 25−0.020−0.041 mm, find allowance and identify the type of fit.
Show solution HLH = 25.021, LLH = 25.000, HLS = 24.980, LLS = 24.959.
Allowance = LLH − HLS = 25.000 − 24.980 = +0.020 mm (positive → clearance).
Cmin = 0.020, Cmax = HLH − LLS = 0.062 → both positive → Clearance fit.
▷ Q3 — Surface Roughness A surface profile has peak/valley heights 4, 1, 5, 0, 6, 1, 5, 2, 4, 2 (in µm). Find Ra, RRMS and Rz.
Show solution Σ heights = 4+1+5+0+6+1+5+2+4+2 = 30 → Ra = 3.0 µm.
Σh² = 16+1+25+0+36+1+25+4+16+4 = 128 → RRMS = √(128/10) = √12.8 ≈ 3.58 µm.
Peaks (odd) = 4+5+6+5+4 = 24; Valleys (even) = 1+0+1+2+2 = 6.
Rz = (24 − 6) / 5 = 3.6 µm.
▷ Q4 — Sine Bar A 200 mm sine bar is used. Slip-gauge stack under one roller = 50 mm. Find the measured angle.
Show solution sin θ = h / L = 50 / 200 = 0.25 → θ = sin⁻¹(0.25) ≈ 14.48°.
▷ Q5 — p-Chart 40 samples of n = 100 are inspected; total defectives = 200. Compute the 3σ UCL and LCL of the p-chart.
Show solution p̄ = 200 / (40 × 100) = 0.05.
σp = √(0.05 × 0.95 / 100) = √0.000475 = 0.02179.
UCL = 0.05 + 3(0.02179) = 0.1154.
LCL = 0.05 − 0.0654 = negative → 0.
▷ Q6 — X̄ / R Chart Limits For n = 4, X̿ = 100 mm and R̄ = 5 mm. Compute the 3σ X̄- and R-chart limits.
Show solution n = 4 → A₂ = 0.729, D₃ = 0, D₄ = 2.282.
X̄-chart: UCL = 100 + 0.729 × 5 = 103.65; LCL = 100 − 3.645 = 96.36.
R-chart: UCL = 2.282 × 5 = 11.41; LCL = 0.
▷ Q7 — Process Capability Specification width T = 0.060 mm and process σ = 0.008 mm. Compute Cp and judge against USA/Japan benchmarks.
Show solution Cp = T / 6σ = 0.060 / (6 × 0.008) = 0.060 / 0.048 = 1.25.
Above USA baseline (Cp = 1.00), below Japanese baseline (1.33). Capable but not at Japanese standard — opportunity to improve.
▷ Q8 — Gauge Design For a hole of dimension 25+0.020+0.000 mm, design the GO and NOT-GO plug gauges using 10 % rule for gauge tolerance and 10 % of that for wear allowance.
Show solution Work tolerance = 0.020 mm. Gauge tolerance = 0.002 mm. Wear allowance = 0.0002 mm.
GO Plug (checks LLH = 25.000):
 Lower limit = 25.000 + 0.0002 = 25.0002; Upper = 25.0002 + 0.002 = 25.0022.
NOT-GO Plug (checks HLH = 25.020):
 Upper limit = 25.020; Lower = 25.020 − 0.002 = 25.018.
▷ Q9 — c-Chart A factory's inspection log shows defect counts in 20 finished assemblies: 3, 5, 4, 6, 2, 8, 4, 5, 6, 3, 4, 7, 5, 4, 6, 3, 5, 4, 6, 4. Compute c̄ and the 3σ control limits.

Survival Strategy for the Exam Hall

  • Numerical first. Gauges, fits, tolerances, control charts give the most marks per minute. Hit them while you're fresh.
  • For "40 H8/d9"-style problems: the recipe is bullet-proof — D = √(Dmax · Dmin) → compute i → multiply by IT grade → look up the deviation → draw the schematic to scale. Always confirm the fit type by computing LLH − HLS and HLH − LLS.
  • For gauge design: remember the 10 % rule twice (gauge tolerance is 10 % of work tolerance; wear allowance is 10 % of gauge tolerance). Wear allowance applies only to GO. Direction: toward the work-tolerance zone, away from the LML.
  • For control charts: identify the data type first (attribute vs variable). For p/c charts, always check whether the LCL clips to 0. For X̄/R charts, use the constants table from the n-row that matches the sample size.
