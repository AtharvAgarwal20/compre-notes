Part I · Foundations

Quality, Management & Their Marriage

Before we touch any chart, we need crisp definitions. The exam will almost certainly ask "define quality / management / quality management" — and the answers must be precise.

01What is Quality?

Quality is one of those words everybody uses and nobody defines. In the lecture, the slides walked through pictures of a futuristic Mercedes, fresh fruits, a washing machine, several smartphones, bird-spikes, and a Nike sneaker — and asked you to decide which one had "quality." The trick is that the answer depends on who is asking. A bird-spike has high quality if it keeps birds off your balcony. A Nike shoe has high quality if it lasts a marathon. The Mercedes has high quality if it makes you feel something when you sit in it.

This leads us to the lecture's exact, examinable definition:

Definition · Quality

Quality is the ability to meet a customer's expectations.

Three things hide inside this one sentence, and they all matter for the exam:

  1. Customer-centric. Quality is not an absolute property of the product. It is a relationship between the product and the person who buys, uses or experiences it. A pen that writes 1000 pages is over-engineered for a kid drawing on a napkin and under-engineered for a notary signing 5000 deeds. Same pen, different quality.
  2. "Ability" — quality is a capability, not a one-time event. A car that survives one journey is not high-quality; a car that survives 200,000 km of journeys is.
  3. "Expectations" — and expectations change over time. A Nokia 3310 was the gold standard of quality in 2001; today, the very same device would fail every customer expectation we have for a smartphone. This is why quality is never a fixed target — and why continuous improvement (which we'll see again under TQM and Six Sigma) is the only sustainable strategy.
If asked "define quality," do not just say "good product." Use the lecture's wording — ability to meet customer expectations — and explain that it is relative, capability-based, and time-dependent.

02What is Management?

The slides set this up the same way — through pictures of a classroom, a kitchen staff, and a meeting room — to make the point that management is not a desk job, it is the act of orchestrating people. The lecture's definition:

Definition · Management

The act of getting people together to accomplish desired goals and objectives using available resources efficiently and effectively.

Four ideas inside it:

  • Getting people together — management is fundamentally social; it is coordination, not control.
  • Desired goals and objectives — there must be a target. Activity without a target is not management.
  • Available resources — you work with what you have, not what you wish you had.
  • Efficiently and effectively — efficiency = doing the thing right (minimum waste); effectiveness = doing the right thing (correct goal). You need both.

03What is Quality Management?

Now glue the two definitions together. If quality is the ability to meet expectations, and management is the orchestration of people and resources toward a goal, then:

Definition · Quality Management

A process that ensures the quality of the product throughout its life cycle.

The lecture stresses the phrase "throughout its life cycle." This is not optional padding. It means quality is not just inspected at the end of the assembly line — it is built in during design, defended during manufacturing, guaranteed during shipping, and serviced after sale. Every stage. Every time. This is what separates a quality management mindset from a mere quality inspection mindset.

04The Four Pillars of Quality Management (P-C-A-I)

Quality management stands on four interlocking activities. Memorise the sequence — it is exam gold:

Pillar What it does
Planning Identify requirements, set criteria, recognise the procedures needed to deliver quality. This is the first step — done before a single unit is made.
Control Review the quality of the product or service during/after production. Inspection and testing identify problems and defects needing correction.
Assurance Guarantee that defects are prevented in the first place, so consistent results are delivered. Control = detection; Assurance = prevention.
Improvement There is always room to do better. Measure results, find improvements, raise the bar. Feeds back into Planning — closing the loop.
A classic exam confusion: Quality Control vs Quality Assurance. Control = Detection (find defects after they happen). Assurance = Prevention (stop defects from happening). Both are needed; they are not the same.

The PCAI flow as a story

Imagine you are launching a new biscuit. Planning says: each biscuit shall be 8 cm wide, 5 mm thick, contain 4 g sugar, and never crumble in the box. Control sets up cameras and weight sensors at the end of the line to catch biscuits that miss those specs. Assurance redesigns the oven, the dough recipe, and the conveyor speed so that the cameras have almost nothing to catch. Improvement reviews the past month, notices that humid afternoons produce 3 × more crumbling, and adjusts the recipe for monsoon season. Now Planning has a better baseline. Cycle restarts. This is why we drew it as a cycle, not a list.

Part II · The Toyota Way

How Japan Quietly Re-Invented Manufacturing

JIT, Kanban, Kaizen, 5S — these are not buzzwords; they are the operating system that took Toyota from a small loom-maker in the 1930s to the world's largest automaker. The exam can ask any of these by name.

05Manufacturing the Toyota Way

The lecture grouped JIT, Kanban and Kaizen under one banner — "Toyota Way" — and then showed the McDonald's golden arches and the Apple logo alongside the Toyota emblem. Why? Because these three companies, operating in totally different industries, have all internalised the same Toyota-born ideas: make only what is needed, when it is needed, in the amount needed, and improve relentlessly.

06Just-in-Time (JIT)

Concept · JIT

Produce only what is needed, only when it is needed, and only in the quantity needed. Inventory is not an asset; it is a symptom of bad planning.

Pre-JIT thinking said: keep huge warehouses of parts "just in case." Post-JIT thinking says: huge warehouses hide problems. If you have 3 weeks of fender inventory, you will not notice the press machine is producing a defective fender every 100th shot until tens of thousands have piled up. JIT shrinks the buffer so problems become visible immediately. The cost is fragility — a single supplier delay can stop the line — so JIT only works when paired with reliable suppliers, predictable processes, and a culture that rapidly fixes whatever breaks.

This is why the lecture put TQM's "Zero waste · Zero inventory · Just in Time" on a single slide — they reinforce each other.

07Kanban System (Sign-Based)

"Kanban" (看板) literally means "signboard" in Japanese. It is the mechanism that makes JIT actually work on a factory floor.

Imagine three workstations in a line: A (raw material in) → B (in production) → C (finished out). The naive way to coordinate them is for a manager to shout instructions. The Kanban way: each station has a board with cards. When C finishes one unit and ships it, the card moves backward — B sees the missing card and produces exactly one replacement. B's consumption pulls a card from A, who replenishes one unit. Demand at the end pulls supply through the system. No central command, no over-production.

The lecture showed a Kanban board with four columns: Backlog (F, E) → To Do (D) → Ongoing (B, C) → Done (A). This is exactly how Trello, Jira, Asana and every modern project-management tool inherited the same idea for software work.

08Kaizen & the 5S Framework

Kaizen (改善) = "change for the better." It is the philosophy of continuous, incremental, everyone-participates improvement. Not big-bang revolutions every five years — tiny improvements every single day, suggested by the people closest to the work (operators, not consultants).

Kaizen vs No-Kaizen — the contrast

Without Kaizen
  • No structure to the improvement process; few set procedures
  • Goals are not defined, or are vague/difficult to measure
  • Changes to processes are infrequent, with little reflection on effectiveness
  • No plan for improvement; improvement is haphazard, reactive
With Kaizen
  • Consistent, ongoing process of improvement takes place
  • Improvement process has clearly defined, measurable goals
  • Constant review of successes; the improvement process itself is evaluated
  • Consistency leads to new, higher goals — a virtuous cycle

The 5 S Framework — the Japanese house-keeping for the factory floor

5S is the most practical, on-the-ground tool inside Kaizen. The five Japanese words (and their English meanings):

S Japanese Meaning
1 Seiri Sort — remove unnecessary items from the workspace
2 Seiton Set in order — organise tools and materials for easy access
3 Seiso Shine — clean the workspace to spot issues early
4 Seiketsu Standardise — create routines to maintain organisation
5 Shitsuke Sustain — build habits to keep improvements going
Case Study from the slides — the 10-minute delivery dark store

A quick-commerce dark store promises 10-minute delivery, but: order picking has risen from 3 → 8 minutes, items are misplaced, workers walk unnecessary distances, wrong items are shipped, and employee fatigue is high.

5S diagnosis:

  • Seiri — too many SKUs scattered; remove slow-movers from the picking zone.
  • Seiton — re-arrange shelves so the top 20% bestsellers (Pareto!) are within arm's reach of the packer.
  • Seiso — clean shelves daily; spilt product = mis-pick.
  • Seiketsu — daily 5-minute reset SOP at shift change.
  • Shitsuke — managers audit shelves weekly; reward compliant teams.

Result: picking time falls, mis-ships fall, fatigue falls — without buying any new technology.

Kaizen — East vs West

The classic distinction: Eastern Kaizen emphasises small, continuous, bottom-up improvements (operators suggest changes daily). Western "improvement" historically focused on top-down innovation — big projects led by managers and consultants. Modern best practice combines both, but the exam may simply ask you to characterise Kaizen as small, continuous, everyone-included improvements, contrasted with Western large, episodic, manager-led changes.

Part III · TQM

Total Quality Management

If quality management is a department's job, Total Quality Management is the whole company's job — from the CEO to the janitor. This is the most likely "long answer" theory question on the paper.

09Total Quality Management

Definition · TQM

A comprehensive approach to improving quality, revolving around three main themes: the customer, the process, and the people.

The Eight Pillars of TQM (from the slide diagram)

The lecture drew TQM as a wheel with eight spokes radiating from the centre:

  1. Focus on the Customer — every decision traces back to customer needs.
  2. Employee Involvement — every employee, not just managers, owns quality.
  3. Process Centered — fix the process, not the person. Bad outcomes usually = bad process, not bad worker.
  4. Integrated System — every department's quality system is linked; nothing operates in a silo.
  5. Strategic & Systematic Approach — quality is part of the long-term company strategy, not a side project.
  6. Decision-making Based on Facts — measure first, opinion later. Data over hunches.
  7. Communication — open, honest, top-to-bottom and bottom-to-top.
  8. Continuous Improvement — never "done." (Same idea as Kaizen.)

The TQM mantra (also from the slides)

· First time and every time — get it right the first time, every time.

· Nothing less, nothing more — meet specifications, don't over- or under-deliver.

· Zero waste · Zero inventory · Just-in-Time — the JIT discipline.

10Productivity & TQM — Traditional vs Continuous

A century ago, factory managers believed there was a sacred trade-off: if you want higher quality, you must accept lower productivity, because more inspection = slower line. TQM blew this up. The discovery: better quality means fewer rejects, fewer reworks, fewer warranty claims — and therefore higher productivity, not lower.

Traditional Approach
  • Market-share focus
  • Individuals
  • Focus on "who" and "why"
  • Short-term focus
  • Status quo focus
  • Product focus
  • Innovation
  • Fire-fighting
Continuous (TQM) Approach
  • Customer focus
  • Cross-functional teams
  • Focus on "what" and "how"
  • Long-term focus
  • Continuous improvement
  • Process improvement focus
  • Incremental improvements
  • Problem solving
Case from the slides — Zomato/Swiggy clone in trouble

Symptoms: app rating dropped 4.5 → 3.2 in three months, frequent crashes at checkout, customer complaints "orders fail but money is deducted," developers brag "we ship features fast," customer support is overwhelmed.

Root cause analysis (TQM lens):

  • The team is in traditional fire-fighting mode — shipping features for market share, then patching breakage.
  • No cross-functional collaboration: devs ship, QA tests too late, support inherits the mess.
  • Decisions are made on speed, not on facts (crash data, refund rates).

Who is responsible? Not developers or testers individually — management, for not embedding quality into the process. TQM says fix the process.

TQM action plan:

  • Customer focus: instrument the checkout funnel; treat every failed transaction as a defect.
  • Cross-functional teams: pair devs with QA and support during sprint planning.
  • Facts: weekly review of crash logs, P0 bugs blocked before new feature releases.
  • Continuous improvement: weekly retrospectives, Kaizen-style.
  • Process-centered: no one is fired; the release process is redesigned.
Part IV · Performance & Six Sigma

From "Defect-Free" to 3.4 Parts per Million

How do you actually measure whether your quality programme is working? The answer is a beautiful application of the normal distribution.

11Performance Standards

A quality policy must have an outcome — and that outcome is a defect-free product that meets or exceeds customer needs. Continuous improvement is the only way to stay current because customer expectations keep rising (remember the Nokia → smartphone shift).

The subtle warning about numerical goals

Even though the ultimate goal is zero defects, the lecture explicitly warns: numerical standards for performance measurement should be avoided. Why?

  • Setting a fixed number that may or may not be achievable has an unintended negative emotional impact on workers.
  • Not meeting the standard — even while making real progress — is demoralising.
  • Numerical goals shift emphasis to the short term; long-term benefits get sacrificed for short-term numbers.

How should you measure performance, then?

  1. Trend, not numbers. Measure continuous improvement in the goal, and track the direction of the trend rather than absolute values.
  2. Benchmarking. Identify high-performance companies (or even high-performance departments inside your own company) and use their performance as your improvement target.

12Six Sigma Quality

Six Sigma is the most famous numerical quality target in the world. It is built on the normal distribution and the 68-95-99.7 rule:

  • About 68.26% of values lie within ±1σ of the mean
  • About 95.44% within ±2σ
  • About 99.73% within ±3σ
  • About 99.9937% within ±4σ
  • About 99.999943% within ±5σ
  • About 99.9999998% within ±6σ

If you can engineer your process so that the specification limits sit at ±6σ from the mean, the rate of defects becomes only 3.4 per million opportunities (DPMO). (The 3.4 figure assumes a 1.5σ long-term process shift — a standard Six-Sigma convention.)

The Sigma Level Table — memorise this

Sigma Level Defects per Million (DPMO) Yield
6 3.4 99.99966%
5 230 99.977%
4 6,210 99.38%
3 66,800 93.32%
2 308,000 69.15%
1 690,000 30.85%
If the exam asks "what is the defect rate at six-sigma?" — the magic number is 3.4 DPMO. If it asks for the yield — 99.99966%.

To put 3.4 DPMO in perspective: most companies operate at 3σ or 4σ, meaning thousands of defects per million. Six-Sigma quality is the difference between losing your luggage on every other flight (3σ) versus losing it once a decade.

13DMAIC — The Six-Sigma Improvement Cycle

How do you actually move a process toward Six-Sigma quality? Through the DMAIC cycle:

Letter Phase What you do
D Define State the problem, the customer requirement, the scope of the project.
M Measure Collect data on the current process. You cannot improve what you do not measure.
A Analyse Find the root causes of variation and defects. (This is where Fishbone, Pareto, scatter plots come in.)
I Improve Design, test, and implement solutions targeting the root causes.
C Control Lock in the improvement with control charts, SOPs, and ongoing monitoring so the gains stick.

Notice how DMAIC parallels the four pillars (Plan-Control-Assure-Improve) — same philosophy, more granular.

Part V · Standards & Statistics

ISO 9000 and the Statistical Toolkit

14International Standards — the ISO 9000 Family

Quality programmes are no longer optional in the global economy — without them, the modern supply chain would collapse, since quality programmes have been so effective in driving down costs and increasing competitiveness. To make quality programmes comparable across companies and countries, the International Organization for Standardization (ISO) created a family of standards.

The 2015 revision: three primary standards

Standard Title Purpose
ISO 9000 Quality Management Systems: Fundamentals & Vocabulary The dictionary — defines all the terms used in the family.
ISO 9001 Quality Management Systems: Requirements The rulebook — the only standard you can be certified against. Generic — works for manufacturing or services. Companies may omit requirements that don't apply to them.
ISO 9004 Quality Management Systems: Guidelines for Performance Improvement Best-practice advice for going beyond the 9001 minimum.
If the exam asks "which ISO 9000 standard can you be certified to?" — the answer is ISO 9001. The other two are reference (9000) and guidelines (9004); you cannot be "certified" to them.

15Statistical Techniques in QC

Definition · Statistics

The science that deals with the collection, classification, analysis, and making of inferences from data or information.

Two great branches

  • Descriptive statistics — describes the characteristics of a product or process using information collected on it. (Means, ranges, standard deviations, histograms.)
  • Inferential statistics — draws conclusions on unknown product or process parameters based on information contained in a sample.

Four key techniques used in QC

1 · Hypothesis Testing

Two hypotheses are evaluated: a null hypothesis (H₀) and an alternative hypothesis (H₁). The conclusion is to either reject or fail to reject the null hypothesis.

Example: H₀ = "the machine produces parts of mean diameter 10.00 mm." H₁ = "the mean has shifted." We take a sample, compute a test statistic, and decide.

Important nuance: we never "accept" H₀; we only "fail to reject" it — because the absence of evidence is not evidence of absence.

2 · Regression Analysis

Determines a mathematical expression describing the functional relationship between one response and one or more independent variables.

Example: Strength of a weld (response) as a function of current, voltage, and electrode angle (independents). A regression model tells you how each input drives the output.

3 · Statistical Process Control (SPC)

Monitors, controls, and improves processes through statistical techniques. SPC identifies when processes are out of control due to special-cause variation (variation caused by special circumstances, not inherent to the process). Once identified, practitioners may seek ways to remove that variation from the process. — This is so important we devote all of Part VI to it.

4 · Design and Analysis of Experiments (DOE)

Planning, conducting, analysing, and interpreting controlled tests to evaluate the factors that may influence a response variable. (Taguchi methods, which the course visits later, are the most famous DOE technique.)

Part VI · SPC & Control Charts

The Statistical Engine of Quality Control

This part is the heart of the numerical syllabus. Expect at least one calculation question on a control chart.

16Statistical Process Control (SPC)

Definition · SPC

The monitoring of a production process to detect and prevent poor quality, using statistical techniques.

Three core ideas:

  • Sample — a subset of items produced, used for inspection. (Inspecting 100% is usually impossible or destructive.)
  • Control chart — a graph that tells you whether the process is within statistical control limits.
  • Two types of characteristics: Attributes (discrete: good/bad, yes/no) and Variables (continuous: weight, length, temperature). Variables provide more information than attributes.

SPC is a tool for identifying problems in order to make improvements, and contributes directly to the TQM goal of continuous improvement.

17The Two Faces of Variation — Random vs Non-Random

Every process produces variation. Two parts coming off the same machine are never identical at the micron level. SPC sorts this variation into two camps:

Random (Common-Cause) Variation
  • Inherent in a process
  • Natural occurrences
  • Depends on equipment & machinery, engineering, operator, and system of measurement
  • Cannot be removed without redesigning the process
  • Process is "in control"
Non-Random (Special-Cause) Variation
  • Special causes
  • Identifiable and correctable
  • Includes: equipment out of adjustment, defective materials, changes in parts or materials, broken machinery, operator fatigue or poor work methods, errors due to lack of training
  • Process is "out of control"
  • Must be investigated and removed

SPC's only job is to tell you which type of variation you are looking at — so you waste no time chasing common-cause noise, and never miss a real special-cause signal.

18SPC Applied to Services

The nature of a "defect" is different in services. A service defect is any failure to meet customer requirements. What we monitor: time and customer satisfaction.

Industry Typical metrics monitored with SPC
Hospitals Timeliness & quickness of care, staff response to requests, lab-test accuracy, cleanliness, courtesy, paperwork accuracy, admittance & checkout speed
Grocery stores Checkout waiting time, frequency of out-of-stock items, food quality, cleanliness, customer complaints, register errors
Airlines Flight delays, lost / mis-handled luggage, ticket-counter waiting, agent & flight-attendant courtesy, flight info accuracy, cabin cleanliness
Fast-food restaurants Service waiting time, complaints, cleanliness, food quality, order accuracy, employee courtesy
Catalogue-order companies Order accuracy, operator knowledge & courtesy, packaging, delivery time, phone-order waiting time
Insurance companies Billing accuracy, timeliness of claims processing, agent availability & response time

19Where Should Control Charts Be Used?

You cannot chart every step of every process — it would cost too much. Use control charts where:

  • The process has a tendency to go out of control
  • The process is particularly harmful and costly if it goes out of control

Specific high-value locations:

  • At the start of a process — wasteful to begin production with bad supplies, so chart incoming quality
  • Before a costly or irreversible step — once you've welded, painted, or heat-treated, undoing it is expensive; catch defects before this point
  • Before and after assembly or painting operations that might cover defects
  • Before the final outgoing product or service is delivered to the customer

20Control Charts — Overview

Definition · Control Chart

A graphical tool for monitoring the activity of an ongoing process. It has a centreline (process average) and two control limits (Upper Control Limit, UCL, and Lower Control Limit, LCL) — usually placed at ±3σ from the centreline.

The four signals that a process is under control

  1. No sample points outside the limits
  2. Most points lie near the process average (centreline)
  3. Roughly equal number of points above and below the centreline
  4. Points appear randomly distributed — no patterns, trends, runs, or cycles
If any of these four conditions is violated — even if no point is outside the limits — the process is suspect. Patterns and trends matter as much as individual outliers.

The four types of control charts

Category Chart What it tracks
Attributes p-chart The proportion of defective items in a sample
c-chart The number of defects per sample
Variables X̄-chart The average of a quality characteristic in samples
R-chart The range (max − min) — i.e. dispersion — in samples
Easy mnemonic: p = proportion, c = count, X̄ = mean, R = range.

21The p-Chart — Proportion Defective

Use when you can classify each unit as defective or not, and report the proportion. Useful when sample sizes are large and you want one number per sample.

Formulas

UCL = p̄ + z · σp
LCL = p̄ − z · σp

σp = √[ p̄(1 − p̄) / n ] where p̄ = average sample proportion defective (estimate of process average), n = sample size, z = number of standard deviations (z = 3 for the usual 3-sigma chart, which gives the 99.73% confidence)

How to compute step-by-step

  1. Inspect a fixed number n from each sample; record number nonconforming, x.
  2. For each sample, p = x / n.
  3. Average across g samples: p̄ = (Σ x) / (g · n) — the centreline.
  4. Compute σp using the formula above.
  5. Compute UCL and LCL. If LCL comes out negative, set LCL = 0 (a proportion cannot be negative).
  6. Plot. Any point outside limits ⇒ investigate.
Lecture example — container nonconformities

25 samples of n = 50 containers each were inspected; total nonconforming items = 90 across all 25 × 50 = 1250 items.

Step 1. p̄ = 90 / 1250 = 0.072

Step 2. σp = √[0.072 × 0.928 / 50] = √(0.001336) ≈ 0.03658

Step 3. UCL = 0.072 + 3 × 0.03658 ≈ 0.1817

Step 4. LCL = 0.072 − 3 × 0.03658 ≈ −0.0377 → set to 0

Step 5. Plot the 25 sample proportions. Sample 18 hits 0.20, exceeding UCL = 0.1817 → an out-of-control signal. The log notes "Drop in pressure" at that time — special cause identified.

Step 6. Remove sample 18 from the data set, recompute p̄, σp, UCL, LCL. This is the revised p-chart.

22The c-Chart — Number of Defects per Sample

Use when the unit being inspected can have multiple defects (a sheet of fabric may have many flaws; a circuit board may have many soldering errors) and you count how many, rather than classifying the whole unit as good/bad.

Formulas

UCL = c̄ + z · σc
LCL = c̄ − z · σc

σc = √c̄ where c̄ = average number of defects per sample (centreline). Defect counts follow the Poisson distribution, hence σ = √mean.
Lecture example — fabric foreign-matter occurrences

25 samples of 100 m² fabric each are inspected. Number of nonconformities per sample is recorded.

Sum of nonconformities across 25 samples = 189 (from the table). So:

Step 1. c̄ = 189 / 25 = 7.56

Step 2. σc = √7.56 ≈ 2.749

Step 3. UCL = 7.56 + 3 × 2.749 ≈ 15.81

Step 4. LCL = 7.56 − 3 × 2.749 ≈ −0.69 → set to 0

Sample 9 has c = 16 nonconformities, just above UCL = 15.81 → out-of-control. Investigate, find the special cause, remove that sample, and replot → revised c-chart.

The square-root in σc = √c̄ comes from the Poisson distribution, where variance equals the mean. Don't forget to set LCL = 0 if the calculation gives a negative number — neither proportions nor counts can be negative.

23X̄ and R Charts — Variable Control Charts

Variable characteristics are measurable on a numerical scale (length, thickness, diameter, breaking strength, temperature, resistance). For these we need two charts working together:

  • X̄-chart monitors the process mean (centre of distribution)
  • R-chart monitors the process dispersion (spread of the distribution)

Why both? Because a process can drift in two ways: the mean shifts (parts get bigger or smaller on average) or the variability changes (parts get more inconsistent). A single chart cannot tell these apart.

Formulas — the foundational ones

For each sample of size n:
X̄ = ( Σ Xi ) / n     (sample mean)
R = Xmax − Xmin     (sample range)

Across g samples:
X̄̄ = ( Σ X̄i ) / g    (grand mean, centreline of X̄-chart)
R̄ = ( Σ Ri ) / g    (mean range, centreline of R-chart)

The X̄-chart limits (using ASTM constants)

UCLX̄ = X̄̄ + A2 · R̄
LCLX̄ = X̄̄ − A2 · R̄ A₂ is read from the ASTM table based on sample size n. (Conceptually, ±3σ from the grand mean, but the constant A₂ collapses the σ-from-R̄ calculation into a single look-up.)

The R-chart limits

UCLR = D4 · R̄
LCLR = D3 · R̄ D₃ and D₄ are also read from the ASTM table. For n ≤ 6, D₃ = 0 (so LCLR = 0).

Capability, Measurement, Sampling & Reliability

24Process Capability Analysis — the Cp Index

Once your process is in statistical control (no special causes), the next question is: is it good enough? "Good enough" means the natural process spread fits inside the customer's specification limits.

Assumption: 6σ (i.e. ±3σ) captures 99.74% of all process output — this is the "process spread." We compare this spread to the customer's tolerance band T = USL − LSL.

Cp = ( USL − LSL ) / ( 6σ ) = T / ( 6σ ) Also written as T / 6σ, or 2T / 6σ where T is half the tolerance. Used as written in the lecture: Cp = 2T / 6σ = T / 3σ.

Interpretation of Cp values

Cp Meaning Where it's the norm
< 1.00 Process spread exceeds tolerance → not capable. Defects guaranteed. —
= 1.00 Process spread exactly equals tolerance. Marginal — any drift and you fail. USA (historic baseline)
= 1.33 Comfortable margin — process spread is 75% of tolerance. Japan
= 1.66 Strong margin — process spread is 60% of tolerance. Japanese watch-making
= 2.00 "Six-Sigma" capable — process spread is 50% of tolerance. Aspirational target

The lecture's cost-vs-Cp diagram makes the economics visible: at Cp < 1 you need total inspection (most expensive), around Cp = 1 you can use lot inspection, by Cp = 1.33 sampling inspection is enough, by Cp = 2 you can rely on statistical process control, and beyond Cp = 5 there is essentially no defect. Higher capability = lower inspection cost.

25Measurement System Analysis (MSA)

Every control-chart decision relies on measurements. If the measurement is wrong, the decision is wrong.

Purpose · MSA

To qualify a measurement system for use by quantifying its accuracy, precision, and stability.

Accuracy vs Precision — the dartboard analogy

The lecture's four-target diagram captures this perfectly:

Pattern Accuracy Precision
Tight cluster on the bullseye ✓ Accurate ✓ Precise
Tight cluster, but off-centre ✗ Not accurate ✓ Precise
Scattered shots around the bullseye ✓ Accurate (on average) ✗ Not precise
Scattered shots, far from the bullseye ✗ Not accurate ✗ Not precise

Accuracy = closeness to the true value. Precision = closeness of repeated measurements to each other. They are independent properties; a measurement system must be both.

Requirements for a good measurement system

  1. Statistical stability over time — repeated measurements of the same item over hours/days/weeks must not drift.
  2. Variability small compared to process variability — if the gauge varies as much as the process, you cannot see the process.
  3. Variability small compared to specification limits (tolerance) — if the gauge varies as much as the tolerance band, you cannot tell good parts from bad.
  4. Resolution must be small relative to the smaller of (a) the specification tolerance or (b) the process spread. Rule of thumb: gauge resolution ≤ 10% of tolerance.

26Acceptance Sampling Plans

Imagine a truck arrives with 10,000 castings. Inspecting all 10,000 is too expensive (or destructive — destructive testing literally destroys the part). Instead you take a sample, inspect it, and decide whether to accept or reject the entire lot. That decision-procedure is the acceptance sampling plan.

Where it's used

  • Incoming raw materials, components, sub-assemblies
  • Various phases of in-process operations
  • Final product or service inspection
  • Between companies and their vendors
  • Between manufacturers and customers
  • Between departments or divisions of the same company

Two variants

  • Attribute sampling plan — a product item is classified as conforming or not, but the degree of conformance is not specified. (Some plans use "defect" and "defective" interchangeably with "nonconformity" and "nonconforming item".)
  • Variable sampling plan — the quality characteristic is expressed as a numerical value.

Honest limitations — the lecture stresses these

  • Acceptance sampling does not control or improve the quality of the process — it only sorts lots.
  • By the very nature of sampling, it will accept some lots and reject others, even when they are of the same quality — there is producer's risk and consumer's risk.
  • Therefore, methods of process control and improvement are essential — they are the only way to actually improve quality.

Why we still do it

  • If inspection is destructive (crash-testing helmets, burst-testing pressure cookers), 100% inspection is impossible.
  • Sampling is economical and causes less damage from handling.
  • Sampling provides a strong motivation to improve quality — because an entire batch can be rejected based on a sample, suppliers are incentivised to ship clean lots.

27Reliability Assessment of Systems

Definition · Reliability

The ability of a product to function effectively over a certain period under certain conditions. Related to the concept of quality of performance. The higher the reliability, the greater the customer satisfaction.

Note the three things baked into the definition: function effectively (it must do its job, not just exist), over a period (time matters), under conditions (those conditions must be specified — a car reliable on smooth German Autobahns may not be reliable on monsoon-soaked Indian roads).

How reliability is tested

  • Tests are usually destructive — the unit is used until it fails.
  • They involve observing a sample of items until a certain number of failures occur, observing over a fixed time to record how many fail, or a combination.
  • Usually done at the prototype stage — expensive depending on the unit cost.
  • Long accumulated test time gives a more precise estimate, but costs more — there is a trade-off.
  • The test should mimic actual operating conditions as closely as possible.

The three types of reliability tests

Test type Termination rule
Failure-Terminated Test Terminates when a preassigned number of failures occurs in the chosen sample.
Time-Terminated Test Terminates when a preassigned time is reached, regardless of how many units have failed.
Sequential Reliability Test No prior decision is made about number of failures or time. The accumulated results decide whether to accept the lot, reject the lot, or continue testing.
Easy way to remember: Failure-terminated ends on a count; Time-terminated ends on the clock; Sequential ends when the data says so.
Part VIII · Pareto Chart · Lab 1

The 80/20 Rule, Engineered

Pareto isn't just a graph — it is a decision-making tool that tells you where to act first based on data. Expect a numerical question.

28What is a Pareto Chart?

Definition · Pareto Chart

A bar chart in which categories are arranged in descending order of importance — usually by frequency, cost, or impact. A cumulative-percentage line is overlaid on the bars.

Behind it is the Pareto Principle (a.k.a. the 80/20 rule): in many real systems, a small number of causes is responsible for most of the observed effect. Roughly 80% of defects come from 20% of causes; 80% of profit from 20% of products; 80% of complaints from 20% of customers. It is not a mathematical law — it is an empirical regularity that holds astonishingly often.

Where it is used in industry

  • Manufacturing defect analysis
  • Machine breakdown analysis
  • Customer complaint analysis
  • Service quality improvement
  • Maintenance planning
  • Cost reduction studies

Industrial purpose: find the small number of causes that create the maximum loss, and attack those first.

Lecture mini-case — sheet metal factory

A factory has five recurring defects with these monthly costs: Incorrect dimensions $15,000, Burrs $5,000, Tool wear $2,000, Surface scratches $1,000, Machine vibration $500. Total = $23,500.

Two defects — Dimensions + Burrs = $20,000 — account for over 85% of the total loss. Recalibrating one pressing machine eliminated both. Result: $20,000 saved immediately, with zero effort wasted on minor vibration issues.

29How to Build a Pareto Chart — The 7 Steps

  1. List all categories of the problem.
  2. Find the frequency and/or unit cost of each.
  3. Compute total cost (or total impact) per category = frequency × unit cost.
  4. Arrange categories in descending order of total impact.
  5. Plot bars in that order; overlay the cumulative-percentage line.
  6. Interpret the chart — find the 80% cutoff.
  7. Decide which causes to eliminate first.
In industrial problems, cost-based Pareto is more useful than frequency-based Pareto. A high-frequency defect that costs almost nothing per occurrence may be far less important than a low-frequency defect that is catastrophic when it happens. Always rank by economic impact, not by raw count.

Reading the cumulative-percentage line

The line plots a running total of percentages. If the biggest bar is 50% of total cost, the line hits 50% at bar 1. If the next bar adds 30%, the line climbs to 80% at bar 2. Look for the point where the line crosses the 80% mark on the right-hand Y-axis. The bars sitting to the left of that intersection are your "vital few" — the categories that demand immediate engineering action. Everything to the right is the "trivial many."

Ishikawa & The Search for Root Causes

Where Pareto tells you which problem matters most, Fishbone tells you why that problem is happening.

33What is a Fishbone Diagram?

Definition · Fishbone (Ishikawa) Diagram

A visual tool used to systematically identify and present all possible causes of a specific problem (the "effect") in order to discover its root causes.

It is named after Kaoru Ishikawa, the Japanese quality guru. The visual looks like a fish: the head holds the effect (the problem); the spine runs left from the head; major branches angle off the spine like ribs, each labelled with one category of cause; sub-branches off each rib hold the specific causes brainstormed under that category.

Main idea

  • Problems are rarely caused by a single isolated factor.
  • By categorising causes (typically into the 6Ms), the diagram forces a team to look in every direction, not just the obvious one.

Why it is useful

  • Prevents engineering teams from focusing on symptoms rather than actual causes.
  • Structures cross-functional brainstorming sessions effectively (everyone gets to contribute in their area of expertise).

Where it is used in industry

  • Manufacturing defect root-cause analysis (RCA)
  • Investigating machinery breakdowns or automation failures
  • Resolving assembly-line bottlenecks
  • Quality-control failures in precision engineering

Industrial purpose: map out complex process interactions before allocating capital and engineering hours to fixes that turn out to address the wrong cause.

34The 6 Ms — Standard Branch Categories

M Category What goes here
1 Man Human factors: operator skill, training, fatigue, attention, motivation
2 Machine Equipment: machine condition, maintenance, calibration, tool wear, age
3 Material Inputs: raw-material quality, supplier variation, moisture, impurities
4 Method Process: SOPs, sequence, parameters, settings, pouring/feeding rates
5 Measurement Gauges & data: instrument accuracy, calibration, sampling method, data integrity
6 Mother Nature (Environment) Surroundings: temperature, humidity, dust, vibration, lighting

The 5-step recipe

  1. Define the effect clearly at the head of the fish.
  2. Determine categories — draw the spine and add a branch for each of the 6 Ms (or fewer, if some clearly don't apply).
  3. Brainstorm causes — for each category, ask "Why?" repeatedly and add sub-branches.
  4. Analyse — look for causes that appear repeatedly across categories, or represent significant deviations from standards.
  5. Action plan — select the most likely root causes for further investigation (often metallurgical, mechanical, or experimental).
Always combine the Ishikawa diagram with the "5 Whys" technique — keep asking "why?" until you reach a fundamental cause rather than a surface symptom. A good rule: typically five "whys" deep gets you to a true root cause.

35Worked Problem 1 — Casting Porosity

Scenario: An automotive foundry making cast-iron engine blocks. Ultrasonic inspection reports a severe spike in internal porosity (gas bubbles trapped in the metal) this week, leading to a high scrap rate. Directive: build a fishbone diagram before halting the whole foundry line.

Analysis

  • Material × Environment correlation: high moisture in the molding sand AND high shop-floor humidity are both sources of dissolved gases. They reinforce each other — this is the strongest signal.
  • Method variables (gating design + venting) decide whether the gases that form can escape the mold before solidification. Poor venting traps them as porosity.

Action / Decision

  • Immediate: test moisture content and permeability of the current sand batch; reduce pouring speed.
  • Secondary: review the CAD flow simulation of the gating system to confirm adequate venting for trapped gases.

36Worked Problem 2 — Sheet Metal Tearing

Scenario: Manufacturing plant producing deep-drawn aluminum enclosures for industrial switchgear. During the forming press operation, the sheet metal tears and fractures at the corners of the die. The production manager wants to increase lubrication, but the quality engineer suspects a tooling or material issue.

Analysis

  • Tearing in deep drawing is a delicate balance between Machine (blank-holder force) and Material (flow characteristics, grain orientation).
  • If the blank-holder force is too high, the material stretches and tears instead of flowing into the die.
  • If grain orientation is wrong, the metal cannot deform isotropically; corners — where the highest strain occurs — fail first.

Action / Decision

  • First step: verify incoming sheet-metal thickness and grain direction against supplier specifications.
  • Second step: recalibrate the blank-holder pressure on the hydraulic press and inspect the die radius for galling or wear.

(Note how the quality engineer's hunch — that the issue is tooling/material rather than lubrication — is validated by the diagram. Adding more lubrication addresses only one of eight identified causes, and not even the most likely one.)

Part X · SPC Lab · Control Charts in Practice

From Formulas to the Factory Floor

38Common Cause vs Special Cause — Lab View

Definition · Control Chart (Lab)

A time-ordered plot of sample statistics (like the mean or range) used to determine if a process is in a state of statistical control.

The lab restates the two flavours of variation we met in Part VI — but with sharper action-rules:

Common Cause Variation
  • Natural, expected background noise in the machine
  • Inherent to the process — present every shift, every batch
  • Action rule: leave the process alone
  • Reacting to common-cause noise (called "tampering") usually increases variation
Special Cause Variation
  • Unusual spikes caused by tool breakage, operator error, or material defects
  • Identifiable, removable, and not part of the normal process
  • Action rule: stop the machine and fix it
  • Ignoring a special cause is how you produce a truck-load of scrap

The whole point of SPC is to distinguish these two. If you treat noise as a signal, you tamper with a good machine. If you treat a signal as noise, you ship defects. Control charts are the only objective way to tell them apart.

39The X̄ & R Subgroup Strategy

In mass production we don't measure every single part — that would be prohibitively expensive (and sometimes destructive). Instead, we measure small batches called subgroups, taken at regular time intervals (every hour, every shift, every 100 parts).

From each subgroup of size n we compute two numbers, and plot them on two charts simultaneously:

Chart What is plotted What it tells you
X̄ chart The average of each subgroup Accuracy / centering — is the process drifting away from the target?
R chart The range (max − min) within each subgroup Precision / consistency — is the process becoming more variable inside each batch?

UCL and LCL for both charts are computed at ±3σ from the centreline (using the constants A₂, D₃, D₄ from the ASTM table you already have in Part VI). Any point outside these lines fires a defect trigger.

A process can be accurate but not precise, or precise but not accurate, or both, or neither. The X̄ chart alone cannot tell these apart — and neither can the R chart alone. You must read them as a pair. The two upcoming worked problems are designed to make this point unforgettable.

40Worked Lab Problem 1 — CNC Slot Milling (an X̄ violation)

Scenario: A CNC mill cuts a 10.0 mm slot. The operator pulls 4 parts every hour and measures the width.

Hour Part 1 Part 2 Part 3 Part 4 X̄ R
8:00 AM 10.1 10.0 10.1 9.9 10.025 0.2

11:00 AM 10.1 10.0 9.9 10.0 10.000 0.2

  • X̄̄ = (10.025 + 10.000 + 10.500 + 10.000)/4 = 10.1375 mm
  • R̄ = (0.2 + 0.2 + 0.2 + 0.2)/4 = 0.20 mm (the actual R-chart in the lab used a recalculated R̄ of 0.175 from including all values — the lab's Origin output shows R̄ = 0.175)

The machine produced four nicely-matched parts at 10:00 AM, but at the wrong target. 42The Rule of 7 — Drift Without Crossing the Limits

So far we have flagged out-of-control points by them crossing the UCL or LCL. But there is a subtler signal — and the assignment problem is designed to test it.

Rule · The Rule of 7 (a.k.a. Run Rule)

If 7 (or more) consecutive points trend steadily in the same direction (all increasing or all decreasing), the process is "out of control" — even if no individual point has crossed the UCL or LCL.

Why? Because in a truly random process, getting 7 increasing points in a row by chance is about 1 in 64 — vanishingly rare. So when it happens, it almost certainly means something is drifting: a wire tensioner loosening, a cutting tool wearing, a temperature sensor slowly miscalibrating.

Assignment scenario — resistor winding drift

An electronics manufacturer is winding 100 Ω resistors. They sample 5 resistors per shift:

Look at the X̄ column: 100.00 → 100.04 → 100.24 → 100.46 → 100.64. Every single shift the mean increases. No point may have crossed the UCL yet — but the trend is unmistakable.

This is the proactive power of SPC. You do not have to wait for a defect to be produced; the chart screams "drift!" 7 shifts before the first scrapped part. Action: stop the line, inspect and reset the tensioner, then resume.

Final lab takeaway — the SQC journey

  • Quality is proactive, not reactive. Control charts let you see a machine breaking before it actually produces a scrapped part.
  • Always do the dual check. A process can have perfect accuracy (X̄) but terrible precision (R), or vice versa.
  • The full SQC toolchain you have now learned: Fishbone to brainstorm root causes → Pareto to prioritise → Scatter / Multivariable charts to verify relationships → Taguchi to optimise → SPC to monitor and sustain. That is the full life-cycle of quality engineering on a factory floor.
Part XI · Design of Experiments · Taguchi Method

Building Quality into the Design

Taguchi is the Japanese-engineered shortcut to robust products. Instead of running 100 experiments to optimise a process, you run 4, or 9, and let an orthogonal array do the heavy lifting.

43What is the Taguchi Method?

Definition · Taguchi Method

A standardised approach to Design of Experiments (DoE) that optimises process parameters to make the product "robust" to noise.

The Taguchi Philosophy

Traditional DoE asks: "what settings give the best average result?" Taguchi asks a different, deeper question: "what settings give the most consistent result, even when the environment misbehaves?"

  • Traditional DoE hunts for the mean.
  • Taguchi hunts for consistency. A bracket with average strength 50 MPa that fluctuates 40–60 MPa is worse than a bracket with average 48 MPa that always sits at 47–49 MPa — even though the first sounds stronger on paper.

The mathematical heart of this philosophy is Taguchi's "Loss to Society" concept: any deviation from the target value is a cost, not just deviations that cross a spec limit. A bracket that is 49.9 MPa when the target is 50 MPa is slightly wasteful even though it passes inspection. Taguchi wants to minimise the squared deviation from target — and the bigger your variance, the larger that loss.

If asked "what does Taguchi optimise that traditional DoE does not?" — answer: robustness to noise / variance around the target, not just the mean. Always cite "Loss to Society" as the formal philosophy.

44The Signal-to-Noise (S/N) Ratio

Taguchi rolls "performance" and "consistency" into a single quality metric: the S/N ratio.

Concept · S/N Ratio

Signal = the desired effect (strength, accuracy, the thing you want). Noise = uncontrollable variation (temperature swings, humidity, batch-to-batch drift). The S/N ratio measures how much signal you get per unit of noise.

Goal: identify the parameter settings that maximise the S/N ratio. Higher is always better, regardless of the underlying problem type.

45Three S/N Objectives

Depending on what your quality characteristic measures, Taguchi defines three flavours of S/N:

Objective When to use S/N Formula (per run, n responses yi)
Smaller-the-Better Defects, shrinkage, surface roughness, wear, noise — anything you want to minimise S/N = −10·log₁₀[ (1/n) Σ yi² ]
Larger-the-Better Tensile strength, power, fuel efficiency, throughput — anything you want to maximise S/N = −10·log₁₀[ (1/n) Σ (1/yi²) ]
Nominal-the-Best Dimensions with a specific target (e.g. 10.0 mm shaft) — neither over nor under is good S/N = 10·log₁₀( ȳ² / s² )

Critical rule — works for ALL three types:

The optimum is always the parameter level with the highest (largest, most positive) S/N ratio. Even for Smaller-the-Better — you do NOT pick the most negative S/N. The negative numbers just mean noise > signal; higher (less negative) is still better.

46Orthogonal Arrays — The Taguchi Shortcut

If you have 3 factors, each at 2 levels, a full factorial experiment requires 2³ = 8 runs. With 7 factors at 2 levels, full factorial blows up to 2⁷ = 128 runs. Taguchi noticed that you can pick a clever subset that still extracts the main effect of each factor — and standardised these subsets into orthogonal arrays.

Array Runs Factors Levels per factor Saves vs full factorial
L4 4 up to 3 2 50% (vs 8 runs)
L8 8 up to 7 2 ~94% (vs 128 runs)
L9 9 up to 4 3 ~89% (vs 81 runs)
L18 18 mixed levels 2 & 3 even larger

Origin Pro and Minitab automatically pick the smallest valid array once you tell them "3 factors, 2 levels" → L4; "3 factors, 3 levels" → L9; etc.

The output you read: the Main Effects Plot & the Response Table

  • For each factor, average the S/N ratio at each level → plot these averages as line segments.
  • A steeper line (larger Delta = max − min S/N within that factor) means that factor has a larger effect on the response.
  • The response table ranks factors by Delta: Rank 1 = most influential, Rank n = least influential.
  • To find the optimum, pick the level with the highest S/N for each factor independently — this works because the array is orthogonal (factors are independent in the design).

Part XII · Battery Equivalent Circuit Modelling

Why a Lithium-Ion Cell Doesn't Behave Like an Ideal Battery

A whole separate strand of the syllabus: the equivalent-circuit model (ECM) used to describe how lithium-ion cells behave under real load — the foundation of every modern Battery Management System (BMS) and the basis of State-of-Charge estimation in electric vehicles.

51Why ECM? The State-of-Charge Estimation Problem

The problem statement

Performance testing of a battery for State-of-Charge (SoC) estimation.

Why does this matter?

To prevent over-charge (which damages the cathode and can cause thermal runaway) and over-discharge (which damages the anode and shortens cell life) — and to give the driver an accurate "% battery remaining" reading. Without an accurate SoC estimate, you cannot operate an EV safely.

The trouble: you cannot measure SoC directly

You cannot pop open a lithium-ion cell and count electrons. What you can measure is the terminal voltage, the current flowing in or out, and the cell temperature. From these noisy electrical signals, the BMS has to infer how much charge is left.

The trick is that terminal voltage ≠ open-circuit voltage while current is flowing. The voltage you measure under load is distorted by internal losses inside the cell. To peel back those distortions and recover the true open-circuit voltage (from which SoC can be looked up via an OCV-SoC curve), you need a model of those internal losses. That model is the equivalent circuit.

52The 1RC Equivalent Circuit Model

The simplest non-trivial ECM has four elements:

1RC ECM Components
  • Voc — Open Circuit Voltage. The terminal voltage when no load is connected. Depends on SoC.
  • R0 — Ohmic Resistance. Captures the instantaneous losses: resistance of the electrodes, electrolyte resistance, charge-transfer resistance, contact resistance between the current collector and the active materials. Manifests as an instantaneous voltage drop the moment a load is connected.
  • Ra, Ca — The Active (Electrochemical) Polarisation pair. Models the slow build-up of voltage loss that occurs as the chemical reaction at the electrode surface gets started. The capacitor Ca charges over a time constant τa = RaCa.
  • VL — the Load Voltage you actually measure at the terminals.

The whole story: VL = Voc − I·R0 − VRC, where VRC is the voltage across the parallel RaCa branch. The negative signs are for discharge (current flowing out); during charge they flip.

Circuit topology

The voltage-response signature — how to read the curve

If you inject a step pulse of current into the cell (e.g. a discharge pulse), the terminal voltage responds in a characteristic shape that reveals the model parameters:

  1. Instantaneous step down the moment current starts → this drop equals I·R0. So R0 can be read directly from the size of the step.
  2. Slow exponential droop after the step → this is the RaCa branch charging up. The time constant of the droop is τa = Ra·Ca, and the final magnitude tells you Ra.
  3. When the current stops, the voltage recovers in a mirror-image: instantaneous jump (R0) + slow exponential return to Voc (RaCa).

53The Three Types of Polarisation — Kinetics of Electron Transfer

Definition · Polarisation Effect

Polarisation = the voltage losses caused by electrochemical processes during charge or discharge. This is precisely why the output (terminal) voltage of a battery is different from its open-circuit voltage when current flows.

There are three distinct mechanisms, each operating on a different time scale, each captured by a different element of the ECM:

Type Physical mechanism Time scale ECM element
1. Ohmic Polarisation Pure resistance — electrons forcing their way through electrodes, electrolyte, and contacts. Pure I·R losses. Instantaneous (microseconds) R0
2. Active (Electrochemical) Polarisation The voltage required to drive the chemical reaction at the electrode surface — overcoming the activation energy of Li⁺ leaving / entering the lattice. Seconds to minutes Ra·Ca (1st RC pair)
3. Concentration Polarisation Ions cannot diffuse through the electrolyte fast enough to replace those consumed at the electrode surface. Local concentration gradients build up. Becomes dominant as the cell approaches empty. Minutes to hours (very slow) Rc·Cc (2nd RC pair)

You can see these three regimes plotted against time during a discharge:

  • "Discharge begins" — the voltage drops vertically. This is the ohmic R0 talking. Nearly instantaneous.
  • Mid-discharge — a long, gentle exponential droop dominated by the active/electrochemical polarisation (RaCa).
  • "Discharge ends" — a sharp downward knee right before the cell collapses. This is the concentration polarisation (RcCc) kicking in: the lithium ions cannot diffuse fast enough through the depleted electrolyte to keep the surface reactions going, so voltage falls off a cliff.
If the exam shows a discharge curve and asks "which polarisation dominates here?" — read the time scale: instantaneous step = Ohmic, gradual middle = Active, sharp knee at end = Concentration.

54The 2RC Equivalent Circuit Model

The 1RC model captures only ohmic + active polarisation. To capture the third effect (concentration polarisation, dominant at the end of discharge), we add a second RC branch in series:

2RC ECM Components
  • Voc — Open Circuit Voltage
  • R0 — Ohmic resistance (instantaneous)
  • Ra·Ca — Active / Electrochemical polarisation (fast RC dynamics)
  • Rc·Cc — Concentration polarisation (slow RC dynamics — captures the ion-diffusion physics through the electrolyte)
  • VL — Load voltage

Topologically it's the 1RC model with one more R-C-parallel pair stuffed into the series chain.

Why bother with a second RC branch?

Because the 1RC model is wrong at the end of discharge. After current stops, the 1RC predicts the voltage recovers cleanly to Voc after the RaCa transient. But real cells show an additional, slower initial voltage relaxation tail — a much longer-time-scale return-to-equilibrium as the lithium-ion concentration gradients in the electrolyte diffuse out. The 2RC's slow Rc·Cc pair captures this exactly.

55Performance of the ECMs — Comparing 1RC vs 2RC

Why we add more RC branches

To evaluate the performance of the battery during charging and discharging conditions, as ions transfer between electrodes through the electrolyte. Each additional RC pair captures a slower physical phenomenon that the simpler model missed.

Reading the voltage-response plots

If you feed the same pulse-current waveform into both a 1RC and a 2RC model, the voltage responses differ in revealing ways:

Model Captures Signature in V(t)
1RC Ohmic + Active/Electrochemical polarisation Sharp step (R₀) followed by a smooth exponential droop (RaCa). Misses the slow recovery tail.
2RC Ohmic + Active + Concentration polarisation Sharp step (R₀) + fast exponential (RaCa) + slow exponential tail (RcCc). Matches real-cell behaviour much better, especially near end-of-discharge.

SoC and Temperature trends

When the same pulse profile is applied to both models:

  • State of Charge (SoC): The 1RC model shows a coarse, blocky SoC trace (because it cannot capture slow dynamics, it under-predicts the cell's true reserve). The 2RC model produces a smoother, more realistic SoC profile — and at much higher absolute SoC values, closer to what the cell actually has remaining.
  • Temperature: The 1RC model predicts large temperature swings (up to ~34°C during pulses) because all the heat dissipation is concentrated into one resistor (R0). The 2RC model spreads the heat across three resistors, predicting much smaller, more physically realistic temperature excursions (~20–20.5°C).

The bigger lesson: ECM is a trade-off between fidelity and computational cost. A real BMS in an EV has milliseconds to estimate SoC for the dashboard, so it cannot afford a 10-RC model. 2RC is the sweet spot for most lithium-ion cells — captures the three dominant polarisation effects, runs fast enough for real-time control, and is the de-facto industry standard.

56Reading the Voltage Relaxation Curve — Putting It All Together

Imagine the cell is charged, then a load is connected, then disconnected. The full V(t) waveform reads like a story written in three different time scales:

  1. Charging mode (current in): Voltage climbs slowly as SoC rises; small ripples reflect transient RC charging.
  2. Steady state (no current): VL = Voc. This is the only condition under which you can read SoC directly from the OCV-SoC lookup curve.
  3. Discharge step (current out):
    • Instantaneous vertical drop of magnitude I·R0 → Ohmic loss
    • Slow exponential additional droop, time constant ≈ RaCa → Active / Electrochemical polarisation
  4. Current cut off (relaxation):
    • Instantaneous upward jump of I·R0 (the ohmic loss is gone — instantly)
    • Slow exponential recovery from RaCa discharging
    • Even slower additional recovery tail from RcCc (the initial voltage relaxation as ion gradients diffuse out) — this is only seen in 2RC and real cells, not in 1RC
If you're asked to label a load-voltage-vs-time plot: label the vertical edges as R₀ (ohmic), the fast curved bits as RaCa (active), the slow recovery tail as RcCc (concentration), and the horizontal plateaus as Voc.

Why this is the foundation of EV battery management

Every modern EV's BMS runs an online version of this ECM, continuously fitting R0, Ra, Ca, Rc, Cc to live voltage/current measurements (typically via a Kalman filter or recursive least squares). From these fitted parameters it back-calculates Voc, then looks up SoC. Without ECM, no accurate SoC. Without SoC, no safe EV operation.

Part XIII · Scatter Plots & Correlation · Lab

Proving Cause & Effect with Two Variables

The bridge between guessing and knowing. Where Fishbone suggests a cause, a scatter plot proves it — with hard numbers and a correlation coefficient that survives any argument.

57What is a Scatter Plot?

Definition · Scatter Plot

A two-dimensional graph used to visualise the relationship (correlation) between two continuous numerical variables. Each point on the graph represents one paired observation — an X-value and a Y-value measured at the same instant, on the same unit.

By convention the X-axis holds the independent variable — the cause, the parameter you control (temperature, RPM, voltage, sand moisture). The Y-axis holds the dependent variable — the effect, the quality characteristic you measure (hardness, roughness, pressure, shrinkage).

The single question every scatter plot is built to answer is:

"If I change variable X, what happens to variable Y?"

If the cloud of points slopes, the variables are correlated. If it's a random blob, they're not. That's the whole technique — the rest is interpretation.

Why it is useful

The previous tool in your syllabus — the Fishbone diagram — only suggests possible causes. It is a brainstorming map. A scatter plot is the proof step. It takes one of those suspected causes and tests it against data: "You said cutting speed affects surface finish? Show me the numbers." If r ≈ 0.95, you were right. If r ≈ 0.05, you were guessing.

58Where Scatter Plots Are Used in Industry

  • Optimising machine parameters — e.g., spindle RPM vs surface roughness on a CNC lathe.
  • Material property testing — e.g., heat-treatment temperature vs alloy tensile strength.
  • Sensor calibration — comparing a sensor's output voltage to the actual measured pressure.

Industrial purpose: mathematically establish whether two variables are connected before writing Standard Operating Procedures. Without a scatter plot, an SOP that says "run at 170 m/min for best finish" is just opinion. With a scatter plot showing r = −0.97, it becomes evidence-based engineering.

59How to Solve a Scatter Plot Problem — The 5 Steps

  1. Collect paired data. Every (X, Y) pair must be measured at the same instant on the same unit. Mismatched pairs are meaningless.
  2. Determine axes. Controlled/input variable on X. Measured/output variable on Y. Swapping them flips your cause-effect interpretation.
  3. Plot the points on a standard Cartesian graph. Do not connect them with a line — they are independent observations, not a time series.
  4. Analyse the pattern:
    • Positive correlation: cloud slopes upward — X up, Y up.
    • Negative correlation: cloud slopes downward — X up, Y down.
    • No correlation: random scatter, no shape.
    • Non-linear correlation: a clear curve (parabolic, exponential decay, U-shape) — strong relationship, but a straight-line fit won't fully capture it.
  5. Action plan: use the trend to predict, optimise, or set a process window.

60The Origin Pro Workflow

If the exam asks "how would you plot this in Origin Pro?", recite these six steps:

  1. Enter the independent variable into column A(X).
  2. Enter the dependent variable into column B(Y).
  3. Highlight both columns.
  4. Go to Plot → Basic 2D → Scatter.
  5. With the graph active, go to Analysis → Fitting → Linear Fit → Open Dialog.
  6. Click OK. Origin draws the line of best fit and produces a results table containing the slope, intercept, the Pearson correlation coefficient r, and R² (R-Square COD).

63The Pearson Correlation Coefficient r — The Master Number

Pearson's r quantifies the strength and direction of a linear relationship in a single number between −1 and +1.

r value Meaning
+1.0 Perfect positive linear relationship
+0.8 to +1.0 Strong positive
+0.3 to +0.8 Moderate positive
0 No linear relationship
−0.3 to −0.8 Moderate negative
−0.8 to −1.0 Strong negative
−1.0 Perfect negative linear relationship
Engineering Rule of Thumb

If |r| > 0.8 → Strong relationship. You can confidently use variable X to control variable Y.

If |r| < 0.3 → Changing X will likely have no useful effect on Y. Don't waste time tuning X.

The relationship between r and R²

R² = r². R² tells you the fraction of variance in Y that is explained by variation in X. So r = 0.9 ⇒ R² = 0.81, meaning “81% of the variation in Y is explained by X; the remaining 19% is unexplained noise or other variables.”

Hand-calculation formulas (in case the exam disables the lab computer)

Pearson r:
r = Σ(xi − x̄)(yi − ȳ) / √[ Σ(xi − x̄)² · Σ(yi − ȳ)² ]
Best-fit line:
Slope b = Σ(xi − x̄)(yi − ȳ) / Σ(xi − x̄)²
Intercept a = ȳ − b·x̄

65The Killer Final Point — Correlation ≠ Causation

Critical warning: Just because two variables move together on a graph does not guarantee that one causes the other. They might both be reacting to a hidden third variable.

Classic counter-example: ice-cream sales and drowning deaths both rise in summer. There's no causation between them — both are driven by ambient temperature.

Manufacturing version: machine vibration and surface defects might rise simultaneously not because vibration causes defects, but because high ambient temperature causes both. Reducing vibration won't help. Always combine statistical plots with engineering logic.

Scatter Plots in one line: the bridge between guessing and knowing. They prove, with a single number r, whether changing X will actually move Y — but only engineering judgement decides whether the relationship is causal.

Part XIV · Multivariable Charts · Lab

Where Does the Variation Actually Come From?

Total process variation is almost always dominated by one source. Multivariable charts plot the data hierarchically so the dominant source becomes visible — before any ANOVA, before any Taguchi, before anyone wastes a week tweaking the wrong knob.

66What is a Multivariable Chart?

Definition · Multivariable Chart

A visual tool used to analyse the variation in a process by breaking it down into three distinct categories: Positional (within-part), Cyclical (part-to-part), and Temporal (time-to-time).

The main idea is that overall process variation is almost always dominated by ONE major source. Rather than dive straight into expensive ANOVA mathematics, we plot the data hierarchically so we can physically see where the largest spread is. The eye is faster than the calculator.

Why it is useful

It rapidly narrows the search area for root-cause analysis. The chart tells the engineer “look at tool wear” or “look at ambient temperature” instead of leaving them to blindly tweak every machine parameter at once.

67Where Multivariable Charts Are Used in Industry

Critical anywhere there is complex tooling with multiple potential variation sources:

  • CNC Machining — is a shaft tapered along its length (positional) or is the average diameter shrinking across the batch as the tool dulls (cyclical)?
  • Injection Molding — comparing Cavity 1 vs Cavity 2 (positional inside the mold) against Shift 1 vs Shift 2 (temporal).
  • Coating / Plating — measuring paint or plating thickness at the edge vs the centre of an aerospace panel (positional within a single panel).

Industrial purpose: prevent engineers from adjusting the wrong parameters by proving exactly when and where the variation occurs.

68The Three Sources of Variation — Define Each Precisely

1. Positional · Within-Part

Variation appearing on a single unit, at different physical locations on that unit.

Examples: a cylinder that is out of round · a shaft thicker at the top than the bottom · paint thickness at the edge of a panel differing from at the centre.

2. Cyclical · Part-to-Part

Variation between consecutive units produced in the same batch.

Example: Part 1 is perfectly sized, Part 2 is slightly smaller, Part 3 is smaller still — a classic signature of tool wear accumulating across a batch.

3. Temporal · Time-to-Time

Variation occurring hour-to-hour, shift-to-shift, day-to-day.

Example: the morning shift produces different dimensions than the night shift because ambient factory temperature has changed.

Memory hook: P·C·T. Positional = "where on one part." Cyclical = "next part vs this part." Temporal = "different time."

69How to Read a Multivariable Chart Visually

This is the whole technique. When you look at the plotted chart, check three spreads:

  • Long vertical spread within a single time-point or part (the L-C-R or Cavity-1-through-4 lines stretch far apart vertically at one X-position) → Positional variation is dominant.
  • Large jumps between consecutive parts at the same time (Blade A and Blade B are far apart within the same batch) → Cyclical variation is dominant.
  • Whole cluster of points shifts up or down between time periods (an entire batch sits at a totally different height from another batch) → Temporal variation is dominant.

Decision rule: whichever spread is biggest is your culprit. It's that simple.

70The Origin Pro Workflow

The data must be structured in three columns:

  • A(X) — the outer axis (Part number or Time / Shift).
  • B(Y) — the inner Category (Left/Centre/Right, or Cavity 1/2/3/4).
  • C(Y) — the actual Measurement value.
  1. Highlight all three columns.
  2. Go to Plot → Categorical → Grouped Scatter.
  3. Assign the X-axis to your Part/Time column, and the Grouping/Color to your Category column.
  4. Double-click a plotted point → open the Drop Lines or Line tab → tick “Connect Data Points within Group”. This draws the vertical/horizontal spans that reveal the variation pattern.

Scenario: A CNC lathe produces steel drive shafts. Target diameter = 20.0 mm. QC measures three positions (Left, Centre, Right) on three consecutive parts.

Part Left (mm) Centre (mm) Right (mm)
Part 1 20.10 20.05 19.90
Part 2 20.08 20.04 19.88
Part 3 20.09 20.06 19.91

Compute the three spreads

Source Calculation Typical Spread
Positional (Left − Right on each part) 0.20 / 0.20 / 0.18 mm ≈ 0.20 mm
Cyclical (Left across parts: 20.10 / 20.08 / 20.09) range 0.02 mm ≈ 0.02 mm
Temporal only one time period — N/A —

Positional ≈ 10× larger than Cyclical. The vertical spread inside each part dwarfs the horizontal spread across parts.

Conclusion: Dominant source = Positional.

Correct action: Do not change the cutting tool (that would address cyclical wear, which isn't the problem). Realign the CNC tailstock. A misaligned tailstock applies an off-axis force during turning, causing a consistent left-to-right taper that repeats on every part — exactly the pattern we observe.

Positional (Cavity-to-Cavity within a shift) e.g., Night: 5.46 − 5.44 ≈ 0.02 mm

Tooling (positional uniformity) and process control (cyclical consistency) are both healthy; the time-period is the problem.

74Why Multivariable Charts Come Before Taguchi DoE

Two statements to commit to memory.

  1. Multivariable charts don't fix anything; they're a map telling you where to look. They reduce the search space for root-cause analysis from “everything” to “this one specific source.”
  2. Always run a Multivariable chart before designing a Taguchi DoE. If your process has high Temporal variation — drifting day-to-day from ambient conditions — then any DoE you run will be ruined by background noise. Your factor effects will be confounded with time-drift, and your conclusions will be wrong. You need a temporally stable process to experiment on, otherwise you're optimising inside a moving target.
If a question gives you a process with shifting daily means and asks “should you run a Taguchi DoE on this?” — the answer is no, first stabilise the Temporal variation (find and fix whatever drifts day-to-day), then DoE the now-stable process.

Multivariable charts in one line: a fast visual triage that decides which of three variation buckets — Positional, Cyclical, or Temporal — is eating your tolerance, so you can attack the right source and stop wasting effort on the wrong ones.

Pareto numerical recipe (10 sec walkthrough)

  1. Total cost = frequency × unit cost (do this for every row).
  2. Sort descending.
  3. Cumulative % column.
  4. Find where cum % crosses 80%.
  5. Categories to the left of the crossing = your "vital few" to attack.
  6. If budget-constrained, pick top items greedily while staying under budget.

Control-chart workflow (the exam pattern)

  1. Identify chart type from the question: "proportion defective" → p; "defects per unit" → c; "measured length/weight" → X̄ & R.
  2. Compute centreline (p̄ or c̄ or X̄̄ & R̄).
  3. Compute σ (or read A₂, D₃, D₄ from the table).
  4. Compute UCL and LCL.
  5. Plot mentally; check which samples sit outside the limits.
  6. State special causes; revise the chart if a cause is identified.
  7. Lab dual-check: if X̄ is fine but R explodes → precision issue (e.g. one nozzle off); if R is fine but X̄ shifts → accuracy issue (e.g. tool offset drift).
  8. Rule of 7: 7+ consecutive points trending one way = drift, even with no UCL crossing.

Taguchi workflow (the exam pattern)

  1. Identify objective type → pick S/N formula: minimise = Smaller-the-Better; maximise = Larger-the-Better; hit target = Nominal-the-Best.
  2. Compute S/N for each run using the right formula.
  3. For each factor, average S/N at each of its levels.
  4. Compute Delta (max − min) for each factor; rank by Delta size.
  5. Pick optimal level = the level with the highest S/N for each factor (works for all three types).
  6. Identify the dominant factor (Rank 1) — must be controlled tightly. Identify the weak factors (low Rank) — set them at the cheapest level to save cost.

Battery ECM workflow (the exam pattern)

  1. If asked to identify polarisation type from a V(t) curve: instantaneous step → Ohmic (R0); gradual exponential droop → Active (RaCa); sharp knee at end of discharge or slow recovery tail → Concentration (RcCc).
  2. If asked to draw a 1RC or 2RC: Voc source on the left, R0 in series, then 1 or 2 parallel-RC pairs in series, output to terminals VL.
  3. If asked "why 2RC instead of 1RC?" → "to capture concentration polarisation (slow ion-diffusion dynamics), which dominates near end-of-discharge and during long voltage relaxation."
  4. If asked SoC estimation logic → "measure VL and I, use ECM to back-calculate Voc, look up SoC from OCV–SoC curve."

Scatter Plot workflow (the exam pattern)

  1. Identify X = controlled / input and Y = measured / output. Get these the right way around or the cause-effect interpretation flips.
  2. Compute means x̄, ȳ; then deviations (xi − x̄) and (yi − ȳ).
  3. Build the table of (x−x̄)(y−ȳ), (x−x̄)², (y−ȳ)² and sum each column.
  4. Pearson r = Σ(x−x̄)(y−ȳ) / √[ Σ(x−x̄)² · Σ(y−ȳ)² ].
  5. Classify: |r| > 0.8 strong (X can control Y); 0.3 < |r| < 0.8 moderate; |r| < 0.3 negligible.
  6. Compute regression line: b = Σ(x−x̄)(y−ȳ) / Σ(x−x̄)²; a = ȳ − b·x̄. Predict / set process window from the line.
  7. Write the memo: state r, state direction (positive/negative), state strength, recommend the action.
  8. Always close with the disclaimer: correlation ≠ causation; verify with engineering logic.

Multivariable Chart workflow (the exam pattern)

  1. Identify the three variation buckets from the data structure: positions on one part → Positional; consecutive parts in the same batch → Cyclical; different time periods / shifts / batches → Temporal.
  2. Compute the spread for each: Positional = max−min within one part; Cyclical = max−min across parts at one position; Temporal = max−min of grand averages across time periods.
  3. Whichever spread is biggest is the dominant source.
  4. State the conclusion and pair it with the correct corrective action: Positional → fix geometry/setup (tailstock alignment, mold cavity); Cyclical → fix tool wear / replace cutter; Temporal → investigate the specific bad time period (shift, batch, day).
  5. Pre-DoE check: if Temporal variation is high, do not proceed with a Taguchi DoE until that drift is removed.

Seven final pieces of advice for tomorrow:

1. Sketch every control chart, fishbone, scatter plot, multivariable chart, and circuit — even if not asked. Clean diagrams earn marks even when calculation is partial.

2. Always check the sign of LCL. If your formula gives a negative LCL for p or c, set LCL = 0 and write a one-line justification.

3. For Pareto, rank by cost, not frequency. Most common conceptual trap.

4. For Taguchi, pick the highest S/N — always. Don't fall for the "smaller-the-better means most negative S/N" trap.

5. For Scatter Plots, state r and direction — and always add the “correlation ≠ causation” line. That sentence alone is worth a mark in most rubrics.

6. For Multivariable Charts, compute the spreads explicitly (Positional, Cyclical, Temporal) and circle the biggest. Don't just eyeball — show the numbers.

7. For ECM, label every kink in a voltage curve with the right polarisation type. Examiners love this question.
