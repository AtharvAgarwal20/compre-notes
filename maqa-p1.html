Foundations of Metrology — Errors, Tolerances & the ISO System

1.1 What is Metrology?

Definition
Metrology
Metrology is the science of measurement. In this course the focus is on measurement of geometry, surface attributes, and the quality of manufactured products.

Metrology bridges the gap between the designer's vision and the actual product. It has become an indispensable part of modern infrastructure because trading in national & international markets requires conformance to written standards and mutual recognition of measurements/tests.

Beyond industry, metrology establishes standards in health sciences, safety and environment, and most importantly produces the international standards of measurement used by every country in science and industry.

The Three Branches of Metrology

Legal Metrology

Any application of metrology subject to national laws or regulations. Its main objective is uniformity of measurement in a particular country. Example: the labelled net weight on a packaged food item (the "900 g" stamped on a sauce jar).

Dynamic Metrology

Measurement of small variations of a continuous nature. Records continuous measurements over a surface and offers obvious advantages over individual discrete measurements. Example: continuous ECG-style surface profile recording.

Deterministic Metrology

Part measurement is substituted by process measurement. Example: 3-D error compensation in CNC systems. Used in high-precision machinery for micro- and nano-technology accuracies.

(Why all three?)

Each branch is a different philosophy of measurement. Legal protects consumers, dynamic captures phenomena that vary in time/space, deterministic eliminates inspection bottlenecks by controlling the process itself.

1.2 Need for Inspection

Definition
Inspection
A procedure in which a part or product characteristic (such as a dimension) is examined to determine whether it conforms to the design specification.

Inspection is carried out to isolate and evaluate a specific design or quality attribute of a component. It gained importance because of mass production, which involved interchangeability of parts.

Measurement vs Gauging

Measurement TechniqueGauging Technique
Measures the actual dimension of the partDoes not reveal the actual value
SlowerFaster
Returns a numberReturns only "within limits" or "not"

Eight Reasons Inspection Exists

  1. Ascertain conformance to the established/desired standard.
  2. Accomplish interchangeability of manufacture.
  3. Sustain customer goodwill — no defective product should reach customers.
  4. Find inadequacies in manufacture; results are reported back to the manufacturing department.
  5. Ensure good-quality raw materials, tools and equipment are purchased.
  6. Coordinate Quality Control, Production, Purchasing and other departments.
  7. Decide whether rework on defective parts is feasible.
  8. Promote a spirit of competition to manufacture quality products in bulk.

1.3 Accuracy and Precision

Definition · Accuracy
Accuracy
The degree of agreement of the measured dimension with its true magnitude. True value = the mean of an infinite number of measurements when the average deviation due to contributing factors tends to zero.
Definition · Precision
Precision
The degree of repetitiveness of the measuring process. If an instrument is not precise, repeated readings of the same dimension give different results. Precision = repeatability.

The classic dartboard illustration: a tightly grouped cluster off the bullseye is precise but not accurate; scattered shots whose average hits the bullseye are accurate but not precise; a tight cluster on the bullseye is both; scattered shots all around miss both criteria.

  • Accuracy → how close the measurement is to the true value.
  • Precision → quality of measurement; says nothing about correctness.
  • These map directly to systematic errors (accuracy) and random errors (precision).

Sensitivity, Consistency, Range

Definition
Sensitivity
The ability of the measuring equipment to detect small variations in the quantity being measured. Higher accuracy demands higher sensitivity. The permitted degree of sensitivity determines the accuracy of the instrument.
Definition
Consistency
Successive readings of the measured quantity from the instrument are the same all the time.
Definition
Range
The difference between the lower and higher values the instrument can measure.

A highly accurate instrument possesses both sensitivity and consistency. Cost increases exponentially with accuracy — the Accuracy-vs-Cost curve drops off steeply.

1.4 Errors in Measurement

Definition
Error
The difference between the true value and the mean value of a set of readings on the same component — or equivalently, the difference between the indicated value and the true value.
Absolute & Relative Error E = Vm − Vt
Relative Error (%) = (E / Vt) × 100%

An error of ±2 g is negligible for a 1 kg measurement but huge for a 10 g measurement — this is why relative error matters more than absolute. Accuracy is always assessed in terms of error; lower error magnitude = more accurate instrument.

Systematic (Controllable) Errors

Deviate by a fixed amount from the true value. Controllable in both magnitude and direction. When systematic errors are minimum, the measurement is said to be extremely accurate.

Four sub-types:

  • Calibration errors — small variation from nominal in the length standards themselves (slip gauges, engraved scales). Caused also by inertia and hysteresis.
    Definition
    Hysteresis
    Difference between indications of the measuring instrument when the quantity is measured in ascending vs descending order.
  • Ambient conditions — must be maintained at the internationally accepted reference: temperature 20 °C, pressure 760 mmHg. A 1 °C rise increases the length of C25 steel by 0.3 µm — significant for precision work. Strain-gauge measurements include temperature compensation.
  • Workpiece deformation — elastic deformation under stylus pressure depends on applied load, area of contact, and material properties.
  • Avoidable errors — datum errors, reading errors, parallax error, misalignment, zero errors.

Random Errors

Provide a measure of random deviations when measurements are carried out repeatedly. Specific causes cannot be determined — they are unpredictable and uncontrollable and follow a normal (Gaussian) distribution when plotted.

Statistics of Random Errors v̄ = (v₁ + v₂ + … + vn) / n

σ = ± √[ Σ (v − v̄)² / n ]
Visualising the two together: Plot a normal distribution of trial readings. The horizontal distance from the curve's mean to the true value is the systematic error; the horizontal spread of the curve (the standard deviation) represents the random error.

1.5 Calibration

Definition
Calibration
A comparison between a known measurement (the standard) and the measurement obtained using your instrument. The accuracy of the standard should ideally be 10 × the accuracy of the device being tested.

An instrument should be calibrated:

  • According to the manufacturer's recommendation.
  • After any mechanical or electrical shock.
  • Periodically.

1.6 Limits, Fits & Tolerances

The 5 M's & Two Types of Variation

Manufacturing rests on five M's — Man, Machine, Materials, Money, Management. Variations in the first three drive process changes. All are subject to two kinds of variation:

Natural VariationCharacteristic Variation
Random in natureFrom identifiable, controllable causes
Cumulative effect of many small uncontrollable causesImproperly adjusted machines, operator error, tool wear, defective raw materials
Generally small — acceptable levelUsually large compared to natural variability
"common causes""assignable causes"

Eliminating assignable causes brings variability inside prescribed limits. This requires money (economy of inspection) and management action.

Definition
Tolerance
Because no component can be made precisely to a given dimension, the designer specifies two limits — upper (maximum) and lower (minimum) — between which the actual dimension must lie. The total permissible variation = tolerance.

Classification of Tolerance

  • Unilateral tolerance — distribution is on one side of the basic size only. Used in drilling, where hole dimensions tend to deviate in one direction (always oversize). Examples: 40+0.02+0.01, 40+0.02+0.00, 40−0.01−0.02.
  • Bilateral tolerance — distribution on both sides of the basic size, not necessarily equally disposed. Preferred in mass production where machines are set to the basic size. Example: 40 ± 0.02.
  • Compound tolerance — determined by tolerances on more than one dimension acting together. Should be avoided in practice.
  • Geometric tolerance — total amount the dimension of a manufactured part can vary; underlines the shape of a feature as against its size.

Geometric Tolerance — Three Families

  • Form tolerances (independent): straightness, circularity, flatness, cylindricity, line/surface profile.
  • Orientation tolerances (related): perpendicularity, parallelism, angularity.
  • Position tolerances (related): position, symmetry, concentricity, runout, total runout.

Geometric tolerance is read from a feature control frame: ⊚ Ø0.01 | X means "concentricity, with a 0.01 mm tolerance zone, datum X". So the two centres of a stepped cylinder must lie within Ø0.01 of each other, referenced to datum X.

Tolerance Accumulation

If three parts A (30+0.02−0.01), B (20+0.02−0.01) and C (10+0.02−0.01) are stacked:

  • Nominal L = 30 + 20 + 10 = 60 mm
  • Cumulative upper tolerance = +0.02 + 0.02 + 0.02 = +0.06
  • Cumulative lower tolerance = −0.01 − 0.01 − 0.01 = −0.03
  • Result: 60+0.06−0.03

To minimise this buildup, use progressive (baseline) dimensioning — dimension every feature from one common reference instead of chain dimensioning.

1.7 Shaft, Hole, MML & LML

Shaft designates all external features of a part (cylindrical or not). Hole designates all internal features (cylindrical or not).

Definition
Maximum & Minimum Metal Limits
MML = the limit at which the part has the maximum possible amount of metal. LML = the limit at which the part has the least amount of metal.

For a shaft: MML = upper limit (HLS), LML = lower limit (LLS).
For a hole: MML = lower limit (LLH), LML = upper limit (HLH).

Example: Shaft 40 ± 0.05 → MML = 40.05, LML = 39.95. Hole 45 ± 0.05 → MML = 44.95, LML = 45.05.

1.8 Fits

Definition
Fit
The relationship between two mating parts (hole and shaft) with respect to the difference in their dimensions before assembly.

Three basic types:

(a) Clearance Fit

  • Largest permissible diameter of shaft < diameter of smallest hole.
  • Always provides clearance; difference is always positive.
  • Small clearances → precise fit, assembled without tools.
  • Large clearances → relative motion possible (shaft rotating in a bush).

(b) Interference Fit

  • Minimum permissible diameter of shaft > maximum allowable diameter of hole.
  • A tight, essentially permanent fit; difference always negative.
  • Used where accurate location is critical (e.g., alignment of dowel pins).

(c) Transition Fit

  • Diameter of largest hole > diameter of smallest shaft, AND diameter of smallest hole < diameter of largest shaft.
  • Tolerance zones overlap — may give clearance OR interference depending on individual sizes.
HLH − LLS > 0 AND LLH − HLS > 0→ Clearance fit HLH − LLS < 0 AND LLH − HLS < 0→ Interference fit One positive, one negative→ Transition fit

1.9 Allowance

Definition
Allowance
The intentional difference between MML of hole and MML of shaft (i.e., LLH − HLS). It is the prescribed difference between the maximum-metal limits of the mating parts to obtain the desired fit.
Allowance Allowance = LLH − HLS
  • Positive allowance → clearance fit (minimum clearance).
  • Negative allowance → interference fit (maximum interference).

1.10 Hole-Basis vs Shaft-Basis System

Hole Basis

  • Hole size is kept constant; shaft varies to give different fits.
  • Fundamental (lower) deviation of hole = 0 → LLH = basic size.
  • Widely adopted in industry — varying shafts is cheaper than varying holes.
  • Standard drills/reamers can be re-used.

Shaft Basis

  • Shaft size is kept constant; hole varies.
  • Fundamental (upper) deviation of shaft = 0.
  • Not preferred — needs many sizes of reamers/broaches/gauges.
  • Used when a standard shaft fixes the rest (couplings, bearings, collars, gears, bushings).

1.11 ISO System of Limits & Fits

The ISO system comprises 18 grades of fundamental tolerance indicating the degree of manufacturing accuracy — designated IT01, IT0, IT1, IT2, … IT16. The higher the number, the coarser the tolerance.

Fundamental ToleranceTypical Applications
IT01 – IT4Production of gauges, plug gauges, measuring instruments
IT5 – IT7Precision engineering — ball bearings, grinding, fine boring, broaching
IT8 – IT11General engineering — turning, boring, milling, planing, rolling, extrusion, drilling, precision tube drawing
IT12 – IT14Sheet-metal working, press working
IT15 – IT16Casting, stamping, rubber moulding, general cutting, flame cutting

Standard Tolerance Unit

For grades IT5–IT16 the unit i (in µm) is computed from the basic size's diameter step:

Standard tolerance unit (µm) i = 0.453 ∛D + 0.001 D
where D = √(Dmax × Dmin) — geometric mean of the diameter step

Standard diameter steps (mm): 1–3, 3–6, 6–10, 10–18, 18–30, 30–50, 50–80, 80–120, 120–180, 180–250, 250–315, 315–400, 400–500, 500–630, 630–800, 800–1000.

Gauges, Linear & Angular Measurement, Surface Quality

2.1 Design of Gauges & Taylor's Principle

In mass production it is impractical to measure every part. Instead, gauges — scale-less inspection tools — quickly check whether a dimension lies inside the permissible limits. Gauges come in two sizes corresponding to MML and LML of the component, called GO and NOT-GO (or NO-GO) gauges.

Principle
Taylor's Principle
The GO gauge is designed to check maximum-metal conditions (LLH and HLS) and should simultaneously check as many related dimensions (roundness, size, location) as possible.

The NOT-GO gauge is designed to check minimum-metal conditions (HLH and LLS) and should check only one dimension at a time. A separate NOT-GO is required for each dimension.

During inspection the GO side should enter the hole / pass over the shaft under its own weight, with no undue force. The NOT-GO side should not enter / pass.

Key Design Notes

  • The form of GO gauges is a replica of the mating part's form.
  • GO gauges can check several related dimensions simultaneously → "complex gauges".
  • During inspection, GO gauges must be put into conditions of maximum impassability.
  • NOT-GO gauges check a single feature at a time.
  • NOT-GO gauges must be put into conditions of maximum passability.

2.2 Gauge Tolerance & Wear Allowance

Definition
Gauge Maker's Tolerance
The tolerance permitted in the manufacture of gauges themselves. Standard industry practice: gauge tolerance ≈ 10 % of the work tolerance. A wear allowance ≈ 10 % of the gauge tolerance is added to the GO gauge.

Why only the GO gauge gets a wear allowance? Because the GO gauge is the one repeatedly passing over or into the part — it wears. The NOT-GO ideally never enters at all, so it does not wear appreciably.

Direction of wear allowance:

  • For a plug (hole) gauge, the GO plug wears smaller as it slides in and out of holes; to compensate, it is initially made larger than LLH — wear allowance is added away from MML of hole (toward the HLH side).
  • For a snap (shaft) gauge, the GO snap wears larger as it slides over shafts; to compensate, it is initially made smaller than HLS — wear allowance is subtracted from HLS (toward the LLS side).

2.3 Linear Measurement — Line vs End Standards

Distances must be measured (i) between two lines, (ii) between two surfaces, or (iii) between a line and a surface.

Line Standard

Distance is the length between two engraved lines. Examples: yard, metre.

End Standard

Distance is the length between two flat parallel surfaces. Examples: slip gauges, micrometer anvils, Vernier caliper jaws.

Design Considerations for Linear Instruments

  • Accuracy depends on original accuracy of the line graduations — excessive thickness/poor definition reduces accuracy.
  • Any scale instrument is suspect unless it provides compensation against wear.
  • Attachments increase versatility but may add accumulated error.
  • Calipers depend on the feel of the user — training matters.
  • Principle of Alignment: the line of measurement and the line of the dimension being measured should be coincident.
  • Digital and dial versions improve readability; readability is itself a measure of reliability.
  • A micrometer has a finer least count (~0.01 mm) than a steel rule (~0.1 mm), so other things equal, it is more reliable — but it has smaller range.
  • Contact force should be optimum to avoid distortion.

Important Measuring Devices & Setups

  • Surface plate — hard, solid, horizontal flat plate used as the datum for inspection, marking-out, precision tooling set-up. Modern surface plates are granite rather than cast iron: not vulnerable to rust, non-magnetic, free from burrs (fine grain structure).
  • V-block — for inspection of cylindrical jobs. Made of high-grade steel, hardened above 60 Rc, ground to high precision. The job's axis is parallel to both base and sides of the V.
  • Depth gauge — measures holes, grooves, recesses. Has a blade (or rod) that drops to the bottom of the feature while the head rests on the reference surface.
  • Combination set — three devices: (1) combination square = square head + steel rule, (2) protractor head, (3) centre head. Uses: height/depth gauging, angle measurement, finding centres of round jobs.
  • Calipers (firm-joint) — inside, outside, transfer — used in three steps: open caliper to job, lock setting, then transfer to a steel rule.

2.4 Slip Gauges

Slip gauges (a.k.a. gauge blocks) are end standards that counter wear with hardened material and provide a very high degree of accuracy.

  • Made of hardened alloy steel — economical; coefficient of thermal expansion matches typical steel components.
  • Hardening makes them wear-resistant.
  • After hardening they are stabilised at a sub-zero temperature to relieve heat-treatment stresses.
  • Finishing produces a high degree of accuracy, flatness and surface finish on the measuring faces.
  • The block's height is engraved on a rectangular face, with a symbol indicating the two measured planes.
  • Most important requirements: length between measuring surfaces, flatness, and surface condition of the measuring faces.
  • Carbide gauge blocks exist for superior wear resistance and low thermal expansion — expensive, used where wear is severe.
  • Slip gauges are classified into grades by guaranteed accuracy — calibration, inspection, reference, etc.

Wringing — How a Stack Acts as One Block

Definition
Wringing
Phenomenon of adhesion of two flat smooth surfaces brought into close contact. The stack acts as a single block — high enough adhesion that even a sizable perpendicular pull cannot separate them.

Why does wringing work? Two combined factors:

  1. Atmospheric pressure (~ 1 bar) presses the blocks together from outside.
  2. Molecular adhesion at the surfaces — the lapping super-finishing process removes molecules at the molecular level, making the lapped surface highly receptive to the molecules of the mating surface.

The thin film of dirt/air between two clean wrung blocks has negligible thickness, so the stack length ≈ sum of individual heights.

Definition
Wringability
The ability of two surfaces to adhere tightly to each other in the absence of external means. Minimum requirements: surface finish ≤ 0.025 µm, flatness ≤ 0.13 µm.

Technique of Wringing (4 steps)

  1. Overlap gauging surfaces by about one-fourth of their length.
  2. Slide one block perpendicularly across the other with moderate pressure.
  3. Gently rotate one block until it is aligned with the other.
  4. The blocks are now wrung; lift them as a single piece.

2.5 Angular Measurement

Used for measuring angles of interchangeable parts, gears, jigs, fixtures; tapers of bores; flank angle and included angle of gears; also straightness/parallelism/flatness via instruments like the autocollimator (the reading is a measure of the error of alignment).

Devices, in order of increasing sophistication

(1) Simple Protractor — least count typically 1° (for small) or ½° (for large). Limited use in engineering metrology.

(2) Universal Bevel Protractor — main scale (degrees) + Vernier scale (minutes) + a rotatable blade; gives angle readings to within a few minutes of arc. Has an acute-angle attachment for awkward features.

(3) Sine Bar — uses the sine principle. The upper surface forms the hypotenuse of a triangle defined by two rollers separated by a known distance L. With slip gauges of height h under one roller on a flat datum:

Sine Bar sin θ = h / L

Limitation: not recommended for angles greater than 45° — any error in sine bar geometry or slip-gauge height gets magnified.

(4) Angle Gauges — built like slip gauges but to give angles. Made of high-grade wear-resistant steel; come as a standard set of blocks that can be wrung together to build an angle. The unique trick: angle gauges can be added and subtracted (a feature impossible with slip gauges), e.g., 30° block plus 5° = 35°; 30° minus 5° = 25°. So a small set of ~10 blocks can build many angles. Used for tool/die-room inspection and shop-floor setting (revolving centre of magnetic chuck, grinding-notch alignment, etc.).

(5) Autocollimator — optical instrument for measuring very small angles with extreme sensitivity. Used for precision alignment, detection of angular movement, verification of angle standards. A collimated light beam from inside the device strikes a plane reflector tilted by angle θ; the reflected beam is deflected by 2θ and focused onto a graduated reticle/eyepiece (or photodetector). The deflection between incident and reflected beams is the measure of the reflector's angular tilt.

2.6 Surface Quality Inspection

Real surfaces are never ideal — a succession of hills and valleys of varying height and spacing. Sources of these irregularities:

  • Feed marks from cutting tools.
  • Chatter marks from vibrations during machining.
  • Material rupture during cutting.
  • Workpiece deformation under cutting forces.
  • Machine-tool irregularities, e.g., lack of straightness in guideways.

Roughness produced by a single-point cutting tool is uniformly spaced and directional; finish-machined surfaces are irregular and non-directional.

Vocabulary of a Surface Profile

Definition
Roughness
The finer irregularities of the surface texture. Spacing = distance between successive peaks/ridges of the predominant pattern; height = arithmetic average deviation (µm) measured perpendicular to the centre line.
Definition
Waviness
More widely spaced component of surface texture, on which roughness is superimposed. Waviness is an error in form due to incorrect geometry of the tool producing the surface. Spacing = width between successive wave peaks/valleys; height = peak-to-valley distance.
Definition
Lay
The direction of the predominant surface pattern, determined by the production process used.
Definition
Surface Texture
The repetitive or random deviations from the nominal surface that form the surface pattern.

Three Quantitative Measures

Centre-Line Average (Ra) Ra = ( A₁ + A₂ + … + AN ) / L
— mean of areas (above and below mean-line, all taken positive), divided by sampling length
Root-Mean-Square (RMS) hRMS = √[ ( h₁² + h₂² + … ) / n ]
Ten-Point Height (Rz) Rz = ( (h₁+h₃+h₅+h₇+h₉) − (h₂+h₄+h₆+h₈+h₁₀) ) / 5 · (1000 / vertical magnification)
— sum of 5 highest peaks minus sum of 5 lowest valleys, divided by 5
Problem 2.2  Heights of 10 successive peaks & troughs from a datum: 5, 2, 6, 2, 7, 1, 6, 1, 6, 2 µm. Find Ra, RRMS and Rz.

RRMS Σh² = 25+4+36+4+49+1+36+1+36+4 = 196

Rz (treat odd-indexed as peaks, even as valleys) Peaks (h₁,h₃,h₅,h₇,h₉) = 5,6,7,6,6 → sum = 30
Valleys (h₂,h₄,h₆,h₈,h₁₀) = 2,2,1,1,2 → sum = 8

Two Approaches to Surface Finish Measurement

(a) Comparison

Simpler but subjective. Visual observation is deceptive — two surfaces that look identical may differ greatly. Touch is somewhat better but still subjective and operator-dependent.

(b) Direct (stylus) measurement

The stylus system is the most popular. A stylus drawn across the surface generates electrical signals proportional to the asperities. Output is recorded (hardcopy or digital) and quantified.

Optical Measurement & Interferometry

3.1 Why Optics?

Light waves provide the best standard for length. Optical measurement is simple, easy, accurate and reliable. Because optical instruments target precision measurement, the projected image must be clear, sharp and dimensionally accurate, and the mechanical/electronic controls must be compatible with the main optical system.

Essentials of an Optical Instrument

  • A light source.
  • A condensing/collimating lens system to direct light past the workpiece into the optical system.
  • A suitable stage or table for positioning the workpiece — preferably with motion in two directions and possibly rotation about a vertical axis.
  • The projection optics: lenses and mirrors.
  • A viewing screen or eyepiece.
  • Measuring & recording devices.

When two light waves interact, they produce interference. Instruments designed around it are interferometers. Interferometry allows extremely accurate comparison of surface geometry with a master (e.g., the optical flat) and is used with lasers for precision measurement.

Optical measurement has four principal application areas: magnification, alignment, interferometry, and as an absolute standard of length.

3.2 Tool Maker's Microscope (TMM)

A multifunctional shop-floor instrument that provides high magnification plus a convenient way to take readings — enabling both absolute and comparative measurements. Used to measure the shape, size, angle and position of small components within its range. Cross-wires are aligned with the measuring point to read coordinates.

Typical lenses (Mitutoyo TMM):

3.3 Optical Interference

A ray of light is composed of an infinite number of waves of equal wavelength; the wavelength determines the colour.

Superposition

  • Two rays with amplitudes yA and yB in phase → resultant amplitude yR = yA + yB.
  • Out of phase by α → yR = (yA + yB) cos (α / 2).
  • Phase shift = 180° with equal wavelength and amplitude → complete destructive interference — darkness.

One property of light: light from a single source can be split into two component rays. From two coherent point sources A and B, on a screen we observe alternating bright (O₁, O₃, …) and dark (O₂, O₄, …) fringes.

3.4 Interferometry

The number of fringes that appear in a given length on the screen is a measure of the distance between the two point sources — this is the basis for linear measurement. The technique is called interferometry; the instrument is an interferometer.

Optical-Flat Fringes

An optical flat (a precisely flat reference glass) is laid over a surface at a very small angle θ. Light reflecting off the two surfaces interferes, producing alternating dark/bright fringes. Each fringe represents one half-wavelength of height variation. The shape of the fringes reveals the surface condition:

Fringe patternSurface condition
A — straight, parallel, evenBlock is nearly flat along its length
B — fringes curve toward line of contactSurface is convex and high in the centre
C — fringes curve away from contactSurface is concave and low in the centre
D — straight on one end, becoming curvedSurface flat at one end but becoming convex
E — progressively sloped fringesSurface progressively lower toward bottom-left corner
F — two distinct contact pointsTwo points of contact higher than the rest

3.5 Laser Interferometer

Laser-based interferometers are increasingly popular in metrology. A laser can be represented by a sine wave whose wavelength is fixed for a given colour and whose amplitude is the intensity. From a measurement perspective, laser interferometry can handle both small diameters and large displacements.

Typical Layout

A laser source emits a beam to a beam-splitter P. Part goes to a fixed mirror Q, part to a moving retro-reflector R. The two returning beams recombine at a semi-reflector S, fall on photodiodes, are amplified, fed to a digital counter, which counts the fringe transitions caused by motion of R. Each fringe represents a known fraction of the laser wavelength, allowing nanometre-level resolution over metres of travel.

White-Light Interferometer

A specialised laser interferometer using a broad-spectrum source for 3-D topography measurement; outputs fringe patterns that reveal surface conditions across a wide range of materials and shapes.

Measurement systems used for automated production in the slides include CMMs (e.g., Bruker Alicona µCMM) and large-volume optical measuring stations used for inspecting whole car bodies.

4.1 What is Quality?

Working Definition
Quality
The ability to meet a customer's expectations. (Slides show how a "quality" item depends on context — a Mercedes, a piece of fruit, a washing machine, a smartphone, a tee-shirt, a defensive spike — quality is always defined by what the customer expected of it.)

4.2 What is Management?

Working Definition
Management
The act of getting people together to accomplish desired goals and objectives using available resources efficiently and effectively.

4.3 What is Quality Management?

Working Definition
Quality Management
A process that ensures the quality of the product throughout its life cycle.

The Four Pillars

  1. Quality Planning — the first step. Requirements are identified, a criterion is set, and procedures are recognised as part of the plan.
  2. Quality Control — review of product/service quality. Inspection and testing identify problems and defects needing correction.
  3. Quality Assurance — guarantees that defects/mistakes are prevented in manufacturing or service delivery; produces consistent results. (Assurance = prevention; Control = detection.)
  4. Quality Improvement — there is always room. Measure results; identify improvements; raise the bar.

4.4 Manufacturing the TOYOTA Way

Just-In-Time (JIT)

Originated at Toyota; adopted by McDonald's, Apple, etc. Materials and components arrive exactly when needed, eliminating storage inventory and waste.

Kanban (literally "sign-board")

A visual control system for production: cards or signals flow with goods to indicate state — Backlog → To-Do → Ongoing → Done, mirroring goods-in / goods-in-production / goods-out.

Kaizen (Continuous Improvement) & the 5 S's

Kaizen is the philosophy of small, continuous, structured improvements. The 5 S framework:

  • Seiri — sort: remove unnecessary items from the workspace.
  • Seiton — set in order: organize tools/materials for easy access.
  • Seiso — shine: clean the workspace to spot issues early.
  • Seiketsu — standardize: create routines to maintain the organization.
  • Shitsuke — sustain: build the habits that keep improvements going.

Other Kaizen pillars: elimination of waste, standardization, teamwork, self-discipline, improved morale, quality circles, improvement suggestions.

Statistical Quality Control & Control Charts

5.1 Performance Standards

An ideal output of a quality policy is a defect-free product that meets or exceeds customer needs. Continuous improvement is the only way to stay abreast of changing customer needs.

Although the ultimate goal may be zero defects, numerical standards for performance measurement should be avoided:

  • Setting unachievable numbers has an unintended negative emotional impact.
  • Not meeting the standard — even while progressing — demoralises everyone.
  • Numerical goals shift emphasis to the short term, sacrificing long-term benefits.

How then to measure performance?

  • By making continuous improvement in the goal and measuring the trend, not the absolute number.
  • Benchmarking — identify high-performance companies (or intra-company departments) and use their performance as the improvement target.

5.2 Six Sigma Quality

Process output is represented by a normal distribution. The Empirical Rule (68-95-99.7) covers ±1σ, ±2σ, ±3σ; further out the percentages stretch into the very long tail.

5.3 ISO 9000 Family

Without quality programs the global economy would not exist as it does — quality programs drive down costs and increase competitiveness. Total quality systems are no longer optional; they are required. ISO developed standards to support this:

  • ISO 9000 — Quality Management Systems: Fundamentals and Vocabulary.
  • ISO 9001 — Quality Management Systems: Requirements (revised 2015). Generic standard applicable to both manufacturing and service industries; non-applicable requirements may be omitted. Organisations may be certified and registered only to ISO 9001.
  • ISO 9004 — Quality Management Systems: Guidelines for Performance Improvements.

5.4 Statistical Techniques in Quality Control

Definition
Statistics
The science dealing with the collection, classification, analysis, and inference from data or information.

Two Big Branches

  • Descriptive statistics — describes the characteristics of a product or process from collected data.
  • Inferential statistics — draws conclusions about unknown population parameters from sample information.

Four Key Techniques

  • Hypothesis testing — evaluate a null hypothesis H₀ and an alternative H₁. Conclusion: reject H₀ or fail to reject H₀.
  • Regression analysis — find a mathematical expression for the functional relationship between one response and one or more independent variables.
  • Statistical Process Control (SPC) — monitor, control, and improve processes statistically. SPC identifies when processes are out of control due to special cause variation.
  • Design and Analysis of Experiments (DOE) — plan, conduct, analyse and interpret controlled tests to evaluate factors that may influence a response variable.

5.5 Statistical Process Control — Foundations

Definition
SPC
The use of statistical techniques to monitor a production process to detect and prevent poor quality.
  • Sample — a subset of items produced, used for inspection.
  • Control charts — show whether the process is within statistical control limits.

Two Kinds of Variation

Random (Common-cause)Non-Random (Special-cause)
Inherent in a processIdentifiable and correctable
Natural occurrencesExamples: equipment out of adjustment, defective materials, change in part/material, broken machinery, operator fatigue, poor work methods, lack of training

SPC contributes to the TQM goal of continuous improvement

Attribute vs Variable Data

  • Attribute — characteristic evaluated with a discrete response (good/bad, yes/no).
  • Variable — characteristic that is continuous and measurable (weight, length, diameter).

SPC in Services

Service defect = failure to meet customer requirements; monitor time and customer satisfaction.

  • Hospitals — timeliness of care, staff response, lab accuracy, cleanliness, courtesy, paperwork accuracy, speed of admission/checkout.
  • Grocery stores — wait time at checkout, out-of-stock frequency, food quality, cleanliness, complaints, register errors.
  • Airlines — flight delays, lost luggage, agent/attendant courtesy, accurate flight information, cabin cleanliness.
  • Fast food — service time, complaints, cleanliness, food quality, order accuracy, employee courtesy.
  • Catalog-order — order accuracy, operator knowledge/courtesy, packaging, delivery time, phone wait.
  • Insurance — billing accuracy, claims timeliness, agent availability/response.

Where to Use Control Charts

  • Process tends to go out of control.
  • Process is harmful/costly if it goes out of control.
  • At the beginning of a process — wasteful to start with bad supplies.
  • Before a costly or irreversible point — once past it, rework is hard.
  • Before/after assembly or painting operations that might cover defects.
  • Before the outgoing final product/service is delivered.

5.6 Anatomy of a Control Chart

A control chart is a graphical tool for monitoring an ongoing process. It has:

  • Centreline = process average.
  • Upper Control Limit (UCL) and Lower Control Limit (LCL) — usually at ±3σ.

A process is in control if:

  1. No sample points fall outside the limits.
  2. Most points are near the process average.
  3. About equal number of points above and below the centreline.
  4. Points appear randomly distributed (no patterns or runs).

Two Families

  • Attribute charts — p-chart (proportion defective), c-chart (count of defects per unit).
  • Variable charts — X̄-chart (sample mean), R-chart (sample range).

5.7 Attribute Control Charts

p-Chart (proportion of nonconforming items)

p-chart limits UCL = p̄ + z·σp
LCL = p̄ − z·σp

σp = √[ p̄(1 − p̄) / n ]
znumber of standard deviations (typically 3) p̄sample proportion defective; estimate of process average nsize of each sample
Problem 5.1 (p-chart)  25 samples of 50 containers each are inspected. Total nonconforming = 90. Construct a p-chart.
p̄ p̄ = 90 / (25 × 50) = 90 / 1250 = 0.072
σp σp = √(0.072 × 0.928 / 50) = √0.001336 = 0.0366
3σ limits UCL = 0.072 + 3 × 0.0366 = 0.072 + 0.1098 = 0.1818
LCL = 0.072 − 0.1098 = −0.0378 → 0 (proportion can't be negative)
p̄ = 0.072 · UCL = 0.1818 · LCL = 0

c-Chart (count of nonconformities per inspection unit)

Use when each unit can have multiple defects (a fabric, a casting). The defect count follows a Poisson distribution, so σc = √c̄.

c-chart limits UCL = c̄ + z · σc
LCL = c̄ − z · σc

σc = √c̄   (c̄ = mean number of defects per unit)
Problem 5.2 (c-chart)  25 fabric samples (100 m² each) have nonconformity counts summing to 189. Construct a c-chart.
c̄ c̄ = 189 / 25 = 7.56
σc σc = √7.56 = 2.75
3σ limits UCL = 7.56 + 3 × 2.75 = 7.56 + 8.25 = 15.81
LCL = 7.56 − 8.25 = −0.69 → 0
c̄ = 7.56 · UCL = 15.81 · LCL = 0

5.8 Variable Control Charts

Variables are measurable on a numerical scale (length, thickness, diameter, breaking strength, temperature). We need to control both:

  • The mean value of the quality characteristic — the central tendency — using the X̄-chart.
  • The variability — the process dispersion — using the R-chart.

Variables provide more information than attributes.

R = Xmax − Xmin  (sample range)

Interpretation of Shifts

  • A shift in the process mean (from µ₀ to µ₁) is detected by the X̄-chart.
  • A change in process dispersion (σ₀ → σ₁) is detected by the R-chart.

5.9 Process Capability Analysis

Once the process is statistically in control, capability tells you whether it can meet the customer's specifications.

Definition
Process Capability
The variation in the product quality characteristic that remains after removal of all special causes. It measures the inherent ability of the (in-control) process to meet customer expectations.

Convention: process spread = 6σ, covering 99.74 % of a normal distribution's values. Compare this spread to the specification width (tolerance T = USL − LSL).

Process Capability Index (Cp) Cp = 2T / 6σ = T / 3σ

Industry benchmarks for Cp:

  • 1.00 — typical USA acceptance level.
  • 1.33 — typical Japan acceptance level.
  • 1.66 — Japanese watch-making industry; pushing toward 2.0.

As Cp rises, defect rate falls — moving from total inspection at Cp ≈ 1 to "no defect" zones at Cp ≥ 5.

5.10 Measurement System Analysis (MSA)

If decisions are based on measurements, then errors in measurements lead to errors in decisions. MSA qualifies a measurement system by quantifying its accuracy, precision, and stability.

Accuracy vs Precision (revisited as dart-pattern)

  • Accurate & Precise — tight cluster centred on the true value (ideal).
  • Accurate, not precise — wide spread but mean ≈ true value.
  • Not accurate, precise — tight cluster offset from true value.
  • Not accurate, not precise — scattered everywhere.

Efforts to improve a measurement system aim at improving both accuracy and precision.

Requirements of a Good Measurement System

  • Statistical stability over time.
  • Variability small compared to the process variability.
  • Variability small compared to the specification limits (tolerance).
  • Resolution of the measuring device must be small relative to the smaller of (specification tolerance, process spread).

5.11 Acceptance Sampling Plan

An acceptance sampling plan classifies a product as conforming/non-conforming (attribute) — or measures the degree of conformance numerically (variable) — based on a sample of items, then decides whether to accept or reject the whole lot.

  • The terms defect / defective are used interchangeably with nonconformity / nonconforming item in many plans.
  • Applied at incoming raw materials, in-process stages, or final product/service inspection.
  • Used between companies and vendors, between manufacturers and customers, or even between departments inside one company.

What it does NOT do

  • Acceptance sampling does not control or improve the quality level of the process.
  • Because of sampling variability, plans will accept some lots and reject others of the same true quality.
  • The only real route to improvement is process control & improvement.

Why use it then?

  • If inspection is destructive, 100 % inspection is impossible.
  • Sampling is economical and causes less handling damage.
  • Provides strong motivation to improve quality — a rejected lot is a serious cost signal.

5.12 Reliability Assessment of Systems

Definition
Reliability
The ability of a product to function effectively over a certain period under certain conditions. It is closely related to quality of performance; higher reliability → greater customer satisfaction.

Reliability & Life Tests

  • Plans are usually destructive.
  • Involve observing items until a preset number of failures occur, or for a preset time, or both.
  • Usually done at the prototype stage — can be expensive depending on unit cost.
  • Longer accumulated test time → more precise estimate of reliability or mean life — but cost is a constraint.
  • Testing is done under simulated conditions that should mimic actual operating conditions as closely as possible.

Three Types of Reliability Tests

  • Failure-Terminated Test — terminates when a preassigned number of failures occur in the sample.
  • Time-Terminated Test — terminates when a preassigned time is reached.
  • Sequential Reliability Test — no prior decision on number of failures or duration; accumulating results drive the decision to accept the lot, reject the lot, or continue testing.

▷ Q1 — Tolerance Stack Three blocks A (40+0.03−0.01), B (25+0.02−0.01), C (15+0.01−0.00) are stacked. Find the dimension of the total assembly.
Answer Nominal L = 40 + 25 + 15 = 80. Upper tol = +0.03 + 0.02 + 0.01 = +0.06. Lower tol = −0.01 − 0.01 + 0.00 = −0.02. So 80+0.06−0.02 mm.
▷ Q2 — Allowance & Fit Type For Hole = 25+0.021+0.000 mm, Shaft = 25−0.020−0.041 mm, find allowance and identify the fit.
Answer HLH = 25.021, LLH = 25.000, HLS = 24.980, LLS = 24.959. Allowance = LLH − HLS = 25.000 − 24.980 = +0.020 mm. Min clear = 0.020, max clear = 0.062 — both positive → Clearance fit.
▷ Q3 — Surface Roughness A surface profile has peak/valley sequence 4, 1, 5, 0, 6, 1, 5, 2, 4, 2 (in µm). Find Ra, RMS and Rz.
Answer Σ = 30 → Ra = 3.0 µm.
Σh² = 16+1+25+0+36+1+25+4+16+4 = 128 → RMS = √(128/10) = √12.8 = 3.58 µm.
Peaks (odd) = 4+5+6+5+4 = 24, Valleys (even) = 1+0+1+2+2 = 6, Rz = (24−6)/5 = 3.6 µm.
▷ Q4 — Sine Bar A sine bar of length 200 mm is used; the slip gauges placed under one roller stack to 50 mm. Find the angle measured.
Answer sin θ = h / L = 50/200 = 0.25 → θ = sin⁻¹(0.25) ≈ 14.48°.
▷ Q5 — p-Chart Forty samples of n = 100 are inspected; total defectives = 200. Compute UCL and LCL of the p-chart at 3σ.
Answer p̄ = 200 / (40 × 100) = 0.05. σp = √(0.05 × 0.95 / 100) = √0.000475 = 0.02179.
UCL = 0.05 + 3(0.02179) = 0.1154. LCL = 0.05 − 0.0654 = negative → 0.
▷ Q6 — X̄ / R Chart Limits With n = 4, X̿ = 100 mm and R̄ = 5 mm, compute the 3σ control limits.
Answer For n = 4: A₂ = 0.729, D₃ = 0, D₄ = 2.282.
UCLX̄ = 100 + 0.729 × 5 = 103.65.
LCLX̄ = 100 − 3.645 = 96.36.
UCLR = 2.282 × 5 = 11.41.
LCLR = 0.
▷ Q7 — Process Capability Specification width is 0.060 mm and process σ = 0.008 mm. Compute Cp and judge against US/Japan benchmarks.
Answer T = 0.060 mm; Cp = T / (6σ) = 0.060 / (6 × 0.008) = 0.060 / 0.048 = 1.25.
Above US baseline of 1.00 but below Japanese baseline of 1.33 — capable but not yet at Japanese standard.

Survival Strategy for the Exam

  • Numerical problems first — gauges, fits, tolerances, control charts. They give the most marks per minute.
  • For 40 H8/d9–style problems: write D = √(Dmax·Dmin) → compute i → multiply by IT factor → lookup deviation → draw the schematic to scale.
  • For gauge design: remember 10 % rule twice (tolerance, then wear on GO only) and direction of wear (away from MML).
  • For control charts: identify type of data (attribute vs variable) before picking the chart; always check whether LCL clips to 0.
  • For theory: definitions are quick marks — write them word-for-word from the slides.
  • If asked about accuracy vs precision, draw the dartboard. If asked about random vs systematic, draw the Gaussian with the offset mean.
