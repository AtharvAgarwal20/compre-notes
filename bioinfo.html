Part 01

Foundations: What is Phylogenetics, and why do we care?

Welcome, students. By the end of this lecture, you will not only understand every concept in your slides but you'll also be able to solve every phylogenetics question that has appeared in your previous comprehensive exams. Let me teach you this the way I would teach my own students preparing for the final.

Phylogenetics is the study of evolutionary relationships among organisms, genes, or proteins. When we use molecular data (DNA, RNA, protein sequences) to infer these relationships, we call it molecular phylogenetics. The output is a phylogenetic tree — a branching diagram that visually represents how organisms or sequences are related through common ancestry.

Why does this matter? Several reasons that show up in exams and real research:

  1. Finding closest relatives — Which species shares the most recent common ancestor with humans? (Spoiler: chimps and bonobos, not gorillas.)
  2. Identifying orthologs vs paralogs — critical for predicting gene function across species.
  3. Tracing gene origins — mutations, duplications, gain/loss of function.
  4. Epidemiology — Tracking how HIV, influenza, SARS-CoV-2 spread and mutate.
  5. Vaccine design — Predicting viral evolution to develop future-proof vaccines.

Types of homologous genes — memorize these distinctions

  • Orthologs: Genes separated by a speciation event. One ancestor → two species → each species carries a version of that gene. These typically retain the same function across species.
  • Paralogs: Genes that arose from gene duplication within a genome. The two copies diverge and often acquire new functions (think α-globin and β-globin in humans).
  • Xenologs: Genes acquired via horizontal/lateral gene transfer from another organism (common in bacteria, e.g., antibiotic resistance genes).
⚑ Exam tip
This is high-yield because exam questions love to test whether you can distinguish ortholog vs paralog vs xenolog. The trigger words are speciation, duplication, and horizontal transfer respectively.
Part 02

Anatomy of a Phylogenetic Tree

Look at a tree and you'll see these components:

  • Terminal nodes (leaves/tips) — represent taxa or OTUs (Operational Taxonomic Units). These are the things you're comparing: species, genes, populations.
  • Internal nodes — represent hypothetical common ancestors (divergence points).
  • Branches (lineages) — connect nodes; may or may not have meaningful length.
  • Root — the most ancient common ancestor of everything in the tree.
  • Topology — the branching pattern.
  • Branch length — depending on tree type, may represent genetic change, time, or nothing at all.

Newick notation

Trees are often written as nested parentheses. For example: ((A,(B,C)),(D,E)) means B and C cluster together, then A joins them, and D and E form a separate clade.

★ Critical interpretive rule
The vertical spacing between taxa or the top-to-bottom order means nothing. Only the branching pattern matters. A tree can be rotated at any node without changing its meaning — just like a mobile hanging from a ceiling.
Part 03

Types of Trees — know the differences cold

Tree type Branch length means Use
Cladogram Nothing (just topology) Show ancestor-descendant relationships only
Phylogram (additive tree) Amount of genetic change Most common in molecular phylogenetics
Chronogram (ultrametric) Time (e.g., million years) All tips align at present
Dendrogram Generic term — any tree Umbrella term

Rooted vs Unrooted trees — this is exam gold

  • Rooted tree: Has a defined common ancestor and shows direction of evolution. You can read ancestral vs derived states.
  • Unrooted tree: Shows only relationships, not direction. You cannot say who came first.

How to root a tree

  1. Outgroup method (preferred): Include a taxon known to fall outside your group of interest. Example: use birds to root a mammalian tree, or α-globins to root β-globins.
  2. Midpoint method: Place the root at the midpoint of the longest path between two taxa. Assumes a molecular clock (constant evolution rate) — often an unrealistic assumption.

The combinatorial explosion — memorize these formulas

Counting Trees
Number of unrooted trees for N taxa = $(2N - 5)!!$

Number of rooted trees for N taxa = $(2N - 3)!!$

(The "!!" is the double factorial: multiply every other integer down to 1.)

For N = 4: unrooted = 3, rooted = 15. For N = 10: unrooted = 2,027,025. For N = 30: ~3.58 × 10³⁶. This is why exhaustive search is impossible for large datasets and we need heuristic methods.

Part 04

Tree-Building Methods — The Big Picture

Tree-building methods split into two philosophical camps:

Distance-based methods

  • Compress sequence data into a pairwise distance matrix.
  • Build the tree from distances only.
  • Pros: Conceptually simple, computationally cheap, handles large datasets.
  • Cons: Information is lost during compression; less accurate.
  • Examples: UPGMA, Neighbor-Joining.

Character-based methods

  • Use every alignment column (site) directly.
  • Evaluate how characters change across the tree.
  • Pros: More accurate, no information loss.
  • Cons: Computationally expensive, slower.
  • Examples: Maximum Parsimony, Maximum Likelihood.

The decision flowchart (memorize this!)

  1. Strong sequence similarity? → Maximum Parsimony
  2. Otherwise, clearly recognizable similarity? → Distance methods
  3. Otherwise (highly diverged sequences)? → Maximum Likelihood
  4. Always finish with validation (bootstrapping).
Part 05

Maximum Parsimony — Detailed Walkthrough

Principle: The best tree is the one requiring the fewest evolutionary changes (Occam's razor applied to evolution).

Assumptions:

  • Evolutionary events are rare.
  • Events occur independently in different lineages.

Drawbacks:

  • Computationally expensive (must evaluate many trees).
  • Fails when sequence variation is high (long-branch attraction problem).

Informative vs Uninformative Sites — Critical Concept

Definition
A site is informative if:
1. It has at least two different residues, AND
2. Each different residue appears in at least two taxa.

Only informative sites help discriminate between trees. Constant sites and singleton variants (where only one taxon differs) are uninformative.

Worked example from slides

Identifying informative sites

For the alignment:

         1 2 3 4 5 6
human    a g t c t c
chimp    a g a g t c
gorilla  c g g c a g
orang    c g g g a c
  • Site 1: a,a,c,c → two residues, each in ≥2 taxa → informative
  • Site 2: all g → constant → uninformative
  • Site 3: t,a,g,g → g appears twice but t and a are singletons → uninformative
  • Site 4: c,g,c,g → informative
  • Site 5: t,t,a,a → informative
  • Site 6: c,c,g,c → g is a singleton → uninformative

For each informative site, count changes on each of the three possible trees, then sum. The tree with the minimum total changes is the most parsimonious.

Part 06

Distance Methods — Building the Matrix

Step 1: Build the distance matrix

Count differences between each sequence pair. Example:

Human: aactc
Chimp: aagtc
Orang: tagtt
  • Human vs Chimp: 1 difference
  • Human vs Orang: 3 differences
  • Chimp vs Orang: 2 differences

Step 2: Correct for multiple substitutions

Raw counts underestimate true distance because the same site can mutate multiple times (back-mutations, parallel mutations). We use evolutionary models:

Jukes-Cantor Model (all substitutions equally likely)
$$D = -\frac{3}{4}\ln\left(1 - \frac{4}{3}D_s\right)$$

Where D = corrected distance, Ds = raw difference fraction.

Other models: Kimura (separates transitions vs transversions), Felsenstein (incorporates base frequencies).

Part 07

UPGMA — Step-by-Step Algorithm

UPGMA = Unweighted Pair Group Method with Arithmetic Mean

Key assumption: Constant rate of evolution across all branches (molecular clock). This produces an ultrametric, rooted tree.

Algorithm

  1. Each taxon starts as its own cluster.
  2. Find the two closest clusters in the distance matrix; merge them.
  3. Place them at height = distance/2 on the tree.
  4. Recompute distances from the new cluster to all others using the arithmetic mean.
  5. Repeat until only one cluster remains.
Worked example from slides

UPGMA on the 5×5 distance matrix

Starting matrix:

A      0
B     20    0
C     60   50    0
D    100   90   40    0
E     90   80   50   30    0

Smallest distance = 20 (A,B). Merge → cluster AB. Height = 20/2 = 10 from tips.

Step 2 — Recompute distances

  • AB to D = (100+90)/2 = 95
  • AB to E = (90+80)/2 = 85

New matrix:

       AB    C    D    E
AB     0
C     55    0
D     95   40    0
E     85   50   30    0
Step 3

Smallest = 30 (D,E). Merge → DE. Height = 30/2 = 15 from tips.

Step 4 — Recompute
  • AB to DE = (95+85)/2 = 90
  • C to DE = (40+50)/2 = 45
  • C to AB unchanged = 55
       AB    C    DE
AB     0
C     55    0
DE    90   45    0
Step 5

Smallest = 45 (C, DE). Merge → CDE. Height = 45/2 = 22.5. This means C joins DE at height 22.5; the branch from CDE node down to DE node is 22.5 − 15 = 7.5.

Step 6

AB to CDE = (55+90)/2 = 72.5. Final merge at height 72.5/2 = 36.25.

Final branch lengths

  • D and E tips to DE node: 15 each
  • DE node to CDE node: 7.5
  • C tip to CDE node: 22.5
  • AB node to root: 26.25
  • CDE node to root: 13.75

Drawbacks of UPGMA

  1. Assumes constant evolution rate (rarely true).
  2. Irreversible clustering — once joined, taxa can't be re-grouped.
  3. Less accurate than modern methods like NJ or ML.

Part 08

Neighbor-Joining — The Better Distance Method

Key advantages over UPGMA:

  • Does NOT assume a molecular clock — allows unequal evolution rates on different branches.
  • Produces an unrooted tree.
  • More accurate, widely used.

Core idea

UPGMA picks the closest pair. NJ asks: which pair is close to each other AND far from everyone else? This corrects for situations where two taxa look close just because they're both far from the rest.

Algorithm

  1. Start with a star tree (all OTUs from one central node).
  2. For each OTU i, compute: $S_i = \frac{\sum_{k} D_{ik}}{N-2}$
  3. Find the pair (i, j) that minimizes $M_{ij} = D_{ij} - S_i - S_j$.
  4. Join i and j into a new node x. Compute branch lengths:
    • $D_{xi} = 0.5 \cdot D_{ij} + 0.5(S_i - S_j)$
    • $D_{xj} = 0.5 \cdot D_{ij} + 0.5(S_j - S_i)$
  5. Compute distances from new node x to all remaining taxa k: $$D_{xk} = 0.5(D_{ik} + D_{jk} - D_{ij})$$
  6. Repeat until all OTUs are joined.

This minimizes the total branch length of the tree — it's a smart greedy algorithm.

Part 09

Maximum Likelihood — The Probabilistic Champion

Principle: Given the observed data (your MSA), find the tree (and model parameters) that make the data most probable.

$$\text{Likelihood} = P(\text{Data} \mid \text{Model})$$

The "Model" includes: tree topology, branch lengths, nucleotide frequencies (πA, πC, πG, πT), and the substitution rate matrix.

Coin-toss intuition

If you toss a coin 10 times and get 7 heads, what's the best estimate of P(heads)? You try different values of p, compute P(7 heads | p) for each, and find the p that maximizes it. Answer: p = 0.7. That's maximum likelihood in its simplest form.

Coin-toss probability
$$P(X = h) = \binom{n}{h}p^h(1-p)^{n-h}$$

How ML works for phylogeny

  1. Propose a tree topology and parameter values.
  2. For each alignment column, sum over all possible ancestral states at internal nodes (Felsenstein's pruning algorithm).
  3. Multiply the per-column probabilities (assuming columns are independent) to get the alignment likelihood.
  4. Because probabilities get tiny, work with log-likelihoods: $$\ln(L) = \sum_{j=1}^{N} \ln(L_j)$$
  5. Adjust parameters to climb toward maximum likelihood.
  6. Repeat with different starting points.
⚑ Crucial limitation
ML can get stuck on local maxima — it doesn't guarantee finding the global maximum likelihood. That's why you should run ML multiple times with different starting trees.

Assumptions

  • Each column is an independent observation.
  • Columns contain homologous nucleotides.
  • All sequences share a common ancestor.
Part 10

Bootstrapping — Validating Your Tree

You've built a tree. How do you know if you can trust it? Bootstrapping is the answer.

Algorithm

  1. Resample columns from your alignment with replacement to create a pseudo-alignment of the same length.
  2. Build a tree from this pseudo-alignment using your chosen method.
  3. Repeat N times (typically 100, 500, 1000, or 10,000).
  4. For each clade in your original tree, count what % of bootstrap trees also contain that clade.

Interpretation

Bootstrap value Interpretation
> 90% Strong support
70–90% Moderate support
< 70% Weak / unreliable

What bootstrapping DOES tell you

  • Stability of grouping under data resampling.
  • Sensitivity to which columns happened to be in your data.

What bootstrapping does NOT tell you

  • The probability that a clade is biologically true.
  • Whether your evolutionary model is correct.

Why columns, not sequences?

Because columns are treated as independent samples of evolutionary history. Resampling sequences would change which taxa are in the tree entirely.

Part 11

Clades and Monophyletic Groups

A clade = a common ancestor + ALL its descendants. Such a group is called monophyletic.

Clades are biologically meaningful because they reflect actual evolutionary history. Groups that exclude some descendants (paraphyletic) or include unrelated members (polyphyletic) do not.

“A clade is a piece of the tree of life — branch it where you will, nothing inside should be left out.”

Inference about Modern Human (1 mark)

The Modern Human sequence (MH) is vastly more divergent (~20 substitutions) from all four ancient species (H1–H4) than they are from each other (1–8 substitutions). This places MH on a deep, long branch separating it from the ancient human clade. The inference is that MH is the outgroup — it diverged earliest from the common ancestor, OR it represents a distinct lineage that evolved independently for a long period. Importantly, H3 and H4 are nearly identical (1 substitution apart) and form the closest pair, suggesting they are the most recently diverged from each other.

A more biologically subtle inference: if these are truly "ancient human species" and MH is modern, the data suggests MH has accumulated many independent changes (or, alternatively, the ancient species themselves share a tighter common ancestry not shared with MH).

Question 2(a)(ii): UPGMA vs NJ — conceptual differences and which is better

Conceptual differences:

Feature UPGMA Neighbor-Joining
Assumption Constant evolution rate (molecular clock) No clock assumption — unequal rates allowed
Tree type Rooted, ultrametric Unrooted
Selection criterion Closest pair (minimum distance) Pair close to each other AND far from others (minimize $M_{ij} = D_{ij} - S_i - S_j$)
Branch lengths Forced to be equal from each merge point Free to vary
Accuracy Lower; biased when rates vary Higher; widely used

Which is better and why?
NJ is generally better because real biological sequences rarely evolve at a constant rate across all lineages. When rates differ (e.g., a fast-evolving virus vs a slow-evolving host gene), UPGMA produces wrong topologies, particularly grouping fast-evolving lineages incorrectly. NJ accommodates rate variation, minimizes total branch length, and is computationally efficient, making it the standard for distance-based phylogenetics.

Question 2(a)(iii): Unrooted version of UPGMA tree

Take the rooted UPGMA tree, remove the root, and draw it as a network. The unrooted version has the same topology but no directionality:

MH ────────────────┐ ├─── (internal node) ────┐ H2 ──────────────┐ │ ├─── (internal node) ──────┘ H1 ──┐ ├─── (internal node) ───┐ H3 ┐ │ │ ├─┘ H4 ┘

In Newick: (MH, H2, (H1, (H3, H4))) — placed as an unrooted tree with five tips. The root would be on the branch connecting MH to the rest (since MH is the most distant taxon and acts effectively as the outgroup).

Question 2(b): True/false on the gibbon-orangutan-gorilla-bonobo tree

The tree (left to right at tips): gibbons, orangutan, gorilla, bonobo. The branching pattern is a typical pectinate (ladder) tree where gibbons branch off first, then orangutan, then gorilla, then bonobo as the most recent.

(i) Gorilla is more closely related to bonobo than to orangutan — TRUE. Gorilla and bonobo share a more recent common ancestor (the (gorilla, bonobo) node) than gorilla and orangutan, which share the deeper (orangutan, (gorilla, bonobo)) node.

(ii) Orangutan is equally related to gorilla and bonobo — TRUE. Orangutan branches off before gorilla and bonobo diverge from each other. So orangutan's common ancestor with gorilla is the SAME node as its common ancestor with bonobo. Equal relatedness.

(iii) Orangutan is equally related to gorilla and gibbon — FALSE. Orangutan and gorilla share a more recent common ancestor (the (orangutan, (gorilla, bonobo)) node) than orangutan and gibbon (the root). Orangutan is more closely related to gorilla.

(iv) Gibbon is more closely related to orangutan than to bonobo — FALSE. Gibbon is the outgroup — its common ancestor with EVERY other taxon (orangutan, gorilla, bonobo) is the same root node. Gibbon is equally related to all of them.

Exam 1 · Q4 Two trees from glutamate synthatase gene of 5 organisms

The researcher has two trees: an unrooted tree (left) and a rooted phylogram (right).

4(a)(i) — Methods to generate these trees and justify (2 marks)

Unrooted tree (left): This is characteristic of Neighbor-Joining or Maximum Parsimony or Maximum Likelihood output without an explicit outgroup. NJ is the most likely candidate because: (a) it produces unrooted trees by default, (b) branch lengths are unequal (no molecular clock), and (c) it's the standard fast distance method for moderate-sized datasets.

Rooted phylogram (right): This has clear hierarchical structure with branch lengths. This could be produced by: (a) UPGMA (always rooted), (b) NJ followed by outgroup rooting, or (c) Maximum Likelihood with outgroup rooting. Given the simple ultrametric-looking appearance, UPGMA is a strong candidate. If branch lengths visibly vary, it's NJ rooted with an outgroup.

4(a)(ii) — Information from each tree (3 marks)

Unrooted tree
  • Shows evolutionary relationships among the five organisms.
  • Branch lengths show amount of genetic change along each lineage.
  • Reveals which species cluster together — for example, plant species (rice, arabidopsis) likely cluster, photosynthetic prokaryotes (spirulina, synechocystis) cluster, and yeast is on its own branch.
  • Does NOT indicate the direction of evolution or which species diverged first.
Rooted tree
  • Provides the direction of evolution with yeast as outgroup (placed at the root).
  • Tells us ancestral vs derived states.
  • Reveals the order of divergence: yeast diverged first → cyanobacteria (spirulina, synechocystis) → land plants (arabidopsis, rice).
  • Allows us to infer the most recent common ancestor between any two taxa.
  • Branch lengths still inform genetic change but can also approximate divergence time if a molecular clock holds.

4(a)(iii) — How to estimate reliability (1 mark)

Bootstrap analysis. Resample alignment columns with replacement (e.g., 1000 replicates), rebuild trees, and count the percentage of replicates supporting each clade. Bootstrap values >70% indicate moderate support; >90% indicates strong support. Other validation approaches include jackknifing, Bayesian posterior probabilities, or comparing trees built by different methods (NJ vs ML vs MP) for consensus.

3(a) — All possible rooted trees (2 marks)

With an outgroup defined, the ingroup is A, B, C. The number of rooted trees for 3 ingroup taxa with a designated outgroup = (2N-3)!! where N=3 → 3!! = 3. But because the outgroup roots the tree, we effectively have three distinct ingroup topologies, each rooted between the outgroup and the rest:

  • Tree 1: ((A,B),C) with Out as outgroup → (Out, ((A,B), C))
  • Tree 2: ((A,C),B) with Out as outgroup → (Out, ((A,C), B))
  • Tree 3: ((B,C),A) with Out as outgroup → (Out, ((B,C), A))

Visually:

Tree 1: Tree 2: Tree 3: Out Out Out | | | ──┴── ──┴── ──┴── | | | | | | C ─┴─ B ─┴─ A ─┴─ | | | | | | A B A C B C

3(b) — Informative sites (1 mark)

A site is informative if it has ≥2 character states, each present in ≥2 taxa.

  • Site 1: 0,0,1,0 (Out=0, A=0, B=1, C=0) → "1" only in B (1 taxon). Uninformative (autapomorphy).
  • Site 2: 0,1,1,0 → "0" in Out and C (2 taxa), "1" in A and B (2 taxa). INFORMATIVE ✓
  • Site 3: 0,0,0,1 → "1" only in C. Uninformative.
  • Site 4: 0,0,1,1 → "0" in Out and A (2 taxa), "1" in B and C (2 taxa). INFORMATIVE ✓
  • Site 5: 0,1,1,0 → same pattern as site 2: "0" in Out and C, "1" in A and B. INFORMATIVE ✓
  • Site 6: 0,1,1,0 → same pattern: "0" in Out and C, "1" in A and B. INFORMATIVE ✓

Informative sites: 2, 4, 5, 6 (four total).

3(c) — Most parsimonious tree (4 marks)

For each tree, count the minimum changes required at each informative site, then sum.

Sites 2, 5, and 6 all share the same pattern: Out=0, A=1, B=1, C=0. This pattern groups A and B together.

Site 4 pattern: Out=0, A=0, B=1, C=1. This groups B and C together.

Let's count steps on each tree:

TREE 1: ((A,B), C) with Out as outgroup

Site 2 (Out=0, A=1, B=1, C=0): A and B share state "1" and are sisters. Ancestor of (A,B) is "1", change from 0→1 on this branch = 1 change.

Site 4 (Out=0, A=0, B=1, C=1): A and B are sisters but have different states (0 and 1). C has state "1". Most parsimonious: 0→1 on branch to B AND 0→1 on branch to C = 2 changes.

Sites 5 and 6: Same as site 2 → 1 change each.

Total for Tree 1 = 1 + 2 + 1 + 1 = 5 changes

TREE 2: ((A,C), B) with Out as outgroup

Site 2 (A=1, C=0, B=1): A and C are sisters but A=1, C=0. B=1. Most parsimonious: 0→1 on branch to A AND 0→1 on branch to B = 2 changes.

Site 4 (A=0, C=1, B=1): A=0, C=1, B=1. 0→1 on branch to C and 0→1 on branch to B = 2 changes.

Sites 5 and 6: Same as site 2 → 2 changes each.

Total for Tree 2 = 2 + 2 + 2 + 2 = 8 changes

TREE 3: ((B,C), A) with Out as outgroup

Site 2 (B=1, C=0, A=1): B and C sisters but differ. A=1. 0→1 on B and 0→1 on A = 2 changes.

Site 4 (B=1, C=1, A=0): B and C both =1, sisters. Ancestor of (B,C) = 1, single change 0→1 on that branch = 1 change.

Sites 5 and 6: Same as site 2 → 2 changes each.

3(d) — Does the human tree figure indicate humans are most complex/more evolved? (2 marks)

NO. This is a common misconception. A phylogenetic tree shows evolutionary relationships, not progress or hierarchy. Several reasons:

  1. All extant species are equally "evolved" — every living organism today represents the tip of a lineage that has been evolving for exactly the same amount of time since the last common ancestor (LCA). Bacteria are as "evolved" as humans.
  2. Trees can be rotated at any internal node without changing meaning. The convention of placing humans at the right side of a tree is artistic, not biological. We could equally well draw the same tree with a fish at the rightmost tip.
  3. "Complexity" is not directional in evolution. Many lineages have undergone reductive evolution (loss of features) — parasites often lose organs, cave-dwelling species lose eyes. Evolution does not have a goal of increasing complexity.
  4. The tree shows branching order, not progress. The figure suggests a linear "ladder" toward humans, which echoes the discredited scala naturae (great chain of being). Modern evolutionary biology rejects this view: evolution is a branching bush, not a ladder.
  5. Each branch is independently adapted to its own niche. Humans are well-adapted to our environment; sharks are exquisitely adapted to theirs. There's no objective scale on which one is "more evolved" than the other.

Key Takeaways — Exam Survival Guide

If I had to compress everything into a survival guide for your comprehensive exam, here's what I'd want you to know cold:

Conceptual must-knows

Difference between rooted vs unrooted trees, what informative sites are, the distinction between distance-based vs character-based methods, what bootstrapping does (and doesn't) tell you, and why all extant species are equally "evolved."

Algorithmic must-knows

How to compute a distance matrix from an MSA, how to run UPGMA step-by-step (smallest distance → merge → recompute as arithmetic mean → repeat), how parsimony counts changes on competing trees, and the core formulas:

Core formulas
Jukes-Cantor: $D = -\frac{3}{4}\ln(1 - \frac{4}{3}D_s)$

NJ pair selection: $M_{ij} = D_{ij} - S_i - S_j$

NJ branch length: $D_{xi} = 0.5 D_{ij} + 0.5(S_i - S_j)$

Unrooted trees: $(2N-5)!!$  ·  Rooted trees: $(2N-3)!!$

Interpretation must-knows

How to read a tree (clades, sister relationships, outgroup placement), how to interpret bootstrap values, how to identify the most parsimonious tree from a character matrix, and how to recognize when UPGMA's molecular clock assumption is violated.

Common exam traps to avoid

  • Don't confuse "closely related" with "visually adjacent on the tree" (rotation is meaningless).
  • Don't treat bootstrap values as probabilities of biological truth.
  • Remember that branch length only carries meaning in phylograms and chronograms.
  • Always check whether a site is truly informative before counting steps in parsimony.
