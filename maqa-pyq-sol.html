01
Surface Finish · True/False

Grinding generally provides a better surface finish than lapping.

Answer · False

Lapping produces a finer surface finish than grinding. Typical achievable Ra values:

ProcessSurface roughness Ra (μm)
Grinding0.1 – 1.6
Lapping0.025 – 0.4

Grinding uses bonded abrasive wheels rotating at high speed; the abrasive grains plough material and leave grinding marks oriented along the wheel-feed direction. Lapping uses loose abrasive grit suspended in a fluid carrier between the work and a flat lap (cast iron, brass). The abrasive rolls and lightly indents the surface in random directions, removing peaks at the sub-micron level and producing a very fine, non-directional finish.

Because lapping is a low-pressure, low-velocity process with random abrasive motion, it yields better Ra and flatness than grinding. Grinding is a pre-finishing operation; lapping is a super-finishing operation.

02
Process Capability

What is the process capability index? How does it influence repair cost?

The process capability index measures how well a process produces output within specification limits. The two common indices are:

$$C_p = \frac{USL - LSL}{6\sigma}, \qquad C_{pk} = \min\!\left(\frac{USL - \mu}{3\sigma},\ \frac{\mu - LSL}{3\sigma}\right)$$

$C_p$ assumes the process is centred between USL and LSL; $C_{pk}$ accounts for off-centring. A value of $C_{pk} \geq 1.33$ is generally considered capable.

Influence on Repair Cost

A higher capability index means the process spread ($6\sigma$) is narrow relative to the tolerance — very few units fall outside specification. Fewer defective units means fewer products requiring rework, scrap, warranty repair, or customer-side service repair.

Conversely, when $C_{pk} < 1$, the natural variation exceeds the tolerance band, defectives are produced at a high rate, and the repair/rework/warranty cost rises sharply. Quantitatively, the defective fraction (and therefore the expected repair cost per unit produced) drops by orders of magnitude as $C_{pk}$ rises from 1.0 → 1.33 → 1.67 — which is why companies invest in process improvement rather than absorbing downstream repair cost.

03
SPC Fundamentals

What are control charts? Where do we use them?

A control chart is a time-ordered plot of a quality characteristic with a centre line (process mean) and upper and lower control limits, typically at $\pm 3\sigma$ from the mean. It distinguishes between two sources of variation:

  • Common-cause variation — inherent, random noise in the process.
  • Special-cause (assignable) variation — identifiable disturbances that should be investigated and removed.

Where they are used: in any repetitive manufacturing or service process where output is measured over time — to monitor stability, detect process shifts, decide when to intervene, and verify the effect of improvements.

Surface Metrology · True/False

With an increase in depth of cut, the cut-off length for roughness measurement also increases.

Answer · False

The cut-off length (sampling length) for surface roughness measurement is not governed by the depth of cut. It is determined by the expected magnitude of roughness (Ra value) of the surface, as specified by ISO 4288 / ASME B46.1.

Standard cut-off lengths versus expected Ra
Expected Ra (μm)Cut-off λc (mm)
up to 0.10.25
0.1 – 20.8

A larger depth of cut may produce a rougher surface and therefore a higher Ra, which in turn may require the next standard cut-off length — but the relationship is through the resulting surface roughness, not directly through the depth of cut. The cut-off length is chosen to include several wavelengths of the dominant surface texture so that the measurement filter separates roughness from waviness correctly.

05
Six Sigma

What is DMAIC? Explain with an example. How is it different from DMADV?

DMAIC = Define – Measure – Analyse – Improve – Control. The Six Sigma framework for improving an existing process that is producing output below the desired sigma level.

  • Define: problem statement, customer requirements (CTQs), project scope.
  • Measure: collect baseline data, validate measurement system (Gage R&R), establish current performance.
  • Analyse: use Pareto, fishbone, regression, hypothesis tests to find root causes.
  • Improve: generate, pilot, and implement solutions; DoE often used.
  • Control: lock in gains using SOPs, control charts, mistake-proofing.
Example · Car-Door Assembly Line

3.5% of doors fail a gap inspection. Define the gap CTQ (±0.5 mm). Measure current defect rate and confirm gauge capability. Analyse reveals hinge-bolt torque variation as the dominant cause. Improve introduces a torque-controlled driver and a poka-yoke seating fixture. Control uses SPC on bolt torque and weekly gap audits. Defect rate drops to 0.2%.

DMADV = Define – Measure – Analyse – Design – Verify (also called DFSS — Design for Six Sigma). It is used to design a new product or process from scratch, or when an existing process must be redesigned because incremental DMAIC improvement cannot reach the target sigma level.

Key Difference

DMAIC improves what already exists; DMADV designs something new. DMAIC's last two stages are Improve & Control (modify existing process); DMADV's are Design & Verify (build and validate a new design).

06
Surface Measurement

What are interferometers? How do they differ from stylus-based systems?

An interferometer measures surface topography by exploiting interference between two coherent light beams (typically from a laser). One beam reflects off the test surface, the other off a reference flat; their recombined wavefront forms fringe patterns whose phase shift corresponds to height differences at the surface — with sub-nanometre vertical resolution. Common types: Michelson, Mirau, Twyman-Green, white-light scanning interferometers.

A stylus-based system (Talysurf, Mitutoyo Surftest, &c.) drags a fine diamond-tipped stylus across the surface; vertical stylus motion is transduced to give a 2-D roughness profile. Resolution depends on stylus tip radius (typically 2–10 μm) and electronics.

Comparison of measurement systems
AspectInterferometerStylus
ContactNon-contact (optical)Contact — can scratch soft parts
Vertical resolution< 1 nm~10 nm
Lateral resolutionDiffraction-limited (~0.5 μm)Stylus-tip limited (2–10 μm)

Limitations of Interferometers

Require reflective surfaces (struggle with very rough, transparent, or steeply sloped specimens — fringes are lost); sensitive to environmental vibration; expensive; cannot measure deep features beyond the coherence/scan range without phase-unwrapping issues.

Applications: semiconductor wafers, MEMS, optical lenses and mirrors, hard-disk substrates, lapped/polished bearing surfaces — places where the surface is delicate, reflective, and sub-nanometre features matter.

07
Lean Philosophy

What is Kaizen? What benefits does it offer compared to organisations that do not adopt it?

Kaizen is the Japanese philosophy of continuous, incremental improvement involving all employees, from operators on the shop floor to senior management. Rather than large infrequent capital-intensive overhauls, Kaizen relies on many small, low-cost, employee-driven changes that accumulate over time.

  1. Higher productivity at lower cost — small daily improvements remove waste (muda) in motion, waiting, transport, over-processing, defects, over-production, and inventory.
  2. Stronger quality — problems are caught and fixed at the source, so defect rates fall and rework cost drops.
  3. Employee engagement — workers are empowered to propose and implement changes, raising morale, retention, and ownership.
  4. Faster response to market changes — a culture of continuous adjustment is more agile than one relying on episodic top-down projects.
  5. Lower change-resistance — improvements are incremental and familiar, so they are absorbed without the disruption typical of "big-bang" change programmes.
  6. Improved safety — operators routinely identify and eliminate ergonomic and process hazards as part of daily Kaizen.
  7. Compounding gains — many 1–2% improvements stacked over years yield very large cumulative benefit that competitors without the practice cannot match.

Organisations without Kaizen typically rely on periodic management-driven initiatives, which are slower, more expensive, and miss the bottom-up shop-floor knowledge about real process problems.

Chart Interpretation

Comment on the process (rising trend toward UCL).

The chart shows sample values starting near the LCL and steadily rising over time toward the UCL — a classic trend pattern. An assignable cause is producing a sustained, monotonic shift in the process mean. Common-cause variation alone produces random scatter around the centre line; a clear upward drift is a Western-Electric / Nelson-rule violation (six or more consecutive increasing points).

Likely Physical Causes

Tool wear · gradual fixture loosening · ambient temperature rise during a shift · operator fatigue · accumulating contamination of a cutting fluid · sensor drift in the measurement system.

The process is not in statistical control even though no individual point may yet have crossed the UCL — action should be taken before the trend reaches the limit. Investigation and corrective action are required: identify whether the drift is in the process itself or in the measurement system, and either restore the process (tool change, recalibration, temperature compensation) or revise the procedure that allows the drift.

c-Chart · Processing Errors

Processing errors per 100 purchase orders — construct an appropriate control chart and revised chart if required.

Each sample contains 100 purchase orders and the count of errors is recorded for 25 samples. Because we are counting the number of nonconformities per inspection unit of constant size, a c-chart is appropriate.

Step 1 — Compute c̄ $$\bar{c} = \frac{\sum c_i}{k} = \frac{101}{25} = 4.04$$
Step 2 — Trial 3σ limits $$UCL = \bar{c} + 3\sqrt{\bar{c}} = 4.04 + 3\sqrt{4.04} = 10.07$$ $$LCL = \bar{c} - 3\sqrt{\bar{c}} = 4.04 - 6.03 = -1.99 \Rightarrow 0$$ $$CL = 4.04$$
Step 3 — Check for out-of-control points

Only sample 9 (c = 11) exceeds UCL = 10.07. All other samples lie between 0 and 10.07.

Step 4 — Revised limits (remove sample 9, assignable cause) $$\bar{c}_{rev} = \frac{101 - 11}{24} = \frac{90}{24} = 3.75$$ $$UCL_{rev} = 3.75 + 3\sqrt{3.75} = 9.56, \quad LCL_{rev} = 0, \quad CL_{rev} = 3.75$$

Re-checking the remaining 24 samples (maximum = 7) against UCL = 9.56: all in control.

Comment on Quality Level

The revised process averages 3.75 errors per 100 orders — i.e. roughly 3.75% of all purchase orders contain a processing error. For a company whose stated objective is to eliminate such errors totally, this is far from acceptable. The process is statistically stable after removing the special-cause sample, but its capability is poor relative to the target of zero. Further improvement (root-cause analysis on the dominant error types, training, mistake-proofing of the order-entry system, automation of validation checks) is needed to lower c̄ itself.

Step 2 — Trial limits

X̄ chart:

$$UCL = 6.66 + (0.729)(1.82) = 7.99$$ $$LCL = 6.66 - 1.327 = 5.33, \quad CL = 6.66$$

R chart:

$$UCL = (2.282)(1.82) = 4.15, \quad LCL = 0, \quad CL = 1.82$$

R chart: max R = 2.7 (sample 8) < 4.15 → R chart in control.

  • Below LCL → poor water quality (undesirable special cause). Eliminate.

Stability discussion: The R chart is in control throughout, so within-sample variability is acceptable; the issue is variation in the mean dissolved oxygen between observations. The bad samples (13, 14) suggest episodes of higher pollutant discharge; the good samples should be investigated as opportunities — conditions (river flow, lower discharge, aeration) that should be reproduced.

X̄–R · Delay Times

Average and range of delay times (n = 5, k = 25). Lower is preferred.

Constants for $n = 5$: $A_2 = 0.577$, $D_3 = 0$, $D_4 = 2.114$.

Grand averages $$\bar{\bar{X}} = \frac{241.4}{25} = 9.656, \qquad \bar{R} = \frac{89.8}{25} = 3.592$$
Trial limits

X̄ chart:

$$UCL = 9.656 + (0.577)(3.592) = 11.73$$ $$LCL = 9.656 - 2.073 = 7.58, \quad CL = 9.66$$

R chart:

$$UCL = (2.114)(3.592) = 7.59, \quad LCL = 0, \quad CL = 3.59$$

These match the LCL = 7.58 and UCL = 11.72 given in the problem.

Out-of-control points
  • R chart: max R = 4.6 (sample 3) < 7.59 → R chart in control.
  • X̄ chart, Sample 3: 15.8 > 11.73 → above UCL — long delay, undesirable
  • X̄ chart, Sample 6: 5.6 < 7.58 → below LCL — very short delay, desirable
Will Sample 6 Be Removed?

Lower delay is preferred. Sample 6 represents an exceptionally good outcome — a special cause that produced lower-than-expected delay. The right action is to investigate why delay was so low and replicate the cause. For computing revised limits, the standard convention is to remove sample 3 (unwanted excursion) and treat sample 6 as a target to learn from rather than a defect.

Revised limits — removing only sample 3 $$\bar{\bar{X}}_{rev} = \frac{241.4 - 15.8}{24} = 9.40$$ $$\bar{R}_{rev} = \frac{89.8 - 4.6}{24} = 3.55$$ $$UCL_{X,rev} = 9.40 + (0.577)(3.55) = 11.45$$ $$LCL_{X,rev} = 7.35$$ $$UCL_{R,rev} = (2.114)(3.55) = 7.50$$

Performance comment: The process averages roughly 9.4 minutes of delay with most points in the 7–11 minute band. Sample 3 (15.8) flags a one-off problem requiring root-cause analysis. Samples 1 and 6 show that delays under 8 minutes are achievable — so there is room to bring the mean down. Process is statistically stable after removing the high outlier, but the mean delay is not yet at the desired low level.

p-Chart · Customer Satisfaction

Number of dissatisfied customers in 20 samples of size 100.

Step 1 — p̄ $$\sum d = 83, \quad \sum n = 20 \times 100 = 2000$$ $$\bar{p} = \frac{83}{2000} = 0.0415$$
Step 2 — Trial limits $$\sigma_p = \sqrt{\frac{\bar{p}(1-\bar{p})}{n}} = \sqrt{\frac{0.0415 \times 0.9585}{100}} = 0.01994$$ $$UCL = 0.0415 + 3(0.01994) = 0.1013$$ $$LCL = 0.0415 - 0.0598 = -0.0183 \Rightarrow 0$$ $$CL = 0.0415$$
Step 3 — Out-of-control points
  • Sample 10: $p = 0.11$ → above UCL
  • Sample 16: $p = 0.12$ → above UCL

All other samples have $p \leq 0.05$ (in control).

Step 4 — Revised limits (remove 10 and 16) $$\bar{p}_{rev} = \frac{83 - 11 - 12}{18 \times 100} = \frac{60}{1800} = 0.0333$$ $$\sigma_{p,rev} = \sqrt{\frac{0.0333 \times 0.9667}{100}} = 0.01795$$ $$UCL_{rev} = 0.0333 + 3(0.01795) = 0.0871$$ $$LCL_{rev} = 0, \quad CL_{rev} = 0.0333$$
Result

Re-checking the remaining 18 samples: maximum $p = 0.05 < 0.0871$ → all in control. Process is now stable with about 3.33% dissatisfied customers on average — better than the original 4.15%, but the company should still address the dominant causes of dissatisfaction to lower p̄ further.

(a) Discussion

The Pareto principle holds clearly. The top three causes — Lack of proper tools, Work Orders, Wrong Parts/Material — account for 79% of total lost-time cost. Note the contrast with raw incidence count: Work Instructions and Bill of Materials cause the most frequent lost-time incidents (4000 and 3500 respectively) but their unit cost is so low that they are the least expensive causes. Tackling causes in descending cost order:

  • Lack of proper tools (₹12,000) ✓ → ₹6,000 left
  • Work Orders (₹4,000) ✓ → ₹2,000 left
  • Wrong Parts/Material (₹3,000) ✗ exceeds remaining budget

The manager should address "Lack of proper tools" and "Work Orders", absorbing ₹16,000 and removing 66.5% of total lost-time cost. The remaining ₹2,000 can be reserved or rolled over. An alternative combination — Lack of tools + Wrong Parts + Bad assembly outlines — costs ₹17,500 and covers 72.8%, but gives slightly less cost-impact per rupee.
