PART 01

Protein Structure Prediction — Foundations

The Four Levels of Protein Structure

Proteins are polymers of amino acids that fold into three-dimensional structures. There are four hierarchical levels, each driven by different forces:

Primary Structure

The linear sequence of amino acids (polypeptide chain). Length ranges from ~20 to ~30,000 residues.

Driving force: Peptide bond (covalent bond between amino acids)

Secondary Structure

Local folding of the backbone into α-helices, β-sheets, and coils.

Driving force: Hydrogen bonds between backbone atoms

Tertiary Structure

Overall 3D fold — interactions between different secondary structures. Example: Myoglobin.

Driving force: Hydrophobic effect; also H-bonds, salt bridges, Van der Waals, disulfide bonds

Quaternary Structure

Multiple protein subunits assembled together. Example: Hemoglobin (4 subunits).

Analogy: Paragraph = multiple sentences (tertiary structures) combined

💡
Memory trick: Alphabets = Primary, Words = Secondary, Sentence = Tertiary, Paragraph = Quaternary
Why Does Structure Prediction Matter?

There is a massive and growing gap between known sequences and experimentally determined structures:

Experiments are time-consuming, expensive, and laborious. Computational prediction is essential.

Applications
  • Function of novel proteins
  • Effect of mutations on protein function
  • Structure-based drug design

Core Principle: Sequence → Structure → Function

The ultimate goal: given only the amino acid sequence, predict the 3D atomic coordinates (PDB format: X, Y, Z for every atom).

Theoretical Foundations
Anfinsen's Dogma ✅

The native 3D structure of a protein is entirely encoded in its amino acid sequence, and corresponds to the minimum free energy state. This justifies predicting structure from sequence.

Levinthal's Paradox ⚠️

A 100-residue protein with 3 degrees of freedom per residue has 3¹⁰⁰ possible conformations. A supercomputer doing random search would take longer than the age of the universe — yet proteins fold in milliseconds!

This means proteins don't fold randomly; they follow specific pathways. Structure prediction is not trivial.

CASP — Critical Assessment of protein Structure Prediction

A community-wide blind competition held every two years. Research groups predict protein structures before experimental results are released. CASP14 (2020): AlphaFold by Google DeepMind changed the field entirely — achieving near-experimental accuracy.

Predicting Secondary Structure

Secondary structure prediction focuses on predicting whether each residue is in an α-helix, β-sheet, or coil/turn. The key insight is that amino acids have statistical propensities for different secondary structures due to their size, shape, and charge.

Chou-Fasman Algorithm EXAM TARGET

Target Use probability parameters (P_ij) to identify α-helical regions. Understand why sliding windows are used. Know helix vs sheet prediction rules.
The P_ij Formula — Core Concept

The Chou-Fasman algorithm assigns each amino acid a conformational parameter (P_ij) that quantifies how likely amino acid i is to appear in secondary structure j (helix, sheet, or turn).

Chou-Fasman Conformational Parameter
P_ij = (frequency of amino acid i in SS j) / (overall frequency of amino acid i)
Worked Example of P_ij Calculation

Suppose: Database of 50 proteins, 20,000 total AAs, 5,000 AAs in helix. Of 2,000 Alanine residues in the database, 500 are in helices.

P(Ala, helix) = (500/5000) / (2000/20000)
             = 0.10 / 0.10
             = 1.0   ← neutral (no preference)

A value > 1.0 means the amino acid prefers that structure. A value < 1.0 means it is disfavored.

Why can Proline NOT be in an α-helix? Proline has a rigid cyclic side chain that prevents the backbone from adopting the φ torsion angle required for α-helix geometry. It is a "helix breaker."
Predicting α-Helices ★ EXAM

The algorithm operates in three sub-steps for helices: Nucleation → Extension → Conflict Resolution.

Step A: Nucleation (Finding a Helix Seed)
  • 1
    Take a window of 6 consecutive amino acids
  • 2
    Count how many residues in that window have P(α) > 1.0
  • 3
    If at least 4 out of 6 residues have P(α) > 1.0, this is a nucleation site (a potential helix start)
  • 4
    Important: The 4 residues do NOT need to be contiguous — just ≥ 4 in any positions within the window
Step B: Extension (Growing the Helix)
  • 1
    Once a nucleation site is found, try to extend it in both directions (one residue at a time)
  • 2
    After adding the next residue, look at the 4 most recent consecutive residues (tetrapeptide) at the new edge
  • 3
    Calculate the average P(α) of that tetrapeptide
  • 4
    If average P(α) ≥ 1.0: continue extending. If average P(α) < 1.0: STOP — that is the helix boundary
Step C: Conflict Resolution

If a region was identified as both helix AND sheet, compare average P(α) vs average P(β) for the overlapping region:

  • If avg P(α) > avg P(β) → assign as α-helix
  • If avg P(β) > avg P(α) → assign as β-sheet

Predicting β-Sheets

Almost identical logic to helices, but different thresholds:

β-Sheet Nucleation Rule
  • 1
    Take a window of 5 consecutive amino acids (not 6)
  • 2
    At least 3 out of 5 must have P(β) > 1.0 (not 4 out of 6)
  • 3
    Extension: same tetrapeptide rule — average P(β) ≥ 1.0 to continue
Predicting β-Turns

β-turns (hairpin loops) are predicted differently — using position-specific probabilities:

β-Turn Probability Formula
p(t) = f(i) × f(i+1) × f(i+2) × f(i+3)
where f(i), f(i+1), f(i+2), f(i+3) are position-specific turn propensities from the Chou-Fasman table
Conditions to Predict a β-Turn at position i
  • P(t) > 0.000075
  • Average P(turn) for the tetrapeptide > 1.0
  • Average P(turn) > average P(α) AND > average P(β) for that same region
Why Sliding Windows? — Conceptual Understanding KEY CONCEPT

This is critical to understand for your exam. The sliding window concept exists for several deep reasons:

Reason 1: Secondary Structure is Local

α-helices and β-sheets are defined by local backbone geometry. Whether a residue forms a helix depends not just on that residue, but on its neighbors — you need enough consecutive residues to actually form a helix (minimum ~4 H-bonds → needs ~6 residues).

Reason 2: Cooperativity

Helix formation is cooperative — several residues must "agree" to fold together. Checking a single residue in isolation is meaningless. The window captures this cooperative effect statistically.

Reason 3: Noise Reduction

A single amino acid might have P(α) < 1.0 but still be inside a helix if surrounded by strong helix formers. Averaging over a window reduces the effect of individual outliers and captures the context.

Reason 4: Physical Minimum Length

An α-helix needs at least ~4 residues to form a single turn (3.6 residues/turn). A window of 6 ensures the nucleation site is physically long enough to initiate a helix. Similarly, β-sheets need at least 3–5 residues to form a stable strand.

📐
Key insight for the exam: The window size (6 for helix, 5 for sheet) and threshold (4/6, 3/5) are not arbitrary — they reflect the minimum structural units needed to nucleate each type of secondary structure. The sliding approach then "scans" the entire sequence systematically.

PART 03

Homology Modeling & AI Methods

The 6-Step Homology Modeling Pipeline

Homology (comparative) modeling builds a 3D model of a target protein by using known structures of similar (homologous) sequences as templates. Structure is more evolutionarily conserved than sequence.

  • 1
    Template Identification — Search PDB for proteins with similar sequences.
    Rule of thumb: >50% identity is ideal; <30% identity is risky (twilight zone). Use BLAST against PDB.
  • 2
    Sequence Alignment — Align target sequence to template(s).
    Most critical step! Any alignment error propagates into the final 3D model. Errors here are impossible to fix later.
  • 3
    Backbone Construction — Copy the backbone 3D coordinates from the template for conserved regions (Structurally Conserved Regions, SCRs). Backbone geometry is far more conserved than sequence across homologs.
  • 4
    Loop Modeling — Model Structurally Variable Regions (SVRs) — insertions/deletions where the target differs from the template. These are the least reliable parts of the model because no template backbone exists to copy.
  • 5
    Side-Chain Modeling — Assign torsion angles (χ angles) to side chains using rotamer libraries. Goal: minimize steric clashes with neighboring atoms.
  • 6
    Refinement & Validation — Energy minimization, check stereochemistry, assess overall model quality.
    Tools: Ramachandran plot (checks φ/ψ angles), PROCHECK, DOPE scores.
The Ramachandran Plot

A plot of backbone torsion angles φ (phi) vs ψ (psi) for all residues. Residues in allowed regions (red/yellow on the plot) indicate correct geometry. Most residues should fall in the β-sheet region (upper left) or right-handed α-helix region (lower left). Residues outside allowed regions indicate modeling errors.

Limitations of Homology Modeling
Inherent Limitations
  • Requires a template (no template = no model)
  • Quality depends entirely on alignment accuracy
  • Loop prediction is unreliable
  • Fails for proteins with novel folds
Threading: An Alternative

Instead of sequence similarity, use structural compatibility. Thread the target sequence onto many known 3D folds and score how well it fits energetically/structurally. Useful when there is no obvious sequence homolog but a similar fold may exist.

Modern AI Methods: AlphaFold
AlphaFold — The Revolution

AlphaFold (DeepMind, CASP14 2020) moved from template-based to data-driven prediction using deep learning on thousands of known structures.

Key innovations: Uses Multiple Sequence Alignment (MSA) to capture co-evolutionary information — residues that evolve together tend to be in contact. The Evoformer module (48 blocks) processes both sequence and pairwise representations, then a Structure Module (8 blocks) builds the 3D structure.

Result: The AlphaFold Protein Structure Database provides open access to over 200 million protein structure predictions.

Limitations of AI Models (including AlphaFold)
  • Proteins are dynamic — predictions are static snapshots
  • Intrinsically disordered proteins remain challenging
  • Less accurate for protein complexes
  • No dynamics information (can't predict conformational changes)

RNA Structure — Foundations

RNA Types and Functions
RNA Types
  • mRNA — messenger RNA (coding)
  • rRNA — ribosomal RNA (structure of ribosome)
  • tRNA — transfer RNA (amino acid delivery)
  • lncRNA — long non-coding RNA
  • siRNA — small interfering RNA (RNAi)
  • miRNA — micro RNA (gene regulation)
  • snoRNA — small nucleolar RNA (rRNA modification)
  • snRNA — part of spliceosome
Key Difference from DNA

DNA is primarily double-stranded (duplex form). RNA is single-stranded, which allows it to fold back on itself and form complex secondary structures through intramolecular base pairing.

RNA sequence analysis must therefore account for structure, not just sequence. Often, structural conservation is more important than sequence conservation in RNA.

RNA Base Pairs KEY

RNA uses four bases: Adenine, Cytosine, Guanine, Uracil (no Thymine).

A-U (Watson-Crick)

2 hydrogen bonds — less stable

Canonical pairing

G-C (Watson-Crick)

3 hydrogen bonds — more stable

Canonical pairing

G-U (Wobble) EXAM

Non-canonical "wobble" pairing

Allowed in RNA but not in standard Watson-Crick DNA/RNA rules — important for Nussinov!

📌
Rule: Each base can pair with at most ONE other base (no bifurcated bonds). This is a fundamental constraint in all RNA structure algorithms.
RNA Secondary Structure Elements
Element Definition Key Property
Stem Double-stranded region formed by complementary base pairs Most thermodynamically stable
Hairpin Loop Single strand forming a loop at the end of a stem; loops ≥ 4 bases long Minimum 4 unpaired bases in loop
Bulge Loop Unpaired bases on ONE side only of the duplex Asymmetric
Interior Loop Unpaired bases on BOTH sides of the duplex Symmetric disruption
Junction (Multiloop) Two or more double-stranded regions converge to form a closed structure Branch point
Pseudoknot Bases in a loop pair with bases outside the loop, creating crossing pairs Cannot be represented in simple nested structure; hard to predict algorithmically
Kissing Hairpins Unpaired bases of two separate hairpin loops base pair with each other Long-range interaction
🔍
Detecting Pseudoknots: In circular representation, draw the sequence on a circle and draw arcs for each base pair. If any arcs cross, a pseudoknot is present. Most algorithms (including Nussinov) cannot predict pseudoknots — they only find nested structures.

Nussinov Algorithm — Base-Pair Maximization EXAM TARGET

Target Maximize base pairs (Watson-Crick: A-U, G-C; plus G-U wobble). Fill stage matrix. Traceback stage. Understand constraints.
The Core Idea: Base-Pair Maximization

The Nussinov algorithm finds the RNA secondary structure that has the maximum number of base pairs. It uses dynamic programming — solving the problem by first solving all smaller subproblems.

What We're Computing
S(i, j) = maximum number of base pairs in the subsequence from position i to j
Why Dynamic Programming?

Any RNA structure can be decomposed into smaller substructures. The Nussinov insight: there are only 4 possible cases for how any optimal substructure S(i,j) can be built from smaller optimal substructures. This makes it solvable in O(n³) time.

The Four Rules / Constraints ★ EXAM
Rule 1: Minimum Loop Size

i < j − 4

No base pair can form between residues i and j unless they are at least 4 positions apart. This prevents physically impossible tight loops (stems need space for the loop).

Rule 2: Allowed Base Pairs

Only Watson-Crick and wobble pairs:

  • A–U and U–A
  • G–C and C–G
  • G–U and U–G (wobble)
Rule 3: No Bifurcation

Each position can pair with at most one other position. You cannot have a base paired to two different partners simultaneously.

Rule 4: No Pseudoknots

If pair (i,j) and pair (i′,j′) both exist, then i < i′ < j < j′ is forbidden. In other words, base pairs cannot cross each other. Only nested structures are allowed.

The 4-Case Recursion ★ EXAM — KNOW THIS COLD

For any subsequence from position i to j, the maximum number of base pairs S(i,j) is the maximum of exactly four cases:

Nussinov Recursion
S(i,j) = max { S(i+1, j), ← Case 1: i unpaired S(i, j−1), ← Case 2: j unpaired S(i+1, j−1) + δ(i,j), ← Case 3: i and j are paired max{S(i,k) + S(k+1,j)} for i<k<j ← Case 4: bifurcation }
Case 1: Position i is Unpaired

Best structure for (i,j) is the same as the best structure for (i+1, j) — we just skip position i without pairing it.

Case 2: Position j is Unpaired

Best structure for (i,j) is the same as the best structure for (i, j−1) — we skip position j without pairing it.

Case 3: Positions i and j are Paired Together

We gain 1 base pair (δ(i,j)=1 if they can pair, 0 otherwise) plus the optimal structure of the interior subsequence (i+1, j−1).

δ(i,j) = 1 if x_i and x_j are complementary, else 0

Case 4: Bifurcation (the Structure Splits)

Position k splits the sequence into two independent stems. We take the best structure of (i,k) combined with the best structure of (k+1,j), maximized over all possible split points k.

💡
Intuition: These four cases are exhaustive and mutually exclusive. No matter how complex the final structure, every optimal substructure can be explained by one of these four cases applied recursively.
Fill Stage — Complete Worked Example ★ EXAM
Initialization

Before filling the matrix, initialize boundary conditions:

γ(i, i−1) = 0   for i = 2 to L   (empty subsequence, length 0)
γ(i, i)   = 0   for i = 1 to L   (single residue, no pairs possible)

The matrix is upper triangular — only cells where j > i are meaningful.
Cells where j < i (below diagonal) are set to 0 or marked as invalid.

Traceback Stage — Recovering the Structure ★ EXAM

After filling the matrix, we do a traceback to determine which specific base pairs form the optimal structure. This is a stack-based procedure.

Traceback Algorithm (Pseudo-code)
1. Initialize: Push (1, L) onto the stack
2. Repeat until stack is empty:
   a. Pop (i, j)
   b. If i ≥ j: SKIP (no more pairs to find)
   c. Else if γ(i+1, j) == γ(i, j):
         → position i is UNPAIRED
         → Push (i+1, j)
   d. Else if γ(i, j−1) == γ(i, j):
         → position j is UNPAIRED
         → Push (i, j−1)
   e. Else if γ(i+1, j−1) + δ(i,j) == γ(i, j):
         → i and j ARE PAIRED
         → Record base pair (i, j)
         → Push (i+1, j−1)
   f. Else (bifurcation): find k where γ(i,k) + γ(k+1,j) == γ(i,j)
         → Push (k+1, j) and Push (i, k)
Traceback Example GGGAAAUCC — from S(1,9)=3
  1. Pop (1,9). Check: γ(2,9)=3 = γ(1,9)=3? Yes → position 1 is unpaired. Push (2,9).
  2. Pop (2,9). Check: γ(3,9)=2 ≠ γ(2,9)=3. Check: γ(2,8)=2 ≠ 3. Check: γ(3,8)+δ(2,9)=2+1=3=γ(2,9)? G(2) and C(9): G-C ✅ → PAIR (2,9). Push (3,8).

Limitations of Nussinov & Energy Minimization
Nussinov Limitations
  • Simplistic — treats all base pairs equally (doesn't distinguish A-U from G-C stability)
  • Ignores nearest-neighbor stacking interactions
  • Ignores loop length preferences
  • Cannot predict pseudoknots (algorithm only allows nested pairs)
  • Often gives wrong structure despite maximizing base pairs
Energy Minimization (MFOLD, ViennaRNA)

Improvement over Nussinov: instead of just counting base pairs, score thermodynamically using energy parameters (stacking interactions, loop length penalties, etc.). The most stable structure (minimum free energy) is predicted.

  • Uses dynamic programming (same framework)
  • Energy of any position influenced only by local sequence and structure
  • Still cannot handle pseudoknots due to algorithmic constraints
  • Gives only one optimal structure

Tools: MFOLD, ViennaRNA

Comparative Sequence Analysis — The Gold Standard

Use sequence alignment to find conserved residues and identify covariant base pairs. If two positions mutate together in evolution (e.g., G-C → A-U), they are likely base-paired. This is the most trusted method and does not require energy parameters. Requires multiple aligned sequences from different species.
